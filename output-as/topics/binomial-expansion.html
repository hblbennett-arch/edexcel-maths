Guided Approach

The standard approach for AS binomial expansion questions:

1
Identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\) in \((a + b)^n\)
Write down the formula: \((a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r\). Identify what plays the role of \(a\), what plays the role of \(b\) (including its sign), and what \(n\) is.
2
Write out terms using \(\binom{n}{r}\)
For each required term, substitute into \(\binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r\). Keep track of signs carefully: if \(b\) is negative, the sign alternates. Show \(\binom{n}{r}\) values explicitly (e.g. \(\binom{6}{2} = 15\)).
3
Simplify each term and apply any given conditions
Simplify powers and coefficients. If the question gives a condition (e.g. "the coefficient of \(x^2\) is 60"), set up an equation and solve for the unknown constant.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting to raise the constant to the power: In \((2 + kx)^6\), the first term is \(2^6 = 64\), not just 2. Every term needs \(a^{n-r}\) computed correctly.
  • Sign errors with negative \(b\): In \((3 - 2x)^5\), the term \(b = -2x\) carries a negative sign. So \((-2x)^2 = +4x^2\) but \((-2x)^3 = -8x^3\). Many candidates drop the negative.
  • Not simplifying fully: Writing \(\binom{5}{2} \cdot 3^3 \cdot (-2x)^2 = 10 \cdot 27 \cdot 4x^2\) and then stopping. You must compute \(10 \times 27 \times 4 = 1080\).
  • Confusing \(\binom{n}{r}\) with \(^nP_r\): The binomial coefficient is \(\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\), not \(\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\). Use Pascal's triangle or the formula.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write the general term \(\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r\) at the start to earn the method mark even if arithmetic goes wrong later.
  • Use ascending powers of \(x\) (constant term first) unless told otherwise.
  • When finding an unknown constant: write "coefficient of \(x^2\) = ..." as a clear equation before solving.

AS Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 3 (6 marks)

(a) Find the first 4 terms, in ascending powers of \(x\), of the binomial expansion of \((2 + kx)^6\) where \(k\) is a non-zero constant. Give each term in simplest form. [4 marks]

(b) Given that the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the expansion is 60, find the value of \(k\). [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\) ▶
We need to apply the binomial expansion formula. First, identify what corresponds to \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\) in the general formula \((a+b)^n\). Here \((2 + kx)^6\) gives us \(a = 2\), \(b = kx\), and \(n = 6\).

The general binomial expansion is:

$$(a + b)^n = \binom{n}{0}a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2 + \binom{n}{3}a^{n-3}b^3 + \cdots$$

Here: \(a = 2\), \(b = kx\), \(n = 6\)

Part (a) Step 2: Write out the first 4 terms ▶
We need the first 4 terms in ascending powers of \(x\). That means \(r = 0, 1, 2, 3\). For each value of \(r\), compute \(\binom{6}{r} \cdot 2^{6-r} \cdot (kx)^r\). Keep the binomial coefficients visible to earn marks.

Term 1 (\(r = 0\)): \(\binom{6}{0} \cdot 2^6 \cdot (kx)^0 = 1 \cdot 64 \cdot 1 = 64\)

Term 2 (\(r = 1\)): \(\binom{6}{1} \cdot 2^5 \cdot (kx)^1 = 6 \cdot 32 \cdot kx = 192kx\)

Term 3 (\(r = 2\)): \(\binom{6}{2} \cdot 2^4 \cdot (kx)^2 = 15 \cdot 16 \cdot k^2x^2 = 240k^2x^2\)

Term 4 (\(r = 3\)): \(\binom{6}{3} \cdot 2^3 \cdot (kx)^3 = 20 \cdot 8 \cdot k^3x^3 = 160k^3x^3\)

$$(2 + kx)^6 = 64 + 192kx + 240k^2x^2 + 160k^3x^3 + \cdots$$

B1 — First term: 64
M1 — Correct structure using \(\binom{6}{r}\) with powers of 2 and \(kx\)
A1 — Second term correct: \(192kx\)
A1 — Third and fourth terms correct: \(240k^2x^2\), \(160k^3x^3\)
A common error was to write the first term as \(2^6 = 32\) instead of 64. Also, many candidates forgot to raise \(k\) to the appropriate power in the later terms.
Part (b) Step 3: Use the given condition to find \(k\) ▶
Part (b) tells us the coefficient of \(x^2\) is 60. From our expansion, the coefficient of \(x^2\) is \(240k^2\). Setting this equal to 60 gives us an equation we can solve for \(k\).

The coefficient of \(x^2\) is \(240k^2\).

Set equal to 60:

$$240k^2 = 60$$

Divide both sides by 240:

$$k^2 = \frac{60}{240} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Take square root (both signs since \(k\) is non-zero but no sign restriction given):

$$k = \pm\frac{1}{2}$$

M1 — Setting their coefficient of \(x^2\) equal to 60
A1 — \(k = \pm\frac{1}{2}\) (both values needed)
Many candidates only gave \(k = \frac{1}{2}\), forgetting the negative root. Since the question says "non-zero" but doesn't restrict the sign, both values are valid. Candidates who wrote \(k = \frac{1}{2}\) only lost the A1.

AS Paper 1 — June 2019 — Question 3 (4 marks)

Find the first 4 terms, in ascending powers of \(x\), of the binomial expansion of \((3 - 2x)^5\). Give each term in simplest form.

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Step 1: Identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(n\); note the negative sign ▶
Here \((3 - 2x)^5\) means \(a = 3\), \(b = -2x\) (the negative is part of \(b\)), and \(n = 5\). The key thing to watch is that \((-2x)^r\) will alternate sign: positive when \(r\) is even, negative when \(r\) is odd.

Write: \((3 + (-2x))^5\) so that \(a = 3\), \(b = -2x\), \(n = 5\).

The general term is \(\binom{5}{r} \cdot 3^{5-r} \cdot (-2x)^r\)

Step 2: Calculate the first 4 terms (\(r = 0, 1, 2, 3\)) ▶
For each value of \(r\), we need to: (1) compute \(\binom{5}{r}\), (2) compute \(3^{5-r}\), (3) compute \((-2x)^r\) being very careful with the sign. Then multiply them all together.

Term 1 (\(r = 0\)): \(\binom{5}{0} \cdot 3^5 \cdot (-2x)^0 = 1 \cdot 243 \cdot 1 = 243\)

Term 2 (\(r = 1\)): \(\binom{5}{1} \cdot 3^4 \cdot (-2x)^1 = 5 \cdot 81 \cdot (-2x) = -810x\)

Term 3 (\(r = 2\)): \(\binom{5}{2} \cdot 3^3 \cdot (-2x)^2 = 10 \cdot 27 \cdot 4x^2 = +1080x^2\)

Term 4 (\(r = 3\)): \(\binom{5}{3} \cdot 3^2 \cdot (-2x)^3 = 10 \cdot 9 \cdot (-8x^3) = -720x^3\)

$$(3 - 2x)^5 = 243 - 810x + 1080x^2 - 720x^3 + \cdots$$

B1 — First term: 243
M1 — Evidence of using \(\binom{5}{r}\) with correct structure
A1 — Second term: \(-810x\)
A1 — Third and fourth terms: \(+1080x^2 - 720x^3\)
The most common error was sign mistakes. Many candidates wrote \(+810x\) (forgetting \((-2x)^1\) is negative) or \(-1080x^2\) (forgetting \((-2x)^2\) is positive). The alternating sign pattern is: +, -, +, -, ...

AS Paper 1 — June 2023 — Question 7 (5 marks)

(a) Find the first 3 terms, in ascending powers of \(x\), of the binomial expansion of \((2 + px)^8\), where \(p\) is a non-zero constant. Give each term in simplest form. [3 marks]

(b) Given that the coefficient of \(x^2\) in the expansion is equal to the coefficient of \(x^3\), find the value of \(p\). [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Write out the first 3 terms using the formula ▶
Here \(a = 2\), \(b = px\), \(n = 8\). We need terms for \(r = 0, 1, 2\). We'll also need the \(r = 3\) term for part (b), so we might as well compute it now.

Term 1 (\(r = 0\)): \(\binom{8}{0} \cdot 2^8 \cdot (px)^0 = 1 \cdot 256 \cdot 1 = 256\)

Term 2 (\(r = 1\)): \(\binom{8}{1} \cdot 2^7 \cdot (px)^1 = 8 \cdot 128 \cdot px = 1024px\)

Term 3 (\(r = 2\)): \(\binom{8}{2} \cdot 2^6 \cdot (px)^2 = 28 \cdot 64 \cdot p^2x^2 = 1792p^2x^2\)

$$(2 + px)^8 = 256 + 1024px + 1792p^2x^2 + \cdots$$

B1 — First term: 256
M1A1 — Correct second and third terms with \(p\) retained
Part (b) Step 2: Find the \(x^3\) term and set up the equation ▶
We need the coefficient of \(x^3\) to set equal to the coefficient of \(x^2\). The \(x^3\) term uses \(r = 3\): \(\binom{8}{3} \cdot 2^5 \cdot (px)^3\). Then we equate the two coefficients and solve for \(p\).

Term 4 (\(r = 3\)): \(\binom{8}{3} \cdot 2^5 \cdot (px)^3 = 56 \cdot 32 \cdot p^3x^3 = 1792p^3x^3\)

Coefficient of \(x^2\) = \(1792p^2\)

Coefficient of \(x^3\) = \(1792p^3\)

Set them equal:

$$1792p^2 = 1792p^3$$

Divide both sides by \(1792p^2\) (valid since \(p \neq 0\)):

$$1 = p$$

$$\boxed{p = 1}$$

M1 — Setting coefficient of \(x^2\) = coefficient of \(x^3\)
A1 — \(p = 1\)
Some candidates divided by \(p^2\) without noting \(p \neq 0\). Since the question states \(p\) is non-zero, the division is valid. Candidates who cancelled \(1792\) from both sides but then couldn't simplify lost the A1 mark.

Key Formulae

The Binomial Theorem (positive integer \(n\)) In formula booklet

$$(a + b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$$

where \(\displaystyle\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\)

The general term (the term in \(x^r\)) is: \(\binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r\)

Useful \(\binom{n}{r}\) values Learn this

\(\binom{n}{0} = 1\), \(\binom{n}{1} = n\), \(\binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}\), \(\binom{n}{3} = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{6}\)
