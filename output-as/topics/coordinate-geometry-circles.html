Key Formulae

$$(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$$

Circle with centre \((a, b)\) and radius \(r\)

When given in expanded form, complete the square to get back to the standard form above. For example:

$$x^2 + y^2 - 10x - 8y + 32 = 0$$

$$(x - 5)^2 - 25 + (y - 4)^2 - 16 + 32 = 0$$

$$(x - 5)^2 + (y - 4)^2 = 9$$

So centre is \((5, 4)\) and radius is \(\sqrt{9} = 3\)

Key geometric facts:

  • The tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact
  • A vertical line \(x = k\) is tangent to circle if the distance from centre to line equals the radius
  • A line intersects a circle at 0, 1, or 2 points (discriminant test)

Guided Approach

The standard approach for circle questions:

1
Complete the square to find centre and radius
Given \(x^2 + y^2 + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0\), complete the square in \(x\) and \(y\) separately. Compare with \((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2\) to read off centre and radius.
2
Use the tangent-radius relationship
If a tangent touches the circle at point P, the radius to P is perpendicular to the tangent. Use: gradient of radius \(\times\) gradient of tangent \(= -1\).
3
Substitute line into circle for intersections
To find where a line meets a circle: substitute the line equation into the circle equation, get a quadratic, and solve. Use the discriminant: \(b^2 - 4ac > 0\) for two intersections, \(= 0\) for tangent, \(< 0\) for no intersection.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Sign errors in completing the square: In \(x^2 - 10x\), the centre x-coordinate is \(+5\), not \(-5\). Remember: \(x^2 - 10x = (x-5)^2 - 25\).
  • Forgetting to square root for radius: After completing the square, the RHS is \(r^2\), not \(r\). You must take the square root.
  • Wrong perpendicular gradient: The perpendicular gradient is \(-\frac{1}{m}\), not \(-m\). If the tangent has gradient \(\frac{1}{3}\), the radius has gradient \(-3\).
  • Not using discriminant correctly: For a tangent, set discriminant \(= 0\). For "two distinct points", set discriminant \(> 0\). State which condition you're using.
  • Arithmetic errors in expansion: When substituting a line into the circle, expand carefully. Check by substituting your answers back into both equations.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write the completed square form clearly: \((x-5)^2 + (y-4)^2 = 9\), then state "centre (5, 4), radius 3"
  • When finding tangent conditions, always state "tangent means discriminant = 0" before computing
  • Draw a quick sketch showing the circle, its centre, and the relevant line — this catches sign errors
  • For shortest distance problems: find the perpendicular from the centre to the line, solve simultaneously, use Pythagoras, then subtract the radius

Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 14 (9 marks)

The circle C has equation

$$x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 10y + 9 = 0$$

(a) Find (i) the coordinates of the centre of C, (ii) the radius of C. [3 marks]

The line with equation \(y = kx\), where \(k\) is a constant, cuts C at two distinct points.

(b) Find the range of values for \(k\). [6 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Complete the square to find centre and radius ▶
The equation is in expanded form. To find centre and radius, we need it in the form \((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2\). Complete the square separately for the \(x\) terms and the \(y\) terms.

$$x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 10y + 9 = 0$$

Group \(x\) and \(y\) terms:

$$(x^2 - 4x) + (y^2 + 10y) + 9 = 0$$

Complete the square for \(x\): half of \(-4\) is \(-2\), square it to get \(4\)

$$(x - 2)^2 - 4 + (y^2 + 10y) + 9 = 0$$

Complete the square for \(y\): half of \(10\) is \(5\), square it to get \(25\)

$$(x - 2)^2 - 4 + (y + 5)^2 - 25 + 9 = 0$$

Collect constants: \(-4 - 25 + 9 = -20\)

$$(x - 2)^2 + (y + 5)^2 = 20$$

Centre: \((2, -5)\)

Radius: \(\sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\)

B1 — Centre \((2, -5)\)
M1 — Attempt to complete the square in both \(x\) and \(y\)
A1 — Radius \(= 2\sqrt{5}\) (or \(\sqrt{20}\))
Part (b) Step 2: Substitute \(y = kx\) into the circle equation ▶
For the line \(y = kx\) to cut the circle at two distinct points, when we substitute into the circle equation we must get a quadratic with positive discriminant (\(b^2 - 4ac > 0\)). First, do the substitution to get the quadratic.

Substitute \(y = kx\) into \(x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 10y + 9 = 0\):

$$x^2 + (kx)^2 - 4x + 10(kx) + 9 = 0$$

$$x^2 + k^2x^2 - 4x + 10kx + 9 = 0$$

Collect terms by power of \(x\):

$$(1 + k^2)x^2 + (10k - 4)x + 9 = 0$$

M1 — Substituting \(y = kx\) and forming a quadratic in \(x\)
Part (b) Step 3: Apply the discriminant condition ▶
For TWO distinct intersection points, the discriminant must be strictly positive: \(b^2 - 4ac > 0\). Here \(a = (1+k^2)\), \(b = (10k-4)\), \(c = 9\). We compute the discriminant and solve the resulting inequality.

For two distinct points: \(b^2 - 4ac > 0\)

$$(10k - 4)^2 - 4(1 + k^2)(9) > 0$$

Expand \((10k-4)^2 = 100k^2 - 80k + 16\):

$$100k^2 - 80k + 16 - 36(1 + k^2) > 0$$

Expand: \(-36 - 36k^2\):

$$100k^2 - 80k + 16 - 36 - 36k^2 > 0$$

$$64k^2 - 80k - 20 > 0$$

Divide through by 4:

$$16k^2 - 20k - 5 > 0$$

M1 — Setting up discriminant \(> 0\)
A1 — Correct simplified quadratic inequality
Part (b) Step 4: Solve the quadratic inequality ▶
We need to find where \(16k^2 - 20k - 5 > 0\). First find the roots using the quadratic formula, then determine which intervals satisfy the inequality (since the coefficient of \(k^2\) is positive, the quadratic is positive OUTSIDE the roots).

Use the quadratic formula on \(16k^2 - 20k - 5 = 0\):

$$k = \frac{20 \pm \sqrt{400 + 320}}{32} = \frac{20 \pm \sqrt{720}}{32}$$

\(\sqrt{720} = 12\sqrt{5}\):

$$k = \frac{20 \pm 12\sqrt{5}}{32} = \frac{5 \pm 3\sqrt{5}}{8}$$

Since the coefficient of \(k^2\) is positive, the parabola opens upward, so the quadratic is \(> 0\) when:

$$\boxed{k < \frac{5 - 3\sqrt{5}}{8} \quad \text{or} \quad k > \frac{5 + 3\sqrt{5}}{8}}$$

M1 — Using quadratic formula to find critical values
A1 — Correct exact critical values
A1 — Correct inequality (outside the roots)
Many candidates correctly found the critical values but then wrote the wrong inequality direction. Since the quadratic in \(k\) has positive leading coefficient, it's positive OUTSIDE the roots. A quick sketch of the parabola helps you get this right every time.

Paper 1 — June 2019 — Question 10 (5 marks)

A circle C has equation

$$x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 8y - 8 = 0$$

(a) Find (i) the coordinates of the centre of C, (ii) the exact radius of C. [3 marks]

The straight line with equation \(x = k\), where \(k\) is a constant, is a tangent to C.

(b) Find the possible values for \(k\). [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Complete the square ▶
Same technique as always: complete the square in \(x\) and \(y\) to convert from expanded form to standard form.

$$x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 8y - 8 = 0$$

Complete square for \(x\): \(x^2 - 4x = (x-2)^2 - 4\)

Complete square for \(y\): \(y^2 + 8y = (y+4)^2 - 16\)

$$(x-2)^2 - 4 + (y+4)^2 - 16 - 8 = 0$$

$$(x-2)^2 + (y+4)^2 = 28$$

Centre: \((2, -4)\)

Radius: \(\sqrt{28} = 2\sqrt{7}\)

B1 — Centre \((2, -4)\)
M1A1 — Radius \(= 2\sqrt{7}\)
Part (b) Step 2: Find values of \(k\) for vertical tangent ▶
A vertical line \(x = k\) is tangent to a circle when the horizontal distance from the centre to the line equals the radius. The centre is at \(x = 2\), and the radius is \(2\sqrt{7}\). So the tangent lines are at distance \(2\sqrt{7}\) left and right of \(x = 2\).

The vertical line \(x = k\) is tangent to the circle when the distance from the centre to the line equals the radius.

Distance from centre \((2, -4)\) to the line \(x = k\) is \(|k - 2|\).

$$|k - 2| = 2\sqrt{7}$$

Solve:

$$k - 2 = 2\sqrt{7} \quad \text{or} \quad k - 2 = -2\sqrt{7}$$

$$k = 2 + 2\sqrt{7} \quad \text{or} \quad k = 2 - 2\sqrt{7}$$

The possible values are \(k = 2 + 2\sqrt{7}\) and \(k = 2 - 2\sqrt{7}\)

M1 — Setting distance from centre to line equal to radius
A1 — Both correct values of \(k\)
This is a quick question (2 marks) that tests whether you understand the geometric relationship. No quadratic or discriminant is needed here — just the simple fact that vertical tangent lines sit exactly one radius away from the centre horizontally.

Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 11 (8 marks)

Figure 3 shows the circle C with equation

$$x^2 + y^2 - 10x - 8y + 32 = 0$$

and the line \(l\) with equation \(2y + x + 6 = 0\)

(a) Find (i) the coordinates of the centre of C, (ii) the radius of C. [3 marks]

(b) Find the shortest distance between C and \(l\). [5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Complete the square ▶
Standard completing the square to extract centre and radius from expanded form.

$$x^2 + y^2 - 10x - 8y + 32 = 0$$

\(x^2 - 10x = (x-5)^2 - 25\)

\(y^2 - 8y = (y-4)^2 - 16\)

$$(x-5)^2 - 25 + (y-4)^2 - 16 + 32 = 0$$

$$(x-5)^2 + (y-4)^2 = 9$$

Centre: \((5, 4)\), Radius: \(3\)

B1 — Centre \((5, 4)\)
M1A1 — Radius \(= 3\)
Part (b) Step 2: Find the gradient of line \(l\) and the perpendicular gradient ▶
The shortest distance from the circle to the line is along the perpendicular from the centre to the line, minus the radius. So first we need to find the line through the centre that is perpendicular to \(l\).

Rearrange \(l\) into gradient form \(y = mx + c\):

$$2y + x + 6 = 0$$

$$2y = -x - 6$$

$$y = -\tfrac{1}{2}x - 3$$

Gradient of \(l\) is \(-\frac{1}{2}\).

The perpendicular gradient is the negative reciprocal:

$$m_\perp = -\frac{1}{(-\frac{1}{2})} = 2$$

B1 — Deduces gradient of perpendicular to \(l\) is 2
Part (b) Step 3: Find the equation of the perpendicular through the centre ▶
We know the gradient (\(m = 2\)) and a point the line passes through (the centre \((5, 4)\)). When we know a gradient and a point, we can find the equation of the line using \(\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = m\), which rearranges to \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\).

Using \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) with \(m = 2\) and \((x_1, y_1) = (5, 4)\):

$$y - 4 = 2(x - 5)$$

$$y - 4 = 2x - 10$$

$$y = 2x - 6$$

This perpendicular line will meet \(l\) at the closest point on \(l\) to the centre.

Part (b) Step 4: Solve simultaneously to find the intersection point ▶
The point where the perpendicular meets \(l\) is the closest point on \(l\) to the centre. Substitute \(y = 2x - 6\) into the equation of \(l\).

Substitute into \(2y + x + 6 = 0\):

$$2(2x - 6) + x + 6 = 0$$

$$4x - 12 + x + 6 = 0$$

$$5x - 6 = 0$$

$$x = \frac{6}{5}$$

Find \(y\): \(y = 2\left(\frac{6}{5}\right) - 6 = \frac{12}{5} - 6 = -\frac{18}{5}\)

Intersection point: \(\left(\frac{6}{5},\; -\frac{18}{5}\right)\)

M1 — Correct strategy: solving perpendicular simultaneously with \(l\)
A1 — Intersection point \(\left(\frac{6}{5}, -\frac{18}{5}\right)\)
Part (b) Step 5: Use Pythagoras to find the distance, then subtract the radius ▶
Now we have the centre \((5, 4)\) and the nearest point on \(l\) which is \(\left(\frac{6}{5}, -\frac{18}{5}\right)\). The distance between them (using Pythagoras / the distance formula) gives the distance from the centre to the line. Then we subtract the radius to get the shortest distance from the circle to the line.

Distance from centre \((5, 4)\) to intersection \(\left(\frac{6}{5}, -\frac{18}{5}\right)\):

$$d = \sqrt{\left(5 - \frac{6}{5}\right)^2 + \left(4 - \left(-\frac{18}{5}\right)\right)^2}$$

$$= \sqrt{\left(\frac{19}{5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{38}{5}\right)^2}$$

$$= \sqrt{\frac{361}{25} + \frac{1444}{25}}$$

$$= \sqrt{\frac{1805}{25}} = \frac{\sqrt{1805}}{5}$$

\(1805 = 361 \times 5\), so \(\sqrt{1805} = 19\sqrt{5}\)

$$= \frac{19\sqrt{5}}{5}$$

Now subtract the radius:

$$\text{Shortest distance} = \frac{19\sqrt{5}}{5} - 3 = \frac{19\sqrt{5} - 15}{5}$$

Shortest distance \(= \dfrac{19\sqrt{5} - 15}{5}\) (approximately 5.50)

dM1 — Correct use of Pythagoras to find distance from centre to intersection, then subtracting the radius
A1 — Correct exact answer \(\frac{19\sqrt{5} - 15}{5}\)
Common errors: (1) Forgetting to subtract the radius — the question asks for the shortest distance to the circle, not to the centre. (2) Using the y-intercept of \(l\) (which is \((0, -3)\)) instead of finding the actual closest point via the perpendicular — this gives the wrong answer. (3) Arithmetic slips with the fractions — keep fractions over 5 throughout to avoid errors.

Paper 1 — June 2024 — Question 10 (8 marks)

Figure 4 shows a sketch of the circle C:

  • the point \(P(-1, k+8)\) is the centre of C
  • the point \(Q(3, k^2 - 2k)\) lies on C
  • \(k\) is a positive constant
  • the line \(l\) is the tangent to C at Q

Given that the gradient of \(l\) is \(-2\):

(a) show that \(k^2 - 3k - 10 = 0\) [4 marks]

(b) Hence find an equation for C. [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Find the gradient of PQ (radius) ▶
The tangent at Q is perpendicular to the radius PQ. We know the tangent gradient is \(-2\), so the radius PQ has gradient \(\frac{1}{2}\) (negative reciprocal). We can also express the gradient of PQ using the coordinates of P and Q, then set them equal.

Since tangent is perpendicular to radius at the point of contact:

Gradient of tangent \(\times\) gradient of PQ \(= -1\)

$$(-2) \times m_{PQ} = -1$$

$$m_{PQ} = \frac{1}{2}$$

M1 — Using perpendicularity: gradient of radius = \(\frac{1}{2}\)
Part (a) Step 2: Express gradient of PQ in terms of \(k\) and equate ▶
Now calculate the gradient of PQ using the gradient formula \(\frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\) with \(P(-1, k+8)\) and \(Q(3, k^2-2k)\). Set this equal to \(\frac{1}{2}\) and simplify to reach the required equation.

$$m_{PQ} = \frac{(k^2 - 2k) - (k+8)}{3 - (-1)} = \frac{k^2 - 2k - k - 8}{4} = \frac{k^2 - 3k - 8}{4}$$

Set this equal to \(\frac{1}{2}\):

$$\frac{k^2 - 3k - 8}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Multiply both sides by 4:

$$k^2 - 3k - 8 = 2$$

Subtract 2 from both sides:

$$\boxed{k^2 - 3k - 10 = 0} \quad \square$$

M1 — Writing gradient of PQ as \(\frac{k^2 - 3k - 8}{4}\)
A1 — Setting equal to \(\frac{1}{2}\)
A1 — Reaching the given result with clear working
Part (b) Step 3: Solve for \(k\) and find the centre ▶
We solve \(k^2 - 3k - 10 = 0\) to find \(k\). Since we're told \(k\) is positive, we reject the negative root. Then substitute back to find the actual coordinates of P and Q.

$$k^2 - 3k - 10 = 0$$

Factorise: \((k-5)(k+2) = 0\)

$$k = 5 \quad \text{or} \quad k = -2$$

Since \(k\) is positive: \(k = 5\)

Centre P: \((-1, 5+8) = (-1, 13)\)

Point Q: \((3, 25 - 10) = (3, 15)\)

M1 — Solving the quadratic and selecting \(k = 5\)
A1 — Centre \((-1, 13)\)
Part (b) Step 4: Find the radius and write the equation ▶
The radius is the distance PQ. We have both points, so use the distance formula. Then write the equation in the standard form \((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2\).

$$r = PQ = \sqrt{(3-(-1))^2 + (15-13)^2}$$

$$= \sqrt{4^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{16 + 4} = \sqrt{20}$$

The equation of C is:

$$\boxed{(x+1)^2 + (y-13)^2 = 20}$$

M1 — Finding distance PQ as the radius
A1 — Correct equation of C
The equation can also be left in expanded form \(x^2 + y^2 + 2x - 26y + 150 = 0\). Both forms are accepted. Make sure you write \(r^2 = 20\), not \(r^2 = \sqrt{20}\) — a surprisingly common error.

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals:

  • An equation with \(x^2 + y^2\) plus linear terms — always a circle
  • The word "tangent" — use perpendicularity or discriminant = 0
  • "Find the range of values of \(k\)" — always discriminant-based
  • Questions about shortest distance — find the perpendicular from the centre to the line, solve simultaneously for the closest point, use Pythagoras for the distance, then subtract the radius
  • Given a tangent gradient — use the perpendicular gradient relationship for the radius
