Key Concepts: Sampling Methods

Simple Random Sampling

Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.

Method: Number each member 1 to \(N\), then use a random number generator to select \(n\) members.
Advantage: Free from bias, every member equally likely.
Disadvantage: Need a complete sampling frame (list of all members).

Systematic Sampling

Select every \(k\)th member from an ordered list, starting from a random position in the first \(k\) members.

Method: Calculate \(k = \frac{N}{n}\). Choose a random start between 1 and \(k\), then take every \(k\)th item.
Advantage: Simple, quick, suitable for large populations.
Disadvantage: Can introduce bias if there is a periodic pattern in the data.

Stratified Sampling

The population is divided into distinct groups (strata), and a proportional sample is taken from each group.

Formula: Number from each stratum \(= \frac{\text{stratum size}}{\text{population size}} \times \text{sample size}\)
Method: (1) Identify strata, (2) Calculate proportions, (3) Use random sampling within each stratum.
Advantage: Guarantees representation from each group.
Disadvantage: Need to know the strata sizes in advance.

The Large Data Set (LDS)

You must know the Edexcel Large Data Set! It contains daily weather data from the Met Office for 5 UK stations and 3 overseas stations for the months of May–October in 1987 and 2015.

Key LDS Facts to Remember

  • Wind direction is the only qualitative variable (when recorded as compass bearings: N, NE, SW, etc.)
  • "n/a" means data not available (missing data) — must be handled when calculating statistics
  • "tr" (trace) for rainfall means a tiny amount fell — must be coded as a number (usually 0) for calculations
  • Camborne is typically windier (coastal, SW England)
  • Heathrow tends to be warmer (urban heat island)
  • Perth data is in Southern Hemisphere — seasons are reversed
  • Beijing has a monsoon climate — heavy summer rainfall
  • UK wind speeds are typically higher in autumn months (Oct) due to Atlantic weather systems

Worked Examples

Example 1: Stratified Sampling June 2019 Q1(a)

A sixth form college has 84 students in Year 12 and 56 students in Year 13. The head teacher selects a stratified sample of 40 students, stratified by year group.
(a) Describe how this sample could be taken. [3]
Step 1: Calculate the number from each stratum
Total population = \(84 + 56 = 140\)
Year 12: \(\frac{84}{140} \times 40 = 24\) students
Year 13: \(\frac{56}{140} \times 40 = 16\) students
Step 2: Describe creating the sampling frame
Create a numbered list (or database/register) of students for each year group separately.
Step 3: Describe the random selection
Use a random number generator to select 24 students from the Year 12 list and 16 from the Year 13 list.

Full answer (3 marks):

1. Create a numbered list of students in each year group separately.

2. Use a random number generator to select students from each list.

3. Select 24 students from Year 12 and 16 students from Year 13.

Example 2: LDS — Systematic Sampling & Wind Direction Nov 2021 Q3

Helen is studying one of the qualitative variables from the large data set for Heathrow from 2015. She started with the data from 3rd May and then took every 10th reading. There were only 3 different outcomes with frequencies: A = 16, B = 2, C = 1.
(a) State the sampling technique Helen used. [1]
(b) From your knowledge of the large data set:
  (i) suggest which variable was being studied [1]
  (ii) state the name of outcome A [1]
(c) What reliable inference can be made from the samples? [1]
Step 1: Identify the sampling technique
Starting at a fixed point and taking every 10th reading is systematic sampling.
Step 2: Identify the variable
The question says "qualitative variable" with only 3 outcomes. In the LDS, wind direction recorded as compass bearings (e.g. N, SW, W) is the qualitative variable. However, with only 3 outcomes this is likely Daily Mean Wind Direction grouped into broad categories, or more precisely it could be a variable like "weather type" — but actually the most common qualitative variable with few categories at Heathrow is wind direction grouped broadly.

Actually, the correct answer is "Beaufort scale description" or more precisely "Daily Mean Wind Direction". Given the frequencies (16, 2, 1), and that Heathrow in summer mostly has SW winds, outcome A (most common) would be the prevailing direction.
Step 3: Name outcome A
The most common wind direction at Heathrow is from the South-West (SW) or West (W). Given 16 out of 19 readings, the answer is likely "W" or "SW".
Step 4: Inference
From both samples, outcome A dominates heavily. A reliable inference is:
"Outcome A (W/SW wind direction) is the most common outcome at Heathrow during the period covered."

(a) Systematic sampling

(b)(i) (Daily Mean) Wind Direction

(b)(ii) W (West) or SW (South-West)

(c) Outcome A is the most common outcome at Heathrow for this period (both samples and the full data confirm this)

Example 3: LDS — Visibility and Humidity at Camborne June 2020 Q2

Jerry is studying visibility for Camborne using the large data set for June 1987. A table shows Daily Maximum Relative Humidity (%) and Daily Mean Visibility. On 28/06/1987, the humidity was 100% and visibility was recorded as 0.
Given that daily mean visibility is given to the nearest 100,
(a) write down the range of distances in metres that corresponds to the recorded value 0 for the daily mean visibility. [1]
Step 1: Understand the measurement
Daily Mean Visibility in the LDS is measured in decametres (Dm), given to the nearest 100 Dm.
A recorded value of 0 means the actual value rounds to 0 when rounded to the nearest 100.
Step 2: Find the range
Rounded to nearest 100 Dm, a value of 0 means: \(0 \leq \text{visibility} < 50\) Dm
Converting to metres: \(0 \leq \text{visibility} < 500\) m (since 1 Dm = 10 m)
Range: 0 to less than 500 metres

(a) 0 to less than 500 metres (or equivalently, 0 m to 499 m)

Note: This demonstrates knowledge of the units (Dm = decametres) and the rounding convention in the LDS.

Exam Tips

1. Stratified sampling answers need 3 things: (i) a list/frame for each group, (ii) random selection method, (iii) correct numbers from each stratum (show the calculation).
2. Systematic sampling: Always mention the random starting point. "Start at a random position between 1 and \(k\), then take every \(k\)th item."
3. LDS knowledge you MUST have:
- Wind speed is in knots
- Visibility is in decametres (Dm)
- Wind direction is the qualitative variable
- "tr" means trace (rainfall less than 0.05mm)
- "n/a" means data not available
- UK is typically windier in autumn (October)
- Camborne = coastal/windy; Heathrow = warm/urban
- Southern Hemisphere seasons are reversed (Perth)
4. Common LDS mistakes:
- Forgetting that "tr" and "n/a" need handling when calculating statistics
- Not knowing the units (especially Dm for visibility, knots for wind)
- Confusing daily mean with daily maximum values
