Vertical Motion under Gravity

Kinematics with constant acceleration due to gravity — suvat with \(a = \pm g\)

7/7
Sittings
4–12
Marks
Q1
Typical Position
g = 9.8
or 10 m/s²
Why this always comes up: Vertical motion under gravity is the simplest physical application of suvat equations. Examiners love it because it tests sign conventions, multi-stage reasoning, and model refinement — all in one question.

Key Concepts

The suvat equations (with gravity)

For an object moving vertically under gravity alone:

  • \(v = u + at\) where \(a = \pm g\)
  • \(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
  • \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)
  • \(s = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)t\)

Sign convention: Take upwards as positive → \(a = -g\). Or take downwards as positive → \(a = +g\). Be consistent throughout.

Critical facts for exam

  • At highest point: \(v = 0\)
  • Speed on return to same height = initial speed (symmetry)
  • "Show that \(U = ...\)" → use \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) with known endpoint speed
  • Multi-stage: split at direction changes or events (e.g. parachute opens)
  • Model refinements: air resistance, spin, wind, not a particle
Always state your sign convention clearly. Taking "up as positive" means \(g = -9.8\) in your equations. Mark schemes accept either convention as long as it is consistent.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Ball thrown upwards — find time to highest point 3 marks

Style: June 2022 Q1 | View Paper

Question: A small stone is projected vertically upwards with speed \(U\) m s\(^{-1}\) from a point A that is 1.8 m above horizontal ground. The speed of the stone as it hits the ground is 10 m s\(^{-1}\). The acceleration due to gravity is 10 m s\(^{-2}\). Find the value of \(U\).

Step 1: Set up known quantities

Taking downwards as positive (stone ends going down):

\(s = 1.8\) m (net downward displacement from A to ground)

\(v = 10\) m s\(^{-1}\) (speed at ground)

\(u = -U\) m s\(^{-1}\) (initial velocity upwards, so negative in our convention)

\(a = 10\) m s\(^{-2}\) (g downwards)

Step 2: Apply \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) M1

\(10^2 = U^2 + 2(10)(1.8)\)

Wait — taking upwards as positive is cleaner here:

\(u = U\), \(v = -10\) (moving down at ground), \(a = -10\), \(s = -1.8\)

\((-10)^2 = U^2 + 2(-10)(-1.8)\)

\(100 = U^2 + 36\)

Step 3: Solve for \(U\) A1

\(U^2 = 100 - 36 = 64\)

\(U = 8\) m s\(^{-1}\) A1

Example 2: Projected up from height — "show that \(U = 5\)" 4 marks

Based on: June 2020 Q1 | View Paper

Question: At time \(t = 0\), a small ball is projected vertically upwards with speed \(U\) m s\(^{-1}\) from a point A that is 16.8 m above horizontal ground. The speed of the ball at the instant immediately before it hits the ground for the first time is 19 m s\(^{-1}\). The acceleration due to gravity is modelled as 10 m s\(^{-2}\). Show that \(U = 5\).

Step 1: Identify the correct suvat equation

We know \(u\), \(v\), \(s\) and \(a\) — don't need \(t\).

Use \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)

Step 2: Substitute (taking down as positive) M1

Taking down as positive: \(u = -U\), \(v = 19\), \(a = 10\), \(s = 16.8\)

\(19^2 = (-U)^2 + 2(10)(16.8)\)

\(361 = U^2 + 336\) A1

Step 3: Solve and conclude A1

\(U^2 = 361 - 336 = 25\)

\(U = 5\) as required (since \(U > 0\)) A1

For "show that" questions, you must show every algebraic step. Never write the answer first and work backwards. The mark scheme requires seeing the equation before the final line.

Example 3: Multi-stage motion — parachutist 10 marks

Based on: June 2019 Q1 | View Paper

Question: A parachutist falls from rest from a helicopter hovering at 550 m. She free-falls for 3 s (\(g = 10\) m s\(^{-2}\)), then decelerates at 12 m s\(^{-2}\) for 2 s, then travels at constant speed to the ground. Find the total time \(T\).

Step 1: Stage 1 — free fall for 3 s M1

\(v = u + at = 0 + 10(3) = 30\) m s\(^{-1}\) (speed when parachute opens)

Distance: \(s_1 = \frac{1}{2}(10)(3^2) = 45\) m

Step 2: Stage 2 — decelerating for 2 s M1 A1

\(v = 30 - 12(2) = 30 - 24 = 6\) m s\(^{-1}\) (constant speed reached)

Distance: \(s_2 = 30(2) + \frac{1}{2}(-12)(4) = 60 - 24 = 36\) m

Step 3: Stage 3 — constant speed to ground M1 A1

Remaining distance: \(s_3 = 550 - 45 - 36 = 469\) m

At constant speed 6 m s\(^{-1}\): \(t_3 = \frac{469}{6} = 78.1\overline{6}\) s

Step 4: Total time A1

\(T = 3 + 2 + 78.1\overline{6} = 83.1\overline{6}...\)

\(T = 83\) (to nearest whole number) A1

Multi-stage problems: draw a speed-time graph sketch first. Label each phase clearly. The "end speed of one stage = start speed of next stage" is the key linking principle.

Example 4: Model refinement — effect of air resistance 2 marks

Common across all sittings

Question: In a refinement of the model, air resistance is included. The initial speed \(U\) was found to be 5 m s\(^{-1}\) using the model without air resistance. How would the new value of \(U\) compare with 5?

Step 1: Reasoning M1

Air resistance opposes motion. On the way down, air resistance slows the ball, so the final speed would be less than 19 m s\(^{-1}\) without a higher initial speed.

Alternatively: air resistance reduces the speed at which the ball hits the ground, so to still hit at 19 m s\(^{-1}\), the initial speed must be greater.

Step 2: Conclusion A1

The new value of \(U\) would be greater than 5 because air resistance reduces the actual speed gained during the fall, so a larger initial speed is needed to achieve the same final speed.

Model refinement marks require a REASON, not just "higher" or "lower". State the physical effect (air resistance opposes motion / reduces speed) then give the consequence for the quantity asked about.

Common Pitfalls

  • Sign errors: Mixing up positive direction mid-question. Pick one convention and stick to it.
  • Using \(g = 9.8\) when question says 10: Read the question — some use \(g = 10\), others \(g = 9.8\).
  • Forgetting \(v = 0\) at highest point: This is the key to finding max height or time up.
  • Speed vs velocity: Speed is always positive. If asked for speed and you get negative velocity, take the magnitude.
  • "Show that" without working: Must show the substitution step before reaching the given answer.
