Constant Acceleration & Velocity-Time Graphs

Suvat equations and interpreting v-t graphs for multi-stage motion problems

5/7
Sittings
6–8
Marks
Q1–Q2
Typical Position
v-t graph
Often Required
Why this comes up frequently: Velocity-time graph problems combine graphical reasoning with suvat calculations. They test whether students can interpret gradients as acceleration, areas as displacement, and link multiple stages of motion together — all essential for the mechanics toolkit.

Key Concepts

The suvat equations

  • \(v = u + at\)
  • \(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
  • \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)
  • \(s = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t\)
  • \(s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

Only valid when acceleration is constant.

Velocity-time graph interpretation

  • Gradient = acceleration (steeper = greater acceleration)
  • Area under graph = displacement (use trapezium/triangle areas)
  • Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration)
  • Line to axis = deceleration to rest

Multi-stage motion strategy

  1. Draw/label the v-t graph with times and speeds at each transition
  2. Use total area = total distance (for same direction) or set up equation
  3. Calculate gradient for acceleration in each phase
  4. Link phases: end speed of one = start speed of next
When a v-t graph has 3 phases (accelerate, constant, decelerate), the total distance is the area of the resulting trapezium: \(\frac{1}{2}(a + b) \times h\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are the parallel sides (time at top speed and total time) and \(h\) is the max speed.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Train between stations 8 marks

Based on: June 2022 Q2 | View Paper

Question: A train travels from station P to Q. It starts from rest, accelerates to 25 m s\(^{-1}\), maintains this speed, then decelerates to rest. The deceleration time is four times the acceleration time. Total journey: 700 s. Distance: 15 km. Show the acceleration time is 40 s. Find the acceleration.

Step 1: Set up variables from v-t graph M1

Let acceleration time = \(T\) seconds.

Deceleration time = \(4T\) seconds.

Constant speed time = \(700 - T - 4T = 700 - 5T\) seconds.

Max speed = 25 m s\(^{-1}\)

Step 2: Total distance = area under v-t graph M1 A1

The v-t graph is a trapezium with:

Area = \(\frac{1}{2}(a + b) \times h\) where parallel sides are time at constant speed and total time

Or calculate each section:

Phase 1: \(\frac{1}{2}(25)(T)\)

Phase 2: \(25(700 - 5T)\)

Phase 3: \(\frac{1}{2}(25)(4T)\)

Total: \(\frac{25T}{2} + 25(700 - 5T) + 50T = 15000\)

Step 3: Solve for \(T\) A1

\(12.5T + 17500 - 125T + 50T = 15000\)

\(-62.5T + 17500 = 15000\)

\(62.5T = 2500\)

\(T = 40\) s as required

Step 4: Find acceleration A1

Acceleration = gradient of first section = \(\dfrac{25 - 0}{40} = 0.625\) m s\(^{-2}\)

For "show that" questions about time: set up the area equation using algebra, then solve. The key is recognising that total area under v-t graph = total distance.

Example 2: Two children race — same finish time 8 marks

Based on: June 2023 Q1 | View Paper

Question: Pat accelerates from rest at constant rate for 5 s to 4 m s\(^{-1}\), then constant speed. Sam accelerates at 1 m s\(^{-2}\) from rest to \(X\) m s\(^{-1}\), then constant speed. Both take 27.5 s. Find: (a) why areas under graphs are equal, (b) Pat's acceleration, (c) race length, (d) value of \(X\).

Step 1: Why areas are equal B1

Area under v-t graph = displacement (distance). Both children travel the same distance (same race), so areas are equal.

Step 2: Pat's acceleration B1

Acceleration = \(\dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{4 - 0}{5} = 0.8\) m s\(^{-2}\)

Step 3: Race length (from Pat's graph) M1 A1

Pat's distance = area under Pat's v-t graph:

\(= \frac{1}{2}(5)(4) + 4(27.5 - 5)\)

\(= 10 + 4(22.5)\)

\(= 10 + 90 = 100\) m

Step 4: Find \(X\) from Sam's graph M1 A1 A1 A1

Sam accelerates at 1 m s\(^{-2}\) to reach \(X\) m s\(^{-1}\).

Time to accelerate: \(t = \dfrac{X}{1} = X\) seconds

Sam's distance = \(\frac{1}{2}(X)(X) + X(27.5 - X) = 100\)

\(\frac{X^2}{2} + 27.5X - X^2 = 100\)

\(-\frac{X^2}{2} + 27.5X = 100\)

\(X^2 - 55X + 200 = 0\)

Using the quadratic formula:

\(X = \dfrac{55 \pm \sqrt{3025 - 800}}{2} = \dfrac{55 \pm \sqrt{2225}}{2}\)

\(X = \dfrac{55 - \sqrt{2225}}{2} = 3.91\) (3 s.f.)

(Reject \(X = \dfrac{55 + \sqrt{2225}}{2} = 51.1\) since Sam must reach \(X\) before 27.5 s, and \(X > 27.5\) is impossible)

Example 3: Speed-time graph to distance-time graph 6 marks

Based on: June 2024 Q1 | View Paper

Question: A car starts from rest, accelerates uniformly for 5 s to 5 m s\(^{-1}\), travels at constant speed for 15 s, then decelerates to rest over 10 s. Total time: 30 s. Find distance AB. Sketch the distance-time graph.

Step 1: Distance = area under speed-time graph M1

Phase 1 (0 to 5 s): triangle = \(\frac{1}{2}(5)(5) = 12.5\) m

Phase 2 (5 to 20 s): rectangle = \(5 \times 15 = 75\) m

Phase 3 (20 to 30 s): triangle = \(\frac{1}{2}(5)(10) = 25\) m

Step 2: Total distance A1 A1

AB = \(12.5 + 75 + 25 = 112.5\) m

Step 3: Sketch distance-time graph B1 B1 B1

Key features of the distance-time graph:

  • 0 to 5 s: curve (concave up) — increasing gradient as speed increases
  • 5 to 20 s: straight line — constant gradient (constant speed)
  • 20 to 30 s: curve (concave down) — decreasing gradient as car slows
  • Starts at (0, 0), ends at (30, 112.5)
  • No sharp corners between sections — smooth transitions
Distance-time sketches: accelerating = curve getting steeper (concave up); constant speed = straight line; decelerating = curve getting flatter (concave down). The gradient of the distance-time graph IS the speed. Mark schemes require: correct shape in each phase, smooth joins, and correct end values.

Example 4: Find speed at a given time during deceleration 2 marks

Based on: June 2022 Q2(d)

Question: A train accelerates for 40 s to reach 25 m s\(^{-1}\), maintains speed until \(t = 540\) s, then decelerates for 160 s. Find the speed at \(t = 572\) s.

Step 1: Identify phase and find deceleration M1

Deceleration phase: from \(t = 540\) to \(t = 700\) (160 s to stop).

At \(t = 572\): this is 32 s into the deceleration phase.

Deceleration = \(\dfrac{25 - 0}{160} = \dfrac{25}{160}\) m s\(^{-2}\)

Step 2: Apply \(v = u + at\) A1

\(v = 25 - \dfrac{25}{160}(32) = 25 - 5 = 20\) m s\(^{-1}\)

Common Pitfalls

  • Units confusion: Distance in km but speed in m/s — convert! (15 km = 15000 m)
  • Area vs gradient: Area = displacement, gradient = acceleration. Students sometimes mix these up.
  • Deceleration time ≠ acceleration time: Read carefully — they may differ.
  • Quadratic with two solutions: Check both are physically valid. Reject if time > total time or speed > max speed.
  • Model limitations: "Constant acceleration is unrealistic — in practice acceleration varies" gets the mark.
