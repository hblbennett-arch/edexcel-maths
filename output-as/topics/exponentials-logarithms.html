Guided Approach

The key techniques you need for exponentials and logarithms at AS level:

1
Laws of Logarithms
\(\log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a(xy)\) ... \(\log_a x - \log_a y = \log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)\) ... \(n\log_a x = \log_a(x^n)\)
Also: \(\log_a a = 1\) and \(\log_a 1 = 0\). These are used to combine or split log expressions before solving.
2
Solving Log Equations
Use the laws to combine logs into a single \(\log_a(\text{expression}) = k\). Then convert to exponential form: \(\text{expression} = a^k\). Finally solve the resulting algebraic equation. Always check solutions are valid (arguments of logs must be positive).
3
Solving Exponential Equations
For \(a^{f(x)} = b^{g(x)}\): either write both sides with the same base, or take logs of both sides. For equations like \(4^{3x-2} = 2^{4x}\), rewrite \(4\) as \(2^2\) to get a common base, then equate indices.
4
Log-Linear Graphs and Exponential Models
If \(y = ab^x\), take logs: \(\log y = \log a + x\log b\). This is a straight line in \(\log y\) vs \(x\) with gradient \(\log b\) and intercept \(\log a\). Read gradient and intercept from the graph, then solve for \(a\) and \(b\) using \(a = 10^{\text{intercept}}\) and \(b = 10^{\text{gradient}}\).

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Misusing the power law: \(2\log x \neq \log 2x\). Many candidates confuse the multiplier with the argument. Remember: \(2\log x = \log(x^2)\).
  • Forgetting to check domain: If your solution gives a negative argument inside a log (e.g. \(x = -3\) in \(\log_2 x\)), it must be rejected. State why explicitly.
  • Wrong direction on log-linear graphs: Confusing gradient and intercept. If \(\log_{10} P = mx + c\), then \(b = 10^m\) (not \(10^c\)) and \(a = 10^c\) (not \(10^m\)).
  • Not writing logs to the same base: When combining logs, they must all have the same base. You cannot combine \(\log_2\) and \(\log_3\) directly.
  • Treating \(\log(A+B)\) as \(\log A + \log B\): This is the single most common error. The addition law is \(\log A + \log B = \log(AB)\), NOT \(\log(A+B)\).

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write the log law being used at each step (e.g. "using \(\log a - \log b = \log\frac{a}{b}\)")
  • State validity checks: "Since \(3x-2 > 0\) requires \(x > \frac{2}{3}\), reject \(x = ...\)"
  • In log-linear questions, explicitly write \(\log y = \log a + x\log b\) and label which part is the gradient and which is the intercept before reading values from the graph
  • Leave answers in exact form when asked (e.g. \(x = 10^{0.5} = \sqrt{10}\) rather than \(x = 3.16\))

AS Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 9 (8 marks)

Given that \(p = \log_3 x\), where \(x > 0\), find in simplest form in terms of \(p\):

(a)(i) \(\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right)\) [2 marks]

(a)(ii) \(\log_3(9x^3)\) [2 marks]

(b) Hence, or otherwise, solve \(2\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right) + 3\log_3(9x^3) = 11\) [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a)(i) Step 1: Express \(\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right)\) in terms of \(p\) ▶
We are given \(p = \log_3 x\), and need to rewrite \(\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right)\) using \(p\). The subtraction law splits a log of a fraction: \(\log\frac{A}{B} = \log A - \log B\). Since \(9 = 3^2\), we know \(\log_3 9 = 2\).

Apply the subtraction law: \(\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right) = \log_3 x - \log_3 9\)

$$\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right) = \log_3 x - \log_3 9$$

We know \(\log_3 x = p\) and \(\log_3 9 = \log_3 3^2 = 2\):

$$= p - 2$$

M1 — Using the subtraction law of logs correctly
A1 — \(p - 2\)
Part (a)(ii) Step 2: Express \(\log_3(9x^3)\) in terms of \(p\) ▶
This time we have a product inside the log. The addition law splits it: \(\log(AB) = \log A + \log B\). Then \(\log_3(x^3) = 3\log_3 x = 3p\) by the power law.

Apply the addition law: \(\log_3(9x^3) = \log_3 9 + \log_3(x^3)\)

$$\log_3(9x^3) = \log_3 9 + \log_3(x^3)$$

Use \(\log_3 9 = 2\) and the power law \(\log_3(x^3) = 3\log_3 x = 3p\):

$$= 2 + 3p$$

M1 — Using the addition and power laws of logs
A1 — \(2 + 3p\)
Part (b) Step 3: Substitute into the equation and solve for \(p\) ▶
Part (b) says "hence" — this means we should use our answers from (a). Replace each log expression with what we found in terms of \(p\), then solve the resulting linear equation in \(p\).

The equation is: \(2\log_3\left(\frac{x}{9}\right) + 3\log_3(9x^3) = 11\)

Substitute our expressions from (a):

$$2(p-2) + 3(2+3p) = 11$$

Expand the brackets:

$$2p - 4 + 6 + 9p = 11$$

Collect like terms:

$$11p + 2 = 11$$

Subtract 2:

$$11p = 9$$

Divide by 11:

$$p = \frac{9}{11}$$

M1 — Substituting parts (a)(i) and (a)(ii) into the equation
A1 — Correct linear equation in \(p\)
Part (b) Step 4: Convert back to find \(x\) ▶
We found \(p = \frac{9}{11}\), but the question asks us to solve for \(x\). Since \(p = \log_3 x\), converting from log form to exponential form gives \(x = 3^p\).

Since \(p = \log_3 x\), we have \(x = 3^p\):

$$x = 3^{9/11}$$

dM1 — Using \(\log_3 x = p\) to find \(x = 3^p\)
A1 — \(x = 3^{9/11}\)
Many candidates lost the final mark by leaving the answer as \(p = \frac{9}{11}\) without converting back to \(x\). The question says "solve" the equation in \(x\), so you must give your answer in terms of \(x\). Accept equivalent forms like \(x = \sqrt[11]{3^9}\).

AS Paper 1 — June 2020 — Question 5 (5 marks)

Solve the equation

$$4^{3x-2} = 2^{4x}$$

Show each step of your working clearly.

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Solution Step 1: Rewrite both sides with the same base ▶
The equation has \(4^{(\ldots)}\) on one side and \(2^{(\ldots)}\) on the other. We cannot equate the exponents directly because the bases are different. But since \(4 = 2^2\), we can rewrite everything in terms of base 2. Once both sides have the same base, we can equate the exponents.

Since \(4 = 2^2\), rewrite the left side:

$$4^{3x-2} = (2^2)^{3x-2}$$

Use the law \((a^m)^n = a^{mn}\):

$$= 2^{2(3x-2)} = 2^{6x-4}$$

The equation becomes:

$$2^{6x-4} = 2^{4x}$$

M1 — Writing \(4\) as \(2^2\) and attempting to use the index law
A1 — Correct exponent: \(2^{6x-4}\)
Solution Step 2: Equate the exponents and solve ▶
When we have \(2^A = 2^B\), since the bases are equal and the function \(2^x\) is one-to-one, we can conclude \(A = B\). This gives us a simple linear equation to solve.

Since the bases are equal, equate the exponents:

$$6x - 4 = 4x$$

Subtract \(4x\) from both sides:

$$2x - 4 = 0$$

Add 4:

$$2x = 4$$

Divide by 2:

$$\boxed{x = 2}$$

M1 — Setting the exponents equal (implied or stated)
A1 — Correct linear equation \(6x-4 = 4x\)
A1 — \(x = 2\)
Some candidates took logs of both sides (valid alternative method): \((3x-2)\log 4 = 4x \log 2\), which simplifies to \((3x-2) \times 2\log 2 = 4x\log 2\), divide by \(\log 2\) to get \(2(3x-2) = 4x\). Both methods earn full marks. The common base approach is generally cleaner at AS level.

AS Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 7 (6 marks)

A colony of bacteria is growing. Data recorded indicates that there is a linear relationship between \(t\) and \(\log_{10} A\) given by:

$$\log_{10} A = 0.03t + 0.5$$

where \(A\) is the area covered by the bacteria in cm\(^2\) and \(t\) is the time in hours after the experiment began.

(a) Show that this relationship can be written in the form \(A = pq^t\), stating the values of \(p\) and \(q\) to 3 significant figures. [3 marks]

(b) Using the model, find the time at which the area covered will be 4 cm\(^2\). [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Link the log-linear form to \(A = pq^t\) ▶
We need to show that \(\log_{10} A = 0.03t + 0.5\) is equivalent to \(A = pq^t\). The key insight: if \(A = pq^t\), then taking \(\log_{10}\) of both sides gives \(\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + t\log_{10} q\). Comparing this with the given equation tells us the gradient and intercept.

Starting with the target form \(A = pq^t\), take \(\log_{10}\) of both sides:

$$\log_{10} A = \log_{10}(pq^t)$$

Use the addition law \(\log(AB) = \log A + \log B\):

$$\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + \log_{10}(q^t)$$

Use the power law \(\log(q^t) = t\log q\):

$$\log_{10} A = \log_{10} p + t\log_{10} q$$

Comparing with \(\log_{10} A = 0.03t + 0.5\):

  • Intercept: \(\log_{10} p = 0.5\)
  • Gradient: \(\log_{10} q = 0.03\)
M1 — Establishing the link between the linear form and \(A = pq^t\) by taking logs or comparing coefficients
Part (a) Step 2: Find \(p\) and \(q\) ▶
We have \(\log_{10} p = 0.5\) and \(\log_{10} q = 0.03\). To find \(p\) and \(q\) themselves, we convert from log form to exponential form: if \(\log_{10} x = k\) then \(x = 10^k\).

From \(\log_{10} p = 0.5\):

$$p = 10^{0.5} = \sqrt{10} = 3.16 \text{ (3 s.f.)}$$

From \(\log_{10} q = 0.03\):

$$q = 10^{0.03} = 1.07 \text{ (3 s.f.)}$$

Therefore: \(A = 3.16 \times 1.07^t\)

A1 — \(p = 10^{0.5}\) or \(\sqrt{10}\) or 3.16
A1 — \(q = 10^{0.03}\) or 1.07
Both exact forms (\(10^{0.5}\), \(10^{0.03}\)) and decimal approximations (3.16, 1.07) are accepted. The question asks for 3 significant figures, so give decimals. Do not confuse \(p\) and \(q\) — \(p\) is the intercept (initial value), \(q\) is the base (growth factor).
Part (b) Step 3: Find \(t\) when \(A = 4\) ▶
We need to solve for \(t\) when \(A = 4\). The simplest approach is to substitute \(A = 4\) directly into the log-linear equation \(\log_{10} A = 0.03t + 0.5\), since we can compute \(\log_{10} 4\) directly. Alternatively, use the exponential form and take logs.

Substitute \(A = 4\) into \(\log_{10} A = 0.03t + 0.5\):

$$\log_{10} 4 = 0.03t + 0.5$$

Calculate \(\log_{10} 4 = 0.6021...\):

$$0.6021... = 0.03t + 0.5$$

Subtract 0.5:

$$0.1021... = 0.03t$$

Divide by 0.03:

$$t = \frac{0.1021...}{0.03} = 3.40... \approx 3.4 \text{ hours}$$

M1 — Substituting \(A = 4\) into the log-linear equation (or equivalent)
M1 — Correct rearrangement to isolate \(t\)
A1 — \(t = 3.4\) hours (accept awrt 3.4)
Using the rounded model \(A = 3.16 \times 1.07^t\) will give a slightly different answer due to premature rounding. Best practice: use the exact log-linear form for part (b). Accept answers in the range 3.3 to 3.5 depending on the method used.

AS Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 14 (7 marks)

The value, \(V\) pounds, of a vintage car \(t\) years after it was first valued on 1st January 2001, is modelled by the equation \(V = ab^t\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants.

The line \(l\) shown in Figure 3 illustrates the linear relationship between \(t\) and \(\log_{10} V\) since the car was first valued.

The equation of line \(l\) is \(\log_{10} V = 0.037t + 4.4\)

(a) Find, to 4 significant figures, the value of \(a\) and the value of \(b\). [4 marks]

(b) With reference to the model, interpret the value of the constant \(b\). [1 mark]

(c) Find the year during which the value of the car first exceeds \(\pounds 100\,000\). [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Compare with the standard log-linear form ▶
This is the standard log-linear model. If \(V = ab^t\), then \(\log_{10} V = \log_{10} a + t\log_{10} b\). We compare this with the given equation \(\log_{10} V = 0.037t + 4.4\) to read off \(\log_{10} a\) (the constant term) and \(\log_{10} b\) (the coefficient of \(t\)).

Taking \(\log_{10}\) of \(V = ab^t\):

$$\log_{10} V = \log_{10} a + t\log_{10} b$$

Comparing with \(\log_{10} V = 0.037t + 4.4\):

  • Coefficient of \(t\): \(\log_{10} b = 0.037\)
  • Constant term: \(\log_{10} a = 4.4\)
M1 — Correctly identifying \(\log_{10} a = 4.4\) and \(\log_{10} b = 0.037\)
Part (a) Step 2: Calculate \(a\) and \(b\) ▶
Convert from log form to exponential form. If \(\log_{10} a = 4.4\) then \(a = 10^{4.4}\). Similarly for \(b\). Then round to 4 significant figures as the question requires.

From \(\log_{10} a = 4.4\):

$$a = 10^{4.4} = 25\,119 \text{ (4 s.f.)} = 25\,120 \text{ (4 s.f.)}$$

From \(\log_{10} b = 0.037\):

$$b = 10^{0.037} = 1.089 \text{ (4 s.f.)}$$

A1 — \(a = 25\,120\) (accept awrt 25100 or 25120)
A1 — \(b = 1.089\)
The value of \(a\) represents the initial value of the car (in pounds) on 1st January 2001. Make sure you round correctly to 4 significant figures as specified.
Part (b) Step 3: Interpret the constant \(b\) ▶
In the model \(V = ab^t\), when \(t\) increases by 1 (one more year passes), \(V\) is multiplied by \(b\). Since \(b = 1.089\), each year the value is multiplied by 1.089, meaning an 8.9% increase per year.

Each year, the value of the car is multiplied by \(b = 1.089\), representing an annual increase of approximately 8.9%.

B1 — Correct interpretation linking \(b\) to annual growth/multiplication factor in context
The key phrase is "each year the value is multiplied by 1.089" or "8.9% increase per year". Answers must reference the context (the car's value increasing over time).
Part (c) Step 4: Find when \(V > 100\,000\) ▶
We need the year when \(V\) first exceeds 100,000. Substitute \(V = 100\,000\) into the log-linear equation to find the exact \(t\), then identify the year (remembering \(t = 0\) corresponds to 2001).

Substitute \(V = 100\,000\) into \(\log_{10} V = 0.037t + 4.4\):

$$\log_{10}(100\,000) = 0.037t + 4.4$$

Since \(\log_{10}(100\,000) = 5\):

$$5 = 0.037t + 4.4$$

Subtract 4.4:

$$0.6 = 0.037t$$

Divide by 0.037:

$$t = \frac{0.6}{0.037} = 16.2...$$

Since \(t = 0\) is 2001 and \(t = 16.2\) means during the 17th year after 2001:

The car first exceeds \(\pounds 100\,000\) during the year 2017.

M1 — Substituting \(V = 100\,000\) and solving for \(t\)
A1 — Year 2017 (or 2018 if interpreted as "by end of year")
The question asks "during which year" — since \(t = 16.2\) means part-way through the 17th year (2001 + 16 = 2017), the answer is 2017. Some candidates wrote 2018 by adding 17 to 2001, which was also accepted by some marking approaches.

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals in the question:

  • The word "logarithm" or "log" in any form
  • Equations with terms like \(\log_a x\), \(\ln x\), or \(\log_{10}\)
  • Exponential equations like \(3^x = 5\) or \(4^{2x} = 2^{3x+1}\)
  • A graph of \(\log y\) against \(x\) (or \(\log y\) against \(\log x\)) — signals a log-linear model
  • The phrase "using the laws of logarithms" in the question
  • Models of the form \(y = ab^x\) or \(y = ax^n\) (exponential or power models)
  • "Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable" — you must show algebraic log working

Key Formulae Reference

Laws of Logarithms Learn this

  • Addition law: \(\log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a(xy)\)
  • Subtraction law: \(\log_a x - \log_a y = \log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)\)
  • Power law: \(n\log_a x = \log_a(x^n)\)
  • Definition: \(\log_a b = c \iff a^c = b\)
  • Special values: \(\log_a a = 1\), \(\log_a 1 = 0\)

Exponential & Logarithmic Models Learn this

  • Exponential model: \(y = ab^x \implies \log y = \log a + x\log b\) (straight line in \(\log y\) vs \(x\))
  • Power model: \(y = ax^n \implies \log y = \log a + n\log x\) (straight line in \(\log y\) vs \(\log x\))
