Key Formulae

$$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$

Equation of line through \((x_1, y_1)\) with gradient \(m\)

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Gradient between two points

$$m_1 \times m_2 = -1$$

Perpendicular lines condition (negative reciprocal gradients)

$$\text{Midpoint} = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

Midpoint of a line segment

$$\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2}|x_1(y_2-y_3) + x_2(y_3-y_1) + x_3(y_1-y_2)|$$

Area from three vertices (the "shoelace" formula)

Guided Approach

Common strategies for straight line questions:

1
Find the gradient
From two points: \(m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\). From an equation: rearrange to \(y = mx + c\) and read off \(m\). For perpendicular: take the negative reciprocal \(-\frac{1}{m}\).
2
Write the equation of a line
Use \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) when you know the gradient and one point. Rearrange to the required form (e.g. \(ax + by + c = 0\) or \(y = mx + c\)).
3
Find intersections
Set the two line equations equal (or substitute one into the other). Solve the resulting linear equation for \(x\), then back-substitute to find \(y\).
4
Find areas
For triangles: use \(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\) if the base is along an axis. Otherwise use the shoelace formula or split into simpler shapes.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Confusing parallel and perpendicular: Parallel = same gradient. Perpendicular = product of gradients is \(-1\). State which condition you're testing.
  • Arithmetic in gradient calculation: Watch out for double negatives: \(\frac{-3 - 1}{5 - (-3)} = \frac{-4}{8} = -\frac{1}{2}\). Candidates often get the sign wrong.
  • Not rearranging to the required form: If the question says "in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\)", make sure your answer is in that form with integer coefficients.
  • Losing marks on "show that": If asked to show a line has a specific equation, you must show the gradient calculation and the substitution into \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\). Just writing the answer earns zero.
  • Area calculation errors: Using the wrong base or height. Always identify which line segment is your base, and ensure the height is perpendicular to it.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Label gradients clearly: "\(m_1 = \frac{1}{4}\)", "\(m_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\)"
  • State the perpendicularity condition: "\(m_1 \times m_2 = \frac{1}{4} \times (-4) = -1\), so perpendicular"
  • For area questions: draw a quick sketch to identify the vertices and choose the simplest base
  • Check answers by substituting intersection points back into both original equations

Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 4 (4 marks)

The line \(l_1\) has equation \(4y - x = 10\).

The line \(l_2\) passes through the points \((-3, 1)\) and \((5, -3)\).

Determine, giving full reasons for your answer, whether lines \(l_1\) and \(l_2\) are parallel, perpendicular, or neither.

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Step 1: Find the gradient of \(l_1\) ▶
To determine the relationship between two lines, we need both their gradients. For \(l_1\), the equation is given but not in \(y = mx + c\) form. Rearranging will reveal the gradient.

$$4y - x = 10$$

Add \(x\) to both sides:

$$4y = x + 10$$

Divide by 4:

$$y = \frac{1}{4}x + \frac{10}{4}$$

Gradient of \(l_1\): \(m_1 = \frac{1}{4}\)

B1 — Gradient of \(l_1 = \frac{1}{4}\)
Step 2: Find the gradient of \(l_2\) ▶
For \(l_2\) we're given two points. Use the gradient formula \(m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\).

$$m_2 = \frac{-3 - 1}{5 - (-3)} = \frac{-4}{8} = -\frac{1}{2}$$

Gradient of \(l_2\): \(m_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\)

M1A1 — Correct gradient of \(l_2 = -\frac{1}{2}\)
Step 3: Test the conditions and conclude ▶
Now test: are they parallel (\(m_1 = m_2\))? Perpendicular (\(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\))? Or neither?

Test for parallel: \(m_1 = \frac{1}{4} \neq -\frac{1}{2} = m_2\). Not parallel.

Test for perpendicular:

$$m_1 \times m_2 = \frac{1}{4} \times \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = -\frac{1}{8}$$

This is NOT equal to \(-1\), so the lines are NOT perpendicular.

Conclusion: The lines are neither parallel nor perpendicular.

A1 — Correct conclusion "neither" with full reasoning (product shown, not equal to \(-1\))
You must show the product \(m_1 \times m_2 = -\frac{1}{8}\) explicitly and state that this is not equal to \(-1\). Simply writing "neither" without the calculation earns no marks. Also state that the gradients are not equal (for parallel). Full reasoning is required.

Paper 1 — June 2023 — Question 8 (8 marks)

The line \(l_1\) has equation \(y = \frac{3}{5}x - 6\).

The line \(l_2\) is perpendicular to \(l_1\) and passes through the point \(B(8, 0)\).

(a) Show that an equation for line \(l_2\) is \(5x + 3y = 40\). [3 marks]

Given that lines \(l_1\) and \(l_2\) intersect at the point C, and line \(l_1\) crosses the x-axis at point A:

(b) Find the exact area of triangle ABC, giving your answer as a fully simplified fraction. [5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Find the gradient of \(l_2\) ▶
\(l_2\) is perpendicular to \(l_1\). The gradient of \(l_1\) is \(\frac{3}{5}\). For perpendicular lines, \(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\), so \(m_2 = -\frac{5}{3}\).

Gradient of \(l_1 = \frac{3}{5}\)

Since \(l_2 \perp l_1\):

$$m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1} = -\frac{1}{\frac{3}{5}} = -\frac{5}{3}$$

M1 — Using perpendicular gradient relationship
Part (a) Step 2: Write the equation of \(l_2\) using point B(8, 0) ▶
We know the gradient (\(-\frac{5}{3}\)) and a point on the line (\(B(8, 0)\)). Substitute into \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) and rearrange to the required form \(5x + 3y = 40\).

$$y - 0 = -\frac{5}{3}(x - 8)$$

Multiply both sides by 3:

$$3y = -5(x - 8)$$

$$3y = -5x + 40$$

Add \(5x\) to both sides:

$$\boxed{5x + 3y = 40} \quad \square$$

A1 — Correct substitution into line equation
A1 — Reaching given equation with clear working
Part (b) Step 3: Find where \(l_1\) crosses the x-axis (point A) ▶
Point A is where \(l_1\) crosses the x-axis, i.e. where \(y = 0\). Substitute into the equation of \(l_1\).

$$0 = \frac{3}{5}x - 6$$

Add 6 to both sides:

$$6 = \frac{3}{5}x$$

Multiply by \(\frac{5}{3}\):

$$x = 10$$

Point A is \((10, 0)\)

B1 — Point A = (10, 0)
Part (b) Step 4: Find the intersection point C ▶
Point C is where \(l_1\) and \(l_2\) intersect. Substitute \(y = \frac{3}{5}x - 6\) from \(l_1\) into \(5x + 3y = 40\) from \(l_2\), and solve.

Substitute \(y = \frac{3}{5}x - 6\) into \(5x + 3y = 40\):

$$5x + 3\left(\frac{3}{5}x - 6\right) = 40$$

$$5x + \frac{9}{5}x - 18 = 40$$

Multiply through by 5:

$$25x + 9x - 90 = 200$$

$$34x = 290$$

$$x = \frac{290}{34} = \frac{145}{17}$$

Find \(y\): \(y = \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{145}{17} - 6 = \frac{435}{85} - 6 = \frac{87}{17} - 6 = \frac{87 - 102}{17} = -\frac{15}{17}\)

Point C: \(\left(\frac{145}{17}, -\frac{15}{17}\right)\)

M1 — Solving the simultaneous equations
A1 — Correct coordinates of C
Part (b) Step 5: Calculate the area of triangle ABC ▶
We have A(10, 0), B(8, 0), and C(\(\frac{145}{17}, -\frac{15}{17}\)). Since A and B both lie on the x-axis, the base AB is horizontal with length \(|10 - 8| = 2\). The height is the perpendicular distance from C to the x-axis, which is simply \(|y_C| = \frac{15}{17}\).

$$\text{Base} = AB = |10 - 8| = 2$$

$$\text{Height} = |y_C| = \frac{15}{17}$$

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 2 \times \frac{15}{17} = \frac{15}{17}$$

Area of triangle ABC \(= \frac{15}{17}\)

M1 — Using appropriate method to find the area
A1 — Correct exact area \(\frac{15}{17}\)
The key insight is noticing that A and B are both on the x-axis, so the base is simply the horizontal distance between them and the height is the y-coordinate of C. This avoids the more complex shoelace formula. Always look for shortcuts like this.

Paper 1 — November 2021 — Question 5 (5 marks)

A curve C has equation \(y = x^2 + 3x + 1\). The point P has coordinates \((2, 11)\) and lies on C.

(a) Find the gradient of the tangent to the curve at P. [2 marks]

The point Q has coordinates \((2+h, (2+h)^2 + 3(2+h) + 1)\) and also lies on C.

(b) Find the gradient of the line PQ, giving your answer in terms of \(h\) in simplest form. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Differentiate and substitute \(x = 2\) ▶
The gradient of the tangent at a point is found by differentiating the curve equation and evaluating at that x-coordinate.

$$y = x^2 + 3x + 1$$

Differentiate:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x + 3$$

At \(x = 2\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{x=2} = 2(2) + 3 = 7$$

Gradient of tangent at P = 7

M1 — Differentiating \(y = x^2 + 3x + 1\)
A1 — Gradient = 7
Part (b) Step 2: Find the y-coordinate of Q and compute gradient PQ ▶
The gradient of PQ is \(\frac{y_Q - y_P}{x_Q - x_P}\). We know \(P = (2, 11)\) and \(Q = (2+h, \;(2+h)^2 + 3(2+h) + 1)\). The denominator simplifies to \(h\). The numerator needs expanding.

First, find \(y_Q\):

$$y_Q = (2+h)^2 + 3(2+h) + 1$$

Expand \((2+h)^2 = 4 + 4h + h^2\):

$$= 4 + 4h + h^2 + 6 + 3h + 1$$

$$= h^2 + 7h + 11$$

Now find the gradient:

$$m_{PQ} = \frac{y_Q - y_P}{x_Q - x_P} = \frac{(h^2 + 7h + 11) - 11}{(2+h) - 2}$$

$$= \frac{h^2 + 7h}{h}$$

Factor out \(h\) from numerator:

$$= \frac{h(h + 7)}{h} = h + 7$$

Gradient of PQ = \(h + 7\)

M1 — Finding \(y_Q\) by expanding
M1 — Setting up gradient formula \(\frac{y_Q - 11}{h}\)
A1 — Simplified to \(h + 7\)
Notice that as \(h \to 0\), the gradient of PQ approaches \(0 + 7 = 7\), which matches the tangent gradient from Part (a). This is the whole point of the question — it's showing how differentiation from first principles works. The connection earns understanding marks in the actual exam.

Paper 1 — June 2024 — Question 3 (5 marks)

The points A, D, and C lie on a straight line (ADC). Given:

  • \(A = (1, 7)\)
  • \(D\) is the midpoint of AC
  • \(C = (5, 3)\)

(a) Find the coordinates of D. [2 marks]

(b) Find the equation of the line passing through A and C in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\) where \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) are integers. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Find the midpoint D ▶
D is the midpoint of AC. Use the midpoint formula: average the x-coordinates, average the y-coordinates.

$$D = \left(\frac{1 + 5}{2}, \frac{7 + 3}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{6}{2}, \frac{10}{2}\right) = (3, 5)$$

D = (3, 5)

M1 — Using the midpoint formula
A1 — \(D = (3, 5)\)
Part (b) Step 2: Find gradient of AC and write equation ▶
Find the gradient using A(1, 7) and C(5, 3), then use \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) with either point. Finally, rearrange to \(ax + by + c = 0\) with integer coefficients.

$$m = \frac{3 - 7}{5 - 1} = \frac{-4}{4} = -1$$

Use point A(1, 7):

$$y - 7 = -1(x - 1)$$

$$y - 7 = -x + 1$$

Rearrange to \(ax + by + c = 0\):

$$x + y - 8 = 0$$

Equation: \(x + y - 8 = 0\)

M1 — Finding gradient = \(-1\)
M1 — Substituting into \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\)
A1 — Correct equation in required form
Always check: does A satisfy the equation? \(1 + 7 - 8 = 0\). Does C? \(5 + 3 - 8 = 0\). Both work. This 5-second check catches arithmetic errors and is worth the time in an exam.

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals:

  • Two lines given — asked whether parallel, perpendicular, or neither
  • The word "perpendicular" — use \(m_1 \times m_2 = -1\)
  • Points on the x-axis or y-axis — simplifies midpoint and area calculations
  • Finding area of a triangle from three vertices — use base and height or shoelace formula
  • Given a gradient and asked to "show that" a line has a certain equation — requires full working
  • Combined with calculus: gradient of tangent to a curve = derivative evaluated at that point
