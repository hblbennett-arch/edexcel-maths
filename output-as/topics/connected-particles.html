Connected Particles & Pulleys

Newton's second law applied to two-particle systems connected by inextensible strings over pulleys

5/7
Sittings
9–13
Marks
Q2
Typical Position
High
Difficulty
Why this is a big-mark question: Connected particle problems require you to draw TWO separate force diagrams, write TWO F=ma equations, and solve simultaneously. They then often add a "what happens after?" follow-up. This multi-step structure makes them worth 9-13 marks — the highest-value question on the paper.

Key Concepts

The method (ALWAYS the same)

  1. Draw force diagrams for each particle separately
  2. Label: weight (\(mg\) down), tension (\(T\) along string), normal reaction (if on surface)
  3. Write F = ma for each particle in the direction of motion
  4. Key principle: string inextensible → same acceleration magnitude for both
  5. Solve simultaneously — usually add equations to eliminate \(T\) first

Standard setups at AS Level

  • Atwood machine: Two masses hanging over a pulley — heavier one descends
  • Table and pulley: One mass on smooth table, connected over edge pulley to hanging mass
  • After one hits ground/pulley: String goes slack, remaining particle moves freely under gravity or continues with initial velocity
The instruction "show that the acceleration is..." means you must derive it from first principles. The most common approach: write F=ma for each particle, then either add the equations (eliminates T) or substitute. Always state which direction is positive.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Table and pulley — show acceleration 5 marks

Based on: June 2019 Q2 | View Paper

Question: Ball P (0.8 kg) on a smooth horizontal table is attached via a string over a pulley to ball Q (0.6 kg) hanging freely. The system is released from rest. Show that the acceleration of Q is 4.2 m s\(^{-2}\).

Step 1: Force diagram for Q (hanging, moving down) M1

Forces on Q: Weight \(0.6g\) downwards, Tension \(T\) upwards.

Taking down as positive (direction of motion of Q):

\(0.6g - T = 0.6a\) ... (1)

Step 2: Force diagram for P (on table, moving horizontally) M1

Forces on P horizontally: Tension \(T\) towards pulley (only horizontal force — table is smooth).

Taking towards pulley as positive:

\(T = 0.8a\) ... (2)

(Weight and normal reaction cancel vertically.)

Step 3: Add equations (1) and (2) M1 A1

\(0.6g - T + T = 0.6a + 0.8a\)

\(0.6g = 1.4a\)

\(0.6(9.8) = 1.4a\)

\(5.88 = 1.4a\)

Step 4: Solve for \(a\) A1

\(a = \dfrac{5.88}{1.4} = 4.2\) m s\(^{-2}\) as required.

Example 2: Atwood machine — find force on pulley 8 marks

Based on: June 2020 Q2 | View Paper

Question: Ball P (mass \(4m\)) and ball Q (mass \(3m\)) connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley. P released from rest. Find, in terms of \(m\) and \(g\), the force exerted on the pulley by the string while P is falling.

Step 1: F = ma for P (heavier, descending) M1 A1

Taking down as positive for P:

\(4mg - T = 4ma\) ... (1)

Step 2: F = ma for Q (lighter, ascending) M1 A1

Taking up as positive for Q:

\(T - 3mg = 3ma\) ... (2)

Step 3: Find acceleration (add equations) M1

Adding (1) and (2): \(4mg - 3mg = 7ma\)

\(mg = 7ma\)

\(a = \dfrac{g}{7}\)

Step 4: Find tension A1

Substituting into (2): \(T - 3mg = 3m \cdot \dfrac{g}{7}\)

\(T = 3mg + \dfrac{3mg}{7} = \dfrac{21mg + 3mg}{7} = \dfrac{24mg}{7}\)

Step 5: Force on pulley M1 A1

The pulley is smooth, so tension is the same on both sides of the string.

Force on pulley = \(2T\) (both sides of string pull down on pulley)

\(= 2 \times \dfrac{24mg}{7} = \dfrac{48mg}{7}\)

Force on pulley = 2T (for a smooth pulley with string passing over it). This is because both sides of the string exert tension T on the pulley. A common error is to write the force as T or to use weights instead of tension.

Example 3: After Q hits the floor 6 marks

Based on: June 2019 Q2(b) | View Paper

Question: With \(a = 4.2\) m s\(^{-2}\), P is 1.5 m from the pulley and Q is 0.4 m above the floor. Q hits the floor and does not rebound. Find the time from release until P hits the pulley.

Step 1: Stage 1 — while Q is falling (0.4 m) M1 A1

Q falls 0.4 m with \(a = 4.2\), \(u = 0\).

Find speed when Q hits floor: \(v^2 = 0 + 2(4.2)(0.4) = 3.36\)

\(v = \sqrt{3.36}\) m s\(^{-1}\)

Find time: \(v = u + at\) → \(\sqrt{3.36} = 4.2t_1\) → \(t_1 = \dfrac{\sqrt{3.36}}{4.2}\)

P has also moved 0.4 m towards pulley in this time.

Step 2: Stage 2 — P continues on smooth table M1 A1

After Q hits floor: string goes slack, P moves at constant velocity \(\sqrt{3.36}\) m s\(^{-1}\) (smooth table, no friction).

Remaining distance for P = \(1.5 - 0.4 = 1.1\) m

\(t_2 = \dfrac{1.1}{\sqrt{3.36}}\)

Step 3: Total time A1 A1

\(T = t_1 + t_2 = \dfrac{\sqrt{3.36}}{4.2} + \dfrac{1.1}{\sqrt{3.36}}\)

\(= \dfrac{1.833...}{4.2} + \dfrac{1.1}{1.833...}\)

\(= 0.4364... + 0.5999...\)

\(= 1.04\) s (3 s.f.) or \(= 1.0\) s (2 s.f.)

The "what happens after Q hits the ground" part trips up many students. Key fact: once the string goes slack, the only forces on P are weight and normal reaction (which cancel). On a smooth table, P continues at constant speed. If P were hanging, it would continue upward, decelerate under gravity, and eventually fall back.

Example 4: Model limitation 1 mark

Common across sittings

Question: State one limitation of the model that will affect the accuracy of the answer to the acceleration.

Acceptable answers B1

Any ONE of:

  • The table may not be smooth (friction not accounted for)
  • The string has mass (not truly "light")
  • The pulley may not be smooth (friction at pulley)
  • The string may be slightly elastic (not truly inextensible)
  • Air resistance on the falling particle is ignored
  • The particles are not truly point masses (they have size)

Note: Must relate to the model assumptions, not just say "air resistance" without context.

Common Pitfalls

  • Wrong sign convention: If both equations use "positive = direction of motion for that particle", you ADD them. If both use the same global positive direction, you SUBTRACT.
  • Forgetting tension acts on BOTH particles: T appears in both equations.
  • Force on pulley ≠ weight of particles: It equals 2T, not \((m_1 + m_2)g\).
  • After one hits ground: The other particle does NOT immediately stop — it continues with the speed it had at that instant.
  • Table particle has no weight component in horizontal equation: Only T drives it horizontally.
