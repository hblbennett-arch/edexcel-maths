Guided Approach

The key integration techniques for AS Level (Year 1 only):

1
Rewrite in index form before integrating
Rewrite terms like \(\sqrt{x}\), \(\frac{1}{x^2}\), \(\frac{3}{2\sqrt{x}}\) using fractional/negative powers. For example: \(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\), \(\frac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\), \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{-1/2}\). This allows you to apply the power rule directly.
2
Apply the power rule: \(\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + c\)
Increase the power by 1 and divide by the new power. Always add \(+ c\) for indefinite integrals. For definite integrals, evaluate using limits instead (no constant needed).
3
For areas: set up the correct integral
Area under a curve: \(\int_a^b y\, dx\). Area between a curve and a line: \(\int_a^b (\text{top} - \text{bottom})\, dx\). If the curve is below the x-axis, the integral is negative — take the absolute value or negate.
4
Finding a curve from its gradient function
If given \(f'(x)\) (or \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)), integrate to find \(f(x)\). Use a known point on the curve to find the constant of integration \(c\).

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting +c: In indefinite integrals, omitting the constant of integration loses a mark every time. This is the single most common error at AS Level.
  • Not simplifying before integrating: You cannot integrate \(\frac{3x^4 - 4}{2x^3}\) directly. You must split the fraction first: \(\frac{3x}{2} - 2x^{-3}\). Similarly, expand brackets before integrating.
  • Sign errors with negative areas: When a curve is below the x-axis, \(\int_a^b y\, dx\) gives a negative value. The area is positive, so you must negate or take the modulus. Many candidates lose marks by not recognising this.
  • Wrong limits for area between curves: Always find the intersection points first, then integrate \((\text{upper function} - \text{lower function})\) between those limits.
  • Confusing differentiation and integration: Integration increases the power by 1 (and divides). Differentiation decreases the power by 1 (and multiplies). Do not mix these up under exam pressure.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Always show the rewriting step: write \(\frac{4}{x^3} = 4x^{-3}\) explicitly before integrating.
  • For definite integrals, use square brackets with limits: \(\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_a^b\) then show substitution of each limit separately.
  • When finding area between curves, clearly state which is "top" and which is "bottom" in the region.
  • Check your answer by differentiating it — you should get back the original integrand.

AS Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 1 (4 marks)

Find

$$\int \left(8x^{-3} - \frac{3}{2\sqrt{x}} + 5\right) dx$$

giving your answer in simplest form.

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Step 1 Rewrite all terms in index notation ▶
Before integrating, every term must be in the form \(ax^n\). The middle term \(\frac{3}{2\sqrt{x}}\) contains a square root in the denominator — we rewrite \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{-1/2}\). The first term is already in index form.

Rewrite the integrand:

\(8x^{-3}\) is already in index form.

\(\frac{3}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{3}{2} \cdot x^{-1/2}\)

\(5 = 5x^0\) (constant term).

$$\int \left(8x^{-3} - \frac{3}{2}x^{-1/2} + 5\right) dx$$

Step 2 Apply the power rule to each term ▶
The integration rule is \(\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\). Apply this to each term separately, being very careful with the new power and the coefficient.

First term: \(\int 8x^{-3}\, dx = \frac{8x^{-2}}{-2} = -4x^{-2}\)

Second term: \(\int -\frac{3}{2}x^{-1/2}\, dx = -\frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{x^{1/2}}{1/2} = -\frac{3}{2} \cdot 2x^{1/2} = -3x^{1/2}\)

Third term: \(\int 5\, dx = 5x\)

M1 — Integrating to get a term of the form \(ax^{-2}\)
A1 — At least two terms correct
Step 3 Write the final answer in simplest form with +c ▶
The question says "simplest form" — so we should convert back from index notation to fraction/surd form where appropriate. And we must include the constant of integration \(+c\).

\(-4x^{-2} = -\frac{4}{x^2}\)

\(-3x^{1/2} = -3\sqrt{x}\)

$$\boxed{-\frac{4}{x^2} - 3\sqrt{x} + 5x + c}$$

A1 — All three terms correct in simplest form
B1 — +c included
The most common error was with the middle term. Many candidates forgot to divide by the new power (1/2), effectively getting \(-\frac{3}{2}x^{1/2}\) instead of \(-3x^{1/2}\). Always check: differentiating your answer should give back the integrand.

AS Paper 1 — June 2020 — Question 14 (9 marks)

A curve has equation \(y = g(x)\). Given that:

  • \(g(x)\) is a cubic expression in which the coefficient of \(x^3\) is equal to the coefficient of \(x\)
  • the curve passes through the origin
  • the curve has a stationary point at \((2, 9)\)

(a) Find \(g(x)\). [7 marks]

(b) Prove that the stationary point at \((2, 9)\) is a maximum. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Set up the general form of \(g'(x)\) ▶
If \(g(x)\) is cubic, then \(g'(x)\) is quadratic. A stationary point means \(g'(2) = 0\). We also know the coefficient of \(x^3\) in \(g(x)\) equals the coefficient of \(x\). Let us call this common coefficient \(a\). Then \(g(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + ax + d\) (where the \(x^3\) and \(x\) coefficients are both \(a\)).

Since \(g(x)\) is cubic with coefficient of \(x^3\) = coefficient of \(x\), write:

$$g(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + ax + d$$

Differentiating:

$$g'(x) = 3ax^2 + 2bx + a$$

M1 — Setting up cubic with the constraint on coefficients
Part (a) Step 2: Use the condition that the curve passes through the origin ▶
The curve passes through \((0, 0)\), meaning \(g(0) = 0\). Substituting \(x = 0\) into our expression for \(g(x)\) immediately gives us the value of \(d\).

Substitute \(x = 0\), \(g(0) = 0\):

$$a(0)^3 + b(0)^2 + a(0) + d = 0$$

$$d = 0$$

So \(g(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + ax\).

B1 — \(d = 0\) (constant of integration found)
Part (a) Step 3: Use the stationary point condition \(g'(2) = 0\) ▶
At a stationary point, the gradient is zero. So \(g'(2) = 0\). This gives us one equation relating \(a\) and \(b\).

Substitute \(x = 2\) into \(g'(x) = 3ax^2 + 2bx + a\):

$$g'(2) = 3a(4) + 2b(2) + a = 0$$

$$12a + 4b + a = 0$$

$$13a + 4b = 0 \quad \text{...(1)}$$

M1 — Using \(g'(2) = 0\)
Part (a) Step 4: Use the point \((2, 9)\) on the curve: \(g(2) = 9\) ▶
The curve passes through \((2, 9)\), so \(g(2) = 9\). This gives us a second equation in \(a\) and \(b\), which together with equation (1) lets us solve the simultaneous equations.

Substitute \(x = 2\) into \(g(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + ax\):

$$g(2) = 8a + 4b + 2a = 9$$

$$10a + 4b = 9 \quad \text{...(2)}$$

M1 — Using \(g(2) = 9\) to form second equation
Part (a) Step 5: Solve simultaneously for \(a\) and \(b\) ▶
We have two equations in two unknowns. Subtract (2) from (1) to eliminate \(b\), giving us \(a\) directly. Then substitute back to find \(b\).

From (1): \(13a + 4b = 0\)

From (2): \(10a + 4b = 9\)

Subtract (2) from (1):

$$3a = -9$$

$$a = -3$$

Substitute into (1): \(13(-3) + 4b = 0\)

$$-39 + 4b = 0$$

$$b = \frac{39}{4}$$

A1 — \(a = -3\)
A1 — \(b = \frac{39}{4}\)
Part (a) Step 6: Write the final answer ▶
Now substitute the values of \(a\), \(b\), and \(d\) back into \(g(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + ax\).

$$\boxed{g(x) = -3x^3 + \frac{39}{4}x^2 - 3x}$$

A1 — Correct final expression for \(g(x)\)
This is a challenging question that combines integration concepts (finding a function from its derivative) with simultaneous equations. The key insight is recognising that "coefficient of \(x^3\) equals coefficient of \(x\)" reduces the number of unknowns.
Part (b) Step 7: Prove the stationary point is a maximum ▶
To prove a stationary point is a maximum, we use the second derivative test. If \(g''(2) < 0\), the curve is concave (bending downward) at that point, confirming a maximum.

Find \(g''(x)\):

$$g'(x) = -9x^2 + \frac{39}{2}x - 3$$

$$g''(x) = -18x + \frac{39}{2}$$

Substitute \(x = 2\):

$$g''(2) = -18(2) + \frac{39}{2} = -36 + 19.5 = -16.5$$

Since \(g''(2) = -16.5 < 0\), the stationary point at \((2, 9)\) is a maximum.

M1 — Finding \(g''(x)\) and substituting \(x = 2\)
A1 — Correct value, correct sign, correct conclusion "maximum"

AS Paper 1 — June 2020 — Question 10 (10 marks)

\(g(x) = 2x^3 + x^2 - 41x - 70\)

(a) Use the factor theorem to show that \(g(x)\) is divisible by \((x - 5)\). [2 marks]

(b) Hence, showing all your working, write \(g(x)\) as a product of three linear factors. [4 marks]

The finite region R is bounded by the curve with equation \(y = g(x)\) and the x-axis, and lies below the x-axis.

(c) Find, using algebraic integration, the exact value of the area of R. [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Use the factor theorem — show \(g(5) = 0\) ▶
The factor theorem states: if \(g(a) = 0\), then \((x - a)\) is a factor of \(g(x)\). To show \(g(x)\) is divisible by \((x - 5)\), we simply need to show \(g(5) = 0\).

$$g(5) = 2(5)^3 + (5)^2 - 41(5) - 70$$

$$= 2(125) + 25 - 205 - 70$$

$$= 250 + 25 - 205 - 70$$

$$= 0 \checkmark$$

Since \(g(5) = 0\), by the factor theorem \((x - 5)\) is a factor of \(g(x)\).

M1 — Substituting \(x = 5\) into \(g(x)\)
A1 — Correct evaluation showing = 0, with conclusion stated
Part (b) Step 2: Divide \(g(x)\) by \((x-5)\) to find the quadratic factor ▶
We know \(g(x) = (x-5) \times (\text{quadratic})\). To find the quadratic, we can use polynomial division or compare coefficients. The quadratic will then factorise into two linear factors.

Divide \(2x^3 + x^2 - 41x - 70\) by \((x - 5)\):

Using algebraic long division or inspection:

$$g(x) = (x - 5)(2x^2 + 11x + 14)$$

Now factorise the quadratic \(2x^2 + 11x + 14\):

Find two numbers that multiply to give \(2 \times 14 = 28\) and add to give \(11\): these are \(4\) and \(7\).

$$2x^2 + 11x + 14 = (2x + 7)(x + 2)$$

$$\boxed{g(x) = (x-5)(2x+7)(x+2)}$$

M1 — Attempting algebraic division by \((x-5)\)
A1 — Correct quadratic \(2x^2 + 11x + 14\)
M1 — Factorising the quadratic
A1 — Three correct linear factors
Part (c) Step 3: Identify the limits of integration ▶
The region R lies below the x-axis. The roots of \(g(x) = 0\) are \(x = 5\), \(x = -\frac{7}{2}\), and \(x = -2\). We need to identify which interval gives the portion below the x-axis. Since the leading coefficient is positive (\(2x^3\)), the cubic goes from \(-\infty\) to \(+\infty\). The curve is below the x-axis between \(x = -2\) and \(x = 5\) (you can verify by checking \(g(0) = -70 < 0\)).

The roots are \(x = -\frac{7}{2}\), \(x = -2\), and \(x = 5\).

Check \(g(0) = -70 < 0\), so the curve is below the x-axis between \(x = -2\) and \(x = 5\).

Therefore we integrate from \(-2\) to \(5\).

Part (c) Step 4: Integrate \(g(x)\) and evaluate between limits ▶
Since the region is below the x-axis, \(\int_{-2}^{5} g(x)\, dx\) will give a negative value. The area is the absolute value. We integrate term by term, then substitute the limits.

$$\int (2x^3 + x^2 - 41x - 70)\, dx = \frac{2x^4}{4} + \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{41x^2}{2} - 70x$$

$$= \frac{x^4}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{41x^2}{2} - 70x$$

M1 — Integrating to get at least two correct terms

Evaluate at \(x = 5\):

$$\frac{625}{2} + \frac{125}{3} - \frac{1025}{2} - 350 = -200 + \frac{125}{3} - 350 = -550 + \frac{125}{3} = \frac{-1650 + 125}{3} = \frac{-1525}{3}$$

Evaluate at \(x = -2\):

$$\frac{16}{2} + \frac{-8}{3} - \frac{164}{2} - (-140) = 8 - \frac{8}{3} - 82 + 140 = 66 - \frac{8}{3} = \frac{198 - 8}{3} = \frac{190}{3}$$

$$\int_{-2}^{5} g(x)\, dx = \frac{-1525}{3} - \frac{190}{3} = \frac{-1715}{3}$$

M1 — Substituting correct limits and subtracting
Part (c) Step 5: State the area (take the modulus) ▶
The integral gives \(-\frac{1715}{3}\) because the curve is below the x-axis. Area is always positive, so we take the absolute value.

Since R is below the x-axis:

$$\text{Area of } R = \left|\frac{-1715}{3}\right| = \boxed{\frac{1715}{3}}$$

A1 — Correct exact area \(\frac{1715}{3}\) (or \(571\frac{2}{3}\))
Many candidates integrated correctly but forgot to negate the result. Since the question says "the exact value of the area" and area must be positive, you must write the positive value. Candidates who wrote \(-\frac{1715}{3}\) as their final answer lost the A1.

AS Paper 1 — June 2024 — Question 8 (10 marks)

Figure 3 shows a sketch of the curve C with equation \(y = x^3 - 14x + 23\).

The line \(l\) is the tangent to C at the point A, also shown in Figure 3.

Given that \(l\) has equation \(y = -2x + 7\):

(a) Show, using calculus, that the x coordinate of A is 2. [3 marks]

The line \(l\) cuts C again at the point B.

(b) Verify that the x coordinate of B is \(-4\). [2 marks]

The finite region R, shown shaded in Figure 3, is bounded by C and \(l\).

Using algebraic integration,

(c) Show that the area of R is 108. [5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Differentiate the curve and set gradient equal to tangent gradient ▶
The tangent line \(l\) has equation \(y = -2x + 7\), so its gradient is \(-2\). At point A, the gradient of the curve must equal the gradient of the tangent. We differentiate the curve and set \(\frac{dy}{dx} = -2\).

Differentiate \(y = x^3 - 14x + 23\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 14$$

M1 — Differentiating the curve equation

At A, gradient of curve = gradient of tangent:

$$3x^2 - 14 = -2$$

$$3x^2 = 12$$

$$x^2 = 4$$

$$x = \pm 2$$

A1 — Solving to get \(x = \pm 2\)
Part (a) Step 2: Confirm which value gives a point on both the curve and the line ▶
We got \(x = 2\) or \(x = -2\). Point A must lie on both the curve and the line. We check which value satisfies both equations.

Check \(x = 2\) on the curve: \(y = 8 - 28 + 23 = 3\)

Check \(x = 2\) on the line: \(y = -4 + 7 = 3\) — same point, so A is at \((2, 3)\).

Check \(x = -2\) on the curve: \(y = -8 + 28 + 23 = 43\)

Check \(x = -2\) on the line: \(y = 4 + 7 = 11\) — different, so \(x = -2\) is not on the tangent.

Therefore the x coordinate of A is 2.

A1 — Confirming \(x = 2\) with valid reasoning (point lies on both curve and line)
Part (b) Step 3: Verify \(x = -4\) is the other intersection ▶
"Verify" means show that \(x = -4\) satisfies the equation where the curve meets the line. We substitute into both and check they give the same y value.

At \(x = -4\):

On the curve: \(y = (-4)^3 - 14(-4) + 23 = -64 + 56 + 23 = 15\)

On the line: \(y = -2(-4) + 7 = 8 + 7 = 15\)

Since both give \(y = 15\), the point B has coordinates \((-4, 15)\) and lies on both C and \(l\).

M1 — Substituting \(x = -4\) into at least one equation
A1 — Both values shown equal with conclusion stated
Part (c) Step 4: Set up the integral for area between curve and line ▶
The area between two curves (or a curve and a line) from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is \(\int_a^b (\text{top} - \text{bottom})\, dx\). From the sketch, between \(x = -4\) and \(x = 2\), the line \(l\) is above the curve C. So we integrate (line − curve).

Area of R = \(\int_{-4}^{2} [(\text{line}) - (\text{curve})]\, dx\)

$$= \int_{-4}^{2} [(-2x + 7) - (x^3 - 14x + 23)]\, dx$$

Expand and simplify:

$$= \int_{-4}^{2} (-2x + 7 - x^3 + 14x - 23)\, dx$$

$$= \int_{-4}^{2} (-x^3 + 12x - 16)\, dx$$

M1 — Setting up integral of (line − curve) with correct limits
A1 — Correct simplified integrand \(-x^3 + 12x - 16\)
Part (c) Step 5: Integrate term by term ▶
Apply the power rule to each term: increase the power by 1 and divide by the new power. Then evaluate at the upper and lower limits.

$$\int (-x^3 + 12x - 16)\, dx = -\frac{x^4}{4} + 6x^2 - 16x$$

M1 — Integrating to get at least two correct terms (with \(x^n \to \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\))

Evaluate at \(x = 2\):

$$-\frac{16}{4} + 6(4) - 16(2) = -4 + 24 - 32 = -12$$

Evaluate at \(x = -4\):

$$-\frac{256}{4} + 6(16) - 16(-4) = -64 + 96 + 64 = 96$$

Part (c) Step 6: Subtract limits and conclude ▶
The definite integral equals [value at upper limit] minus [value at lower limit]. Since we set up "top minus bottom", the result should be positive (it represents an area).

$$\left[-\frac{x^4}{4} + 6x^2 - 16x\right]_{-4}^{2} = (-12) - (96) = -108$$

Wait — this is negative! That means between \(x=-4\) and \(x=2\), the curve is actually above the line (not below). So we take the absolute value:

$$\text{Area of } R = |{-108}| = \boxed{108} \quad \square$$

A1 — Correct substitution of limits
A1 — Area = 108 (as required)
An alternative (and neater) approach: since the curve is above the line between \(x=-4\) and \(x=2\), you could integrate (curve − line) instead, which gives +108 directly. Either approach is acceptable. The key point: always check whether your "top minus bottom" order is correct, or simply take the modulus at the end.

How to Recognise Integration Questions

Look for these signals in the question:

  • The integral sign \(\int\) — immediate giveaway for basic integration (Q1 position, 3–4 marks)
  • "Find the area of the region R" or "using algebraic integration" — definite integral required
  • "The curve passes through the point..." combined with a derivative — integrate and find \(c\)
  • "Find f(x)" given "f'(x) = ..." — indefinite integration with boundary condition
  • A shaded region on a diagram between a curve and a line or the x-axis
  • The word "exact" — leave your answer as a fraction or surd, do not use decimals

Key Formulae Reference

  • Power rule: \(\displaystyle\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + c \quad (n \neq -1)\)
  • Constant multiple: \(\displaystyle\int af(x)\, dx = a\int f(x)\, dx\)
  • Sum rule: \(\displaystyle\int [f(x) + g(x)]\, dx = \int f(x)\, dx + \int g(x)\, dx\)
  • Definite integral: \(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a)\)
  • Area below x-axis: \(\displaystyle\text{Area} = -\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) when \(f(x) < 0\) on \([a,b]\)
  • Area between curves: \(\displaystyle\text{Area} = \int_a^b [\text{top}(x) - \text{bottom}(x)]\, dx\)
