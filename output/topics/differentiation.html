The Approach

Differentiation questions come in many forms. Here is the general thought process:

1
Identify the type of differentiation required
Is it power rule, product rule, quotient rule, chain rule, implicit, parametric, or from first principles? The question wording and the structure of the function tell you which rule to apply.
2
Rewrite into differentiable form if needed
Convert roots to fractional powers (\(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\)), fractions to negative powers (\(\frac{1}{x} = x^{-1}\)), and expand brackets if that simplifies the expression. This preparation step prevents errors.
3
Apply the differentiation rule carefully
For power rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\). For products: \(\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\). For chain rule: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\). Show each term clearly.
4
Use the derivative to answer the question
Stationary points: set \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\) and solve. Tangent: evaluate gradient at the point, use \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\). Normal: gradient is \(-\frac{1}{m}\). Nature: check sign of \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\).

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting to rewrite before differentiating: You cannot differentiate \(\frac{3}{x^2}\) directly with the power rule. Rewrite as \(3x^{-2}\) first. Many candidates lose easy marks here.
  • Sign errors with negative powers: Differentiating \(5x^{-1}\) gives \(-5x^{-2}\), not \(5x^{-2}\). The negative from the power multiplies the coefficient.
  • Misapplying the product rule: A common error is writing \(\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = \frac{du}{dx} \cdot \frac{dv}{dx}\). The correct rule has two terms: \(u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\).
  • Missing the product rule on implicit terms: When differentiating \(2xy\) implicitly, candidates often forget it requires the product rule: \(2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y\).
  • First principles: skipping the limit: Writing \(h = 0\) instead of \(\lim_{h \to 0}\) loses the final mark. You must show a limiting argument, not just substitute zero.
  • Not concluding after the second derivative test: You must state the value, its sign, AND write "therefore minimum/maximum". All three are required.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Always rewrite expressions using index notation before differentiating: \(\sqrt{x} \to x^{1/2}\), \(\frac{1}{x^3} \to x^{-3}\)
  • For "show that" derivative questions, show every step of the product/chain rule separately before combining
  • State \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\) explicitly before solving for stationary points — this earns a method mark even if algebra fails
  • For tangent/normal questions, clearly state the gradient value before writing the line equation
  • In first principles questions, clearly write the limit notation \(\lim_{h \to 0}\) and cancel \(h\) before applying the limit

Paper 2 — June 2022 — Question 4 (3 marks)

Given that \(y = 2x^2\),

use differentiation from first principles to show that \(\displaystyle\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x\)

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part Step 1: Write the definition of the derivative from first principles ▶
Differentiation from first principles means using the formal limit definition of the derivative. We compute the gradient of a chord between \(x\) and \(x+h\), then let the chord shrink by taking \(h \to 0\). This is the definition — we cannot use the power rule here.

The derivative from first principles is defined as:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

With \(f(x) = 2x^2\), we need to find \(f(x+h)\):

$$f(x+h) = 2(x+h)^2$$

Part Step 2: Expand and form the gradient of the chord ▶
We need to compute the fraction \(\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\) and simplify it. The key step is expanding \((x+h)^2\) correctly as \(x^2 + 2xh + h^2\) (not \(x^2 + h^2\) — a common error). After expansion, the \(x^2\) terms will cancel, leaving terms with \(h\) that we can factor.

Expand \((x+h)^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2\):

$$f(x+h) = 2(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) = 2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2$$

Subtract \(f(x) = 2x^2\):

$$f(x+h) - f(x) = 2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2 - 2x^2 = 4xh + 2h^2$$

Divide by \(h\):

$$\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} = \frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h}$$

M1 — Writing the gradient of the chord and expanding the correct bracket \(2(x+h)^2\)
Part Step 3: Simplify by cancelling \(h\) ▶
Every term in the numerator has a factor of \(h\), so we can cancel it with the \(h\) in the denominator. This is essential — we cannot take the limit while \(h\) is still in the denominator (that would give \(\frac{0}{0}\)). After cancelling, we get an expression where setting \(h \to 0\) gives a finite answer.

Factor \(h\) from the numerator:

$$\frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h} = \frac{h(4x + 2h)}{h} = 4x + 2h$$

A1 — Correct simplified fraction with the \(x^2\) terms cancelled: \(\frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h}\) or equivalent \(4x + 2h\)
Part Step 4: Take the limit as \(h \to 0\) ▶
Now we apply the limit. As \(h \to 0\), the term \(2h\) vanishes, leaving only \(4x\). We must write the limit notation explicitly — simply writing "\(h = 0\)" without the limit symbol loses the final mark.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} (4x + 2h) = 4x$$

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x} \quad \square$$

A1* — Completes the proof by applying a limiting argument and deducing \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x\)
You must reach the form \(4x + 2h\) at the end and then apply the limit. Writing "\(h = 0\)" is acceptable only if the limit notation \(h \to 0\) appears somewhere in the working. Simply substituting \(h = 0\) into the unsimplified fraction (without cancelling the \(h\)) is not acceptable and scores zero for this mark.

Paper 1 — October 2020 — Question 9 (6 marks for parts a & b)

Figure 2 shows a sketch of the curve \(C\) with equation \(y = f(x)\) where

$$f(x) = 4(x^2 - 2)e^{-2x}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}$$

(a) Show that \(f'(x) = 8(2 + x - x^2)e^{-2x}\) [3 marks]

(b) Hence find, in simplest form, the exact coordinates of the stationary points of \(C\). [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Identify this as a product rule question ▶
\(f(x) = 4(x^2-2)e^{-2x}\) is a product of two functions of \(x\): one polynomial part \(4(x^2-2)\) and one exponential part \(e^{-2x}\). We need the product rule. Let us define \(u\) and \(v\) and find their derivatives separately.

Let \(u = 4(x^2 - 2) = 4x^2 - 8\) and \(v = e^{-2x}\)

Differentiate \(u\):

$$\frac{du}{dx} = 8x$$

Differentiate \(v\) (using chain rule on \(e^{-2x}\)):

$$\frac{dv}{dx} = -2e^{-2x}$$

M1 — Applying the product rule with \(e^{-2x} \to ke^{-2x}\) where \(k \neq 0\)
Part (a) Step 2: Apply the product rule and simplify ▶
The product rule gives \(f'(x) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\). After applying it, we will have two terms both containing \(e^{-2x}\). We factor out \(e^{-2x}\) and simplify what remains inside the bracket to match the target form \(8(2 + x - x^2)e^{-2x}\).

Apply \(f'(x) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\):

$$f'(x) = 4(x^2-2) \cdot (-2e^{-2x}) + e^{-2x} \cdot 8x$$

Expand the first term:

$$f'(x) = -8(x^2-2)e^{-2x} + 8xe^{-2x}$$

Factor out \(e^{-2x}\):

$$f'(x) = e^{-2x}\left[-8(x^2-2) + 8x\right]$$

Expand inside the bracket: \(-8x^2 + 16 + 8x\):

$$f'(x) = e^{-2x}(-8x^2 + 8x + 16)$$

Factor out 8 (rearranging the bracket):

$$f'(x) = 8e^{-2x}(-x^2 + x + 2) = 8(2 + x - x^2)e^{-2x}$$

$$\boxed{f'(x) = 8(2 + x - x^2)e^{-2x}} \quad \square$$

A1 — Correct unsimplified derivative (e.g. \(-8(x^2-2)e^{-2x} + 8xe^{-2x}\))
A1* — Reaching the given answer with clear working showing all steps
Since this is a "show that", you must show every step. Moving from the unsimplified product rule result to the factored form in one step is acceptable, but jumping from \(u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\) directly to the answer without expanding loses marks.
Part (b) Step 3: Set \(f'(x) = 0\) and solve ▶
Stationary points occur where \(f'(x) = 0\). Since \(f'(x) = 8(2+x-x^2)e^{-2x}\) and \(e^{-2x} > 0\) for all \(x\) (exponentials are never zero), we only need the quadratic factor to equal zero: \(2 + x - x^2 = 0\).

At stationary points, \(f'(x) = 0\):

$$8(2 + x - x^2)e^{-2x} = 0$$

Since \(e^{-2x} \neq 0\) for any real \(x\), and \(8 \neq 0\), we need:

$$2 + x - x^2 = 0$$

Multiply by \(-1\) and rearrange:

$$x^2 - x - 2 = 0$$

Factorise:

$$(x-2)(x+1) = 0$$

$$x = 2 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -1$$

B1 — States or implies \(x = -1\) and \(x = 2\) as the roots of \(f'(x) = 0\)
Part (b) Step 4: Find the \(y\)-coordinates ▶
We have the \(x\)-values of the stationary points. Now substitute each back into the original function \(f(x) = 4(x^2-2)e^{-2x}\) to find the corresponding \(y\)-coordinates. The question asks for exact form, so leave answers in terms of \(e\).

Substitute into \(f(x) = 4(x^2-2)e^{-2x}\):

When \(x = -1\):

$$f(-1) = 4((-1)^2 - 2)e^{-2(-1)} = 4(1-2)e^{2} = -4e^{2}$$

When \(x = 2\):

$$f(2) = 4((2)^2 - 2)e^{-2(2)} = 4(4-2)e^{-4} = 8e^{-4}$$

Stationary points: \((-1,\; -4e^2)\) and \((2,\; 8e^{-4})\)

M1 — Substituting one \(x\)-value into \(f(x)\) to find a \(y\)-value
A1 — Both coordinates correct in exact form: \((-1, -4e^2)\) and \((2, 8e^{-4})\)
The question asks for "exact coordinates in simplest form" — so answers like \((-1, -4e^2)\) are required, not decimals. Extra solutions (e.g. claiming \(e^{-2x} = 0\) gives a solution) would be penalised on the A1 mark.

Paper 2 — October 2021 — Question 5 (7 marks)

The curve \(C\) has equation

$$y = 5x^4 - 24x^3 + 42x^2 - 32x + 11, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}$$

(a) Find (i) \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)    (ii) \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) [3 marks]

(b)(i) Verify that \(C\) has a stationary point at \(x = 1\) [2 marks]

(b)(ii) Show that this stationary point is a point of inflection, giving reasons for your answer. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a)(i) Step 1: Differentiate to find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) ▶
This is straightforward power rule differentiation — bring down the power, reduce it by one for each term. No product or chain rule needed here since every term is of the form \(ax^n\).

Differentiate \(y = 5x^4 - 24x^3 + 42x^2 - 32x + 11\) term by term:

\(\frac{d}{dx}(5x^4) = 20x^3\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(-24x^3) = -72x^2\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(42x^2) = 84x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(-32x) = -32\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(11) = 0\)

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 20x^3 - 72x^2 + 84x - 32$$

M1 — Differentiating: \(x^n \to nx^{n-1}\) for at least one term
A1 — Fully correct: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 20x^3 - 72x^2 + 84x - 32\)
Part (a)(ii) Step 2: Differentiate again to find \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) ▶
The second derivative \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) is the derivative of the first derivative. We will need this in Part (b)(ii) to investigate the nature of the stationary point. Apply the power rule again to each term of \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

Differentiate \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 20x^3 - 72x^2 + 84x - 32\):

\(\frac{d}{dx}(20x^3) = 60x^2\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(-72x^2) = -144x\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(84x) = 84\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(-32) = 0\)

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 60x^2 - 144x + 84$$

A1ft — Correct second derivative (follow-through from their \(\frac{dy}{dx}\))
Part (b)(i) Step 3: Verify stationary point at \(x = 1\) ▶
"Verify" means we are told the answer and must confirm it is correct. A stationary point occurs where \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\). So we substitute \(x = 1\) into our derivative and check that the result is zero. We must also write a conclusion statement.

Substitute \(x = 1\) into \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 20x^3 - 72x^2 + 84x - 32\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{x=1} = 20(1)^3 - 72(1)^2 + 84(1) - 32$$

$$= 20 - 72 + 84 - 32 = 0 \checkmark$$

Since \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\) when \(x = 1\), there is a stationary point at \(x = 1\).

M1 — Substituting \(x = 1\) into \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) and evaluating
A1 — Showing the result equals 0 AND stating a conclusion (e.g. "therefore stationary point")
Part (b)(ii) Step 4: Show it is a point of inflection ▶
A stationary point of inflection occurs when \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0\) at the point AND the second derivative changes sign either side of it (or equivalently, \(\frac{d^3y}{dx^3} \neq 0\)). Simply having \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0\) is NOT enough on its own — we must show a sign change. One approach: evaluate the second derivative either side of \(x = 1\).

First, check \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) at \(x = 1\):

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\bigg|_{x=1} = 60(1)^2 - 144(1) + 84 = 60 - 144 + 84 = 0$$

The second derivative test is inconclusive (gives 0). We check for a sign change in \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) either side of \(x = 1\):

Try \(x = 0.8\):

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\bigg|_{x=0.8} = 60(0.64) - 144(0.8) + 84 = 38.4 - 115.2 + 84 = 7.2 > 0$$

Try \(x = 1.2\):

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\bigg|_{x=1.2} = 60(1.44) - 144(1.2) + 84 = 86.4 - 172.8 + 84 = -2.4 < 0$$

Since \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) changes sign (from positive to negative) either side of \(x = 1\), this confirms the stationary point is a point of inflection.

M1 — Evaluating \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) at values either side of \(x = 1\) (or finding the third derivative)
A1 — Correct values showing a sign change, with a reasoned conclusion stating "point of inflection"
Three valid methods exist: (1) Show the second derivative changes sign either side of \(x=1\). (2) Show \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0\) at \(x=1\) and \(\frac{d^3y}{dx^3} \neq 0\) at \(x=1\) (here \(\frac{d^3y}{dx^3} = 120x - 144\), giving \(120(1)-144 = -24 \neq 0\)). (3) Show the first derivative has the same sign (both negative) either side of \(x=1\). All three are acceptable. You must give reasons — just stating "point of inflection" with no evidence scores M0 A0.

Paper 2 — June 2023 — Question 6 (7 marks)

A curve has equation

$$x^3 + 2xy + 3y^2 = 47$$

(a) Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) in terms of \(x\) and \(y\). [4 marks]

The point \(P(-2, 5)\) lies on the curve.

(b) Find the equation of the normal to the curve at \(P\), giving your answer in the form \(ax + by + c = 0\), where \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are integers to be found. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Differentiate each term implicitly ▶
The equation \(x^3 + 2xy + 3y^2 = 47\) has \(y\) mixed with \(x\) — we cannot rearrange to get \(y = f(x)\) easily. So we differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\). Every time we differentiate a term containing \(y\), we multiply by \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) (chain rule). The \(2xy\) term needs the product rule.

Differentiate \(x^3 + 2xy + 3y^2 = 47\) with respect to \(x\):

\(\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 3x^2\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}(2xy)\) — this is a product of \(2x\) and \(y\), so use the product rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx}(2xy) = 2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y$$

\(\frac{d}{dx}(3y^2) = 6y\frac{dy}{dx}\) (chain rule: bring down the 2, multiply by \(\frac{dy}{dx}\))

\(\frac{d}{dx}(47) = 0\)

Putting it all together:

$$3x^2 + 2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y + 6y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

M1 — Differentiating \(x^3 \to kx^2\) and \(3y^2 \to ky\frac{dy}{dx}\) where \(k\) are constants
B1 — Correct product rule on \(2xy\): giving \(2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y\)
Part (a) Step 2: Rearrange to make \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) the subject ▶
We need \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) on its own. Strategy: collect all terms containing \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) on one side, everything else on the other side, then factor out \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) and divide.

$$3x^2 + 2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 2y + 6y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

Move non-\(\frac{dy}{dx}\) terms to the right:

$$2x\frac{dy}{dx} + 6y\frac{dy}{dx} = -3x^2 - 2y$$

Factor out \(\frac{dy}{dx}\):

$$(2x + 6y)\frac{dy}{dx} = -3x^2 - 2y$$

Divide both sides by \((2x + 6y)\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-(3x^2 + 2y)}{2x + 6y}$$

M1 — Valid attempt to make \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) the subject, with exactly two \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) terms collected
A1 — Correct: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{2y + 3x^2}{2x + 6y}\) or equivalent
The answer can be written in several equivalent ways, such as \(\frac{-(3x^2+2y)}{2x+6y}\) or \(\frac{-3x^2-2y}{2(x+3y)}\). All are acceptable. Be careful with sign placement — it is sometimes unclear from candidates' working whether the negative applies to the numerator or denominator.
Part (b) Step 3: Find the gradient of the tangent at \(P(-2, 5)\) ▶
To find the normal, we first need the gradient of the tangent at the point. We substitute \(x = -2\) and \(y = 5\) into our expression for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\). Then the normal gradient is the negative reciprocal of the tangent gradient.

Substitute \(x = -2\), \(y = 5\) into \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{-(3x^2 + 2y)}{2x + 6y}\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{P} = \frac{-(3(-2)^2 + 2(5))}{2(-2) + 6(5)}$$

Numerator: \(-(3(4) + 10) = -(12 + 10) = -22\)

Denominator: \(-4 + 30 = 26\)

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{P} = \frac{-22}{26} = -\frac{11}{13}$$

Gradient of tangent at \(P\): \(m_T = -\frac{11}{13}\)

Gradient of normal at \(P\): \(m_N = -\frac{1}{m_T} = -\frac{1}{(-11/13)} = \frac{13}{11}\)

M1 — Substituting \(x = -2\) and \(y = 5\) into \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) to find gradient
Part (b) Step 4: Find the equation of the normal ▶
We now have the gradient of the normal (\(\frac{13}{11}\)) and a point on it (\(P(-2, 5)\)). Use the point-gradient form \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) and rearrange into the required form \(ax + by + c = 0\).

$$y - 5 = \frac{13}{11}(x - (-2))$$

$$y - 5 = \frac{13}{11}(x + 2)$$

Multiply both sides by 11 to clear the fraction:

$$11(y - 5) = 13(x + 2)$$

$$11y - 55 = 13x + 26$$

Rearrange to \(ax + by + c = 0\):

$$13x - 11y + 26 + 55 = 0$$

$$13x - 11y + 81 = 0$$

$$\boxed{13x - 11y + 81 = 0}$$

dM1 — Attempting the equation of the normal using the negative reciprocal gradient and the point \((-2, 5)\)
A1 — Correct answer: \(13x - 11y + 81 = 0\) (or any integer multiple, including "= 0")
The "d" in dM1 means this mark depends on the previous M1 being earned. You must use the negative reciprocal of the tangent gradient — using the tangent gradient itself (finding the tangent instead of normal) is a common error that loses both marks here. Also note: the answer must include "= 0" at the end, and must have integer coefficients.

How to Recognise the Differentiation Type

Different question wordings signal different differentiation techniques:

  • "from first principles" — Use the limit definition \(\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). Never use the power rule.
  • Product of two functions (e.g. \(x^2 e^x\), \((3x+1)\sin x\)) — Product rule: \(uv' + vu'\)
  • Function of a function (e.g. \(\sin(3x^2)\), \(e^{2x+1}\), \((4x-1)^5\)) — Chain rule: differentiate outer, multiply by derivative of inner
  • Fraction of two functions (e.g. \(\frac{x^2}{\cos x}\)) — Quotient rule (or rewrite as product)
  • Equation with \(x\) and \(y\) mixed (e.g. \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\)) — Implicit differentiation
  • "\(x = f(t)\), \(y = g(t)\)" — Parametric: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\)
  • "tangent" or "normal" — Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) at the point; tangent uses that gradient, normal uses \(-\frac{1}{m}\)
  • "stationary point" — Set \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\) and solve
  • "nature" or "minimum/maximum" — Use second derivative test

Key Formulae Reference

  • Power rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\) (works for all real \(n\))
  • Product rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\)
  • Quotient rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \frac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}\)
  • Chain rule: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\)
  • Exponential: \(\frac{d}{dx}(e^{kx}) = ke^{kx}\)
  • Trigonometric: \(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x\), \(\quad \frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x\), \(\quad \frac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x\)
  • Logarithmic: \(\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}\)
  • First principles: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\)
