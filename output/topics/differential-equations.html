The Approach

The standard structure for Edexcel differential equation questions:

1
Set up or identify the differential equation
Read the modelling context carefully. If Part (a) asks you to "show that" a DE holds, translate the English ("rate of change is proportional to...") into symbols. If the DE is given, move straight to solving it.
2
Separate the variables
Get all terms involving the dependent variable (e.g. \(y\), \(H\), \(V\)) on one side with its differential, and all terms involving the independent variable (e.g. \(t\), \(x\)) on the other side with its differential. Write integral signs on both sides.
3
Integrate both sides
Perform the integration. This may require partial fractions, substitution, or standard results. Always include \(+ c\) (the constant of integration). If the LHS requires partial fractions, do that decomposition first.
4
Apply the boundary/initial condition to find \(c\)
Substitute the given values (e.g. "when \(t = 0\), \(H = 5\)") into your integrated equation and solve for the constant. Then write the particular solution. If it's a "show that", rearrange to match the target form.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting \(+\,c\): The most common error. Without the constant of integration, the boundary condition step is impossible and you lose multiple marks downstream. Write \(+\,c\) immediately after integrating.
  • Not applying boundary conditions correctly: You must substitute both the \(t\)-value AND the \(y\)-value simultaneously. Many candidates forget one or substitute into the wrong equation.
  • Partial fractions errors: When the DE requires partial fractions (e.g. \(\frac{1}{V(25-V)}\)), careless sign errors in the decomposition propagate through the entire solution. Check your fractions by recombining before proceeding.
  • Incorrect separation: The dV and dt must be in the correct positions. Writing \(\int \frac{1}{V}\,dt = \int f(t)\,dV\) (swapped) is a fatal error that scores M0.
  • Losing \(\ln\) brackets: Writing \(\ln V(25-V)\) when you mean \(\ln\bigl(V(25-V)\bigr)\) or \(\ln V + \ln(25-V)\) causes confusion and can lose marks. Use brackets or write separate ln terms.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write the separated form with integral signs explicitly: \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{H}\,dH = \int \frac{\cos(0.25t)}{40}\,dt\) — this earns the first M1 even if subsequent work has errors.
  • For "show that" questions: work towards the target form systematically. Combine ln terms using log laws, and show clearly how \(e^{\ln(\ldots)} = \ldots\) when converting back.
  • State boundary conditions explicitly: write "When \(t = 0\), \(H = 5\):" before substituting — this makes your method crystal clear.
  • Check your answer makes physical sense: if the model says something is growing, your solution should increase with \(t\). If it should have a limiting value, check the formula gives it as \(t \to \infty\).

Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 10 (8 marks)

The height above ground, \(H\) metres, of a passenger on a roller coaster can be modelled by the differential equation

$$\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{H\cos(0.25t)}{40}$$

where \(t\) is the time, in seconds, from the start of the ride. Given that the passenger is 5 m above the ground at the start of the ride,

(a) show that \(H = 5e^{0.1\sin(0.25t)}\) [5 marks]

(b) State the maximum height of the passenger above the ground. [1 mark]

(c) Find the value of \(T\), where the passenger reaches the maximum height for the second time at \(T\) seconds. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Separate the variables ▶
The DE has \(H\) on the right-hand side mixed with \(t\). To solve it, we need all \(H\) terms on one side and all \(t\) terms on the other. Since \(H\) is multiplied on the RHS, we divide both sides by \(H\), then "multiply across" by \(dt\).

Starting with:

$$\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{H\cos(0.25t)}{40}$$

Separate: divide both sides by \(H\) and multiply both sides by \(dt\):

$$\frac{1}{H}\,dH = \frac{\cos(0.25t)}{40}\,dt$$

Add integral signs to both sides:

$$\int \frac{1}{H}\,dH = \int \frac{\cos(0.25t)}{40}\,dt$$

M1 — Separates the variables correctly with integral signs present and dH, dt in correct positions
Part (a) Step 2: Integrate both sides ▶
The LHS is a standard integral: \(\int \frac{1}{H}\,dH = \ln|H|\). The RHS requires us to integrate \(\cos(0.25t)\) — recall that \(\int \cos(at)\,dt = \frac{1}{a}\sin(at)\). Here \(a = 0.25\), so we divide by \(0.25\) (i.e. multiply by 4).

LHS: \(\int \frac{1}{H}\,dH = \ln H\) (since \(H > 0\), the height is always positive)

RHS: \(\int \frac{\cos(0.25t)}{40}\,dt = \frac{1}{40} \times \frac{\sin(0.25t)}{0.25} = \frac{\sin(0.25t)}{10}\)

$$\ln H = \frac{\sin(0.25t)}{10} + c$$

Or equivalently: \(\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + c\)

M1 — Integrates both sides: \(\ln H\) on the left, expression involving \(\sin(0.25t)\) on the right
A1 — Correct integration: \(\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + c\)
Part (a) Step 3: Apply the boundary condition to find \(c\) ▶
We're told the passenger starts at 5 m, i.e. when \(t = 0\), \(H = 5\). Substituting these values into our integrated equation will give us the value of \(c\). Note that \(\sin(0) = 0\), which simplifies things nicely.

When \(t = 0\), \(H = 5\):

$$\ln 5 = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0) + c$$

Since \(\sin(0) = 0\):

$$\ln 5 = 0 + c$$

$$c = \ln 5$$

So our equation becomes:

$$\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + \ln 5$$

dM1 — Substitutes \(t = 0\), \(H = 5\) into an integrated equation containing a constant of integration
Part (a) Step 4: Rearrange to reach the given answer ▶
The target answer is \(H = 5e^{0.1\sin(0.25t)}\). We currently have \(\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + \ln 5\). To get \(H\) on its own, we exponentiate both sides. Using the log law \(e^{a+b} = e^a \cdot e^b\) and \(e^{\ln 5} = 5\), we can reach the given form.

Starting with: \(\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + \ln 5\)

Exponentiate both sides (take \(e\) to the power of each side):

$$H = e^{\frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + \ln 5}$$

Use the index law \(e^{a+b} = e^a \cdot e^b\):

$$H = e^{\frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t)} \cdot e^{\ln 5}$$

Since \(e^{\ln 5} = 5\):

$$\boxed{H = 5e^{0.1\sin(0.25t)}} \quad \square$$

A1* — Reaches the given answer with clear, complete working and at least one correct intermediate line
Many candidates lost this mark by jumping from \(\ln H = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t) + \ln 5\) straight to the answer. You must show at least one intermediate step when exponentiating. The most common acceptable intermediate line is \(H = e^{\frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t)} \cdot e^{\ln 5}\) or \(\ln H - \ln 5 = \frac{1}{10}\sin(0.25t)\) leading to \(\ln\frac{H}{5} = ...\)
Part (b) Step 5: State the maximum height ▶
\(H = 5e^{0.1\sin(0.25t)}\). The height is maximised when the exponent \(0.1\sin(0.25t)\) is maximised. Since the maximum value of \(\sin(\text{anything})\) is 1, the maximum exponent is \(0.1 \times 1 = 0.1\), giving maximum height \(5e^{0.1}\).

Maximum of \(\sin(0.25t)\) is 1, so maximum height is:

$$H_{\max} = 5e^{0.1 \times 1} = 5e^{0.1} \approx 5.53 \text{ m}$$

B1 — States \(5e^{0.1}\) (or equivalent decimal)
Part (c) Step 6: Find when maximum height is reached for the second time ▶
Maximum height occurs when \(\sin(0.25t) = 1\), i.e. when \(0.25t = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2n\pi\) for integer \(n\). The first time is \(n=0\): \(t = \frac{\pi/2}{0.25} = 2\pi\). The second time is \(n=1\): \(t = \frac{\pi/2 + 2\pi}{0.25}\).

We need \(\sin(0.25t) = 1\):

$$0.25t = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2n\pi$$

For the second time, \(n = 1\):

$$0.25t = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi = \frac{5\pi}{2}$$

$$t = \frac{5\pi/2}{0.25} = \frac{5\pi}{0.5} = 10\pi$$

$$\boxed{T = 10\pi \approx 31.4 \text{ seconds}}$$

M1 — Sets \(0.25t = \frac{5\pi}{2}\) (or equivalent method for second occurrence)
A1 — \(T = 10\pi\) or awrt 31.4

Paper 2 — June 2022 — Question 11 (10 marks)

When chemical A and chemical B are mixed, oxygen is produced. The total volume of oxygen produced, \(V\) m\(^3\), \(t\) hours after the chemicals were mixed, is modelled by the differential equation

$$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{3V}{(2t-1)(t+1)}, \quad V > 0, \quad t > k$$

where \(k\) is a constant.

(a) Express \(\dfrac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)}\) in partial fractions. [3 marks]

Given that exactly 2 hours after the chemicals were mixed, a total volume of 3 m\(^3\) of oxygen had been produced,

(b) solve the differential equation to show that \(\displaystyle V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\) [5 marks]

(c) Deduce (i) the time delay between mixing and oxygen being produced, (ii) the limit on total volume. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Partial fraction decomposition ▶
Part (a) asks us to decompose \(\frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)}\) into partial fractions. This is preparation for Part (b) — when we separate variables in the DE, the RHS will have exactly this form. We set up the standard identity and find the constants using substitution or comparison.

Let:

$$\frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{2x-1} + \frac{B}{x+1}$$

Multiply both sides by \((2x-1)(x+1)\):

$$3 = A(x+1) + B(2x-1)$$

Set \(x = -1\): \(3 = A(0) + B(-3)\), so \(B = -1\)

Set \(x = \frac{1}{2}\): \(3 = A(\frac{3}{2}) + B(0)\), so \(A = 2\)

$$\frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{2}{2x-1} - \frac{1}{x+1}$$

M1 — Sets up the correct partial fraction form and attempts to find constants
A1 — \(A = 2\)
A1 — \(B = -1\) and full correct expression stated
Part (b) Step 2: Separate the variables ▶
Now we solve the DE. The equation is \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{3V}{(2t-1)(t+1)}\). Since \(V\) appears on the RHS, we need to separate variables: divide both sides by \(V\), multiply by \(dt\), and then use the partial fractions from Part (a) on the RHS.

Divide both sides by \(V\) and multiply by \(dt\):

$$\frac{1}{V}\,dV = \frac{3}{(2t-1)(t+1)}\,dt$$

Add integral signs and substitute the partial fractions from Part (a):

$$\int \frac{1}{V}\,dV = \int \left(\frac{2}{2t-1} - \frac{1}{t+1}\right)\,dt$$

B1 — Separates variables correctly with dV and dt in the correct positions
Part (b) Step 3: Integrate both sides ▶
The LHS integrates to \(\ln V\). For the RHS, recall that \(\int \frac{1}{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\ln|ax+b|\). So \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = \frac{2}{2}\ln|2t-1| = \ln|2t-1|\) and \(\int \frac{1}{t+1}\,dt = \ln|t+1|\).

LHS: \(\int \frac{1}{V}\,dV = \ln V\)

RHS: \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = \ln(2t-1)\) and \(\int \frac{1}{t+1}\,dt = \ln(t+1)\)

$$\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + c$$

M1 — Correct attempt at integration: terms of the form \(\ldots\ln(2t-1) + \ldots\ln(t+1)\)
A1ft — Fully correct equation: \(\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + c\)
The coefficient from \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt\) is a common source of error. Students often write \(2\ln(2t-1)\) instead of \(\ln(2t-1)\). Remember: \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = \frac{2}{2}\ln(2t-1) = \ln(2t-1)\) because the inner derivative is 2.
Part (b) Step 4: Apply boundary condition \(t = 2, V = 3\) ▶
We're told that when \(t = 2\), \(V = 3\). Substituting these values into our equation will determine the constant \(c\). Then we can write the particular solution and rearrange to match the target form.

Substitute \(t = 2\), \(V = 3\):

$$\ln 3 = \ln(2(2)-1) - \ln(2+1) + c$$

$$\ln 3 = \ln 3 - \ln 3 + c$$

$$\ln 3 = 0 + c$$

$$c = \ln 3$$

M1 — Substitutes \(t = 2\), \(V = 3\) and attempts to find the constant
Part (b) Step 5: Rearrange to reach the given answer ▶
We have \(\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + \ln 3\). The target is \(V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\). We combine the ln terms using log laws: \(\ln a + \ln b = \ln(ab)\) and \(\ln a - \ln b = \ln\frac{a}{b}\), then exponentiate.

$$\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + \ln 3$$

Combine using log laws: \(\ln a - \ln b + \ln c = \ln\frac{ac}{b}\):

$$\ln V = \ln\left(\frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\right)$$

Since \(\ln V = \ln(\text{something})\), we have \(V = \text{that something}\):

$$\boxed{V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}} \quad \square$$

A1* — Correct processing leading to the given answer with clear log law manipulation shown
You must show the log combination step explicitly. Jumping from \(\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + \ln 3\) straight to the answer loses the A1* mark. The minimum acceptable intermediate step is \(\ln V = \ln\frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\).
Part (c) Step 6: Deduce time delay and volume limit ▶
For (i): the time delay is when oxygen production starts, i.e. when \(V = 0\). Setting \(V = 0\): \(3(2t-1) = 0\) gives \(t = \frac{1}{2}\) hour = 30 minutes. For (ii): as \(t \to \infty\), \(\frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1} \to \frac{6t}{t} = 6\). So the limiting volume is 6 m\(^3\).

(i) Time delay: Set \(V = 0\):

$$3(2t-1) = 0 \implies t = \frac{1}{2} \text{ hour} = \boxed{30 \text{ minutes}}$$

(ii) Limiting volume: As \(t \to \infty\):

$$V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1} = \frac{6t - 3}{t+1} \to \frac{6t}{t} = \boxed{6 \text{ m}^3}$$

B1 — Time delay: 30 minutes (or \(\frac{1}{2}\) hour)
B1 — Limiting volume: 6 m\(^3\)

Paper 1 — October 2020 — Question 14 (10 marks)

A large spherical balloon is deflating. At time \(t\) seconds the balloon has radius \(r\) cm and volume \(V\) cm\(^3\). The volume of the balloon is modelled as decreasing at a constant rate.

(a) Using this model, show that \(\displaystyle\frac{dr}{dt} = -\frac{k}{r^2}\) where \(k\) is a positive constant. [3 marks]

Given that the initial radius of the balloon is 40 cm, and after 5 seconds the radius is 20 cm,

(b) solve the differential equation to find a complete equation linking \(r\) and \(t\). [5 marks]

(c) Find the limitation on the values of \(t\) for which the equation in part (b) is valid. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Translate the model into a DE using the chain rule ▶
"Volume decreasing at a constant rate" means \(\frac{dV}{dt} = -c\) for some positive constant \(c\). We want to show \(\frac{dr}{dt} = -\frac{k}{r^2}\). The connection between \(V\) and \(r\) is the sphere formula \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\), and the chain rule links \(\frac{dV}{dt}\) to \(\frac{dr}{dt}\).

Volume of a sphere: \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)

Differentiate with respect to \(t\) using the chain rule:

$$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dr} \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$$

"Volume decreasing at a constant rate" means \(\frac{dV}{dt} = -c\) (negative because decreasing):

$$-c = 4\pi r^2 \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$$

Divide both sides by \(4\pi r^2\):

$$\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{-c}{4\pi r^2} = -\frac{k}{r^2}$$

where \(k = \frac{c}{4\pi}\) is a positive constant. \(\square\)

B1 — States or uses \(\frac{dV}{dt} = -c\) (or equivalent constant rate of decrease)
M1 — Uses \(V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\) and chain rule to link \(\frac{dV}{dt}\) with \(\frac{dr}{dt}\)
A1 — Arrives at \(\frac{dr}{dt} = -\frac{k}{r^2}\) with \(k\) identified as positive
Part (b) Step 2: Separate variables and integrate ▶
We need to solve \(\frac{dr}{dt} = -\frac{k}{r^2}\). Separate variables: multiply both sides by \(r^2\) and by \(dt\). Then integrate both sides. The LHS gives a power of \(r\), the RHS gives a multiple of \(t\).

Multiply both sides by \(r^2\,dt\):

$$r^2\,dr = -k\,dt$$

Integrate both sides:

$$\int r^2\,dr = \int -k\,dt$$

$$\frac{r^3}{3} = -kt + c$$

M1 — Separates variables correctly
A1 — Correct integration: \(\frac{r^3}{3} = -kt + c\) (or equivalent form)
Part (b) Step 3: Apply first boundary condition (initial radius) ▶
When \(t = 0\), \(r = 40\). Substituting into \(\frac{r^3}{3} = -kt + c\) gives us \(c\) in terms of the (still unknown) constant \(k\). We'll then use the second condition to pin down \(k\).

When \(t = 0\), \(r = 40\):

$$\frac{40^3}{3} = -k(0) + c$$

$$c = \frac{64000}{3}$$

So: \(\frac{r^3}{3} = -kt + \frac{64000}{3}\)

Or equivalently: \(r^3 = -3kt + 64000\)

dM1 — Substitutes \(t = 0\), \(r = 40\) to find the constant
Part (b) Step 4: Apply second boundary condition to find \(k\) ▶
When \(t = 5\), \(r = 20\). Substituting into our equation (which now contains only one unknown, \(k\)) lets us determine \(k\) and write the complete particular solution.

When \(t = 5\), \(r = 20\):

$$20^3 = -3k(5) + 64000$$

$$8000 = -15k + 64000$$

Rearrange:

$$15k = 64000 - 8000 = 56000$$

$$k = \frac{56000}{15} = \frac{11200}{3}$$

Substituting back:

$$r^3 = -3 \times \frac{11200}{3} \times t + 64000$$

$$\boxed{r^3 = 64000 - 11200t}$$

ddM1 — Substitutes second boundary condition (\(t=5\), \(r=20\)) into their equation to find \(k\)
A1 — Correct complete equation: \(r^3 = 64000 - 11200t\) or equivalent
Both boundary conditions must be used. Some candidates only applied one condition and left \(k\) or \(c\) as unknowns — this does not constitute a "complete equation" and scores at most 3/5 marks.
Part (c) Step 5: Find the valid range of \(t\) ▶
The balloon deflates until it is empty (radius = 0). After that, the model no longer applies. We find when \(r = 0\) using our equation from Part (b). Additionally, \(t \geq 0\) since the model starts at \(t = 0\).

The balloon is empty when \(r = 0\):

$$0 = 64000 - 11200t$$

$$11200t = 64000$$

$$t = \frac{64000}{11200} = \frac{40}{7} \approx 5.71 \text{ seconds}$$

The equation is valid for \(0 \leq t \leq \frac{40}{7}\) (the balloon exists from \(t=0\) until it is empty).

M1 — Sets \(r = 0\) (or \(r^3 = 0\)) in their equation and solves for \(t\)
A1 — \(t \leq \frac{40}{7}\) or equivalent (e.g. \(0 \leq t \leq 5\frac{5}{7}\))

Paper 1 — June 2024 — Question 14 (9 marks)

A balloon is being inflated. In a model:

  • the balloon is modelled as a sphere
  • the rate of increase of the radius is inversely proportional to the square root of the radius

At time \(t\) seconds, the radius of the balloon is \(r\) cm.

(a) Write down a differential equation to model this situation. [1 mark]

At the instant when \(t = 10\), the radius is 16 cm and the radius is increasing at 0.9 cm s\(^{-1}\).

(b) Solve the differential equation to show that \(r^{3/2} = 5.4t + 10\) [5 marks]

(c) Hence find the radius of the balloon when \(t = 20\), giving your answer to the nearest millimetre. [2 marks]

(d) Suggest a limitation of the model. [1 mark]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Write down the differential equation ▶
"Rate of increase of radius" = \(\frac{dr}{dt}\). "Inversely proportional to the square root of the radius" means \(\frac{dr}{dt} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\). We introduce a constant of proportionality \(k\).

Translating the English into symbols:

$$\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{k}{\sqrt{r}} = kr^{-1/2}$$

where \(k\) is a positive constant.

B1 — Correct DE: \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{k}{\sqrt{r}}\) or equivalent with any positive constant
Candidates must include the constant of proportionality. Writing \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\) (without the constant) scores B0.
Part (b) Step 2: Find the value of \(k\) using the given information ▶
We're told that when \(t = 10\), \(r = 16\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt} = 0.9\). Substituting into our DE gives us the value of \(k\). This is a common pattern: using a rate condition to determine the proportionality constant before solving.

Substitute \(r = 16\), \(\frac{dr}{dt} = 0.9\) into \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{k}{\sqrt{r}}\):

$$0.9 = \frac{k}{\sqrt{16}} = \frac{k}{4}$$

$$k = 3.6$$

So the DE becomes: \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{3.6}{\sqrt{r}} = 3.6r^{-1/2}\)

M1 — Uses the given values (\(r=16\), \(\frac{dr}{dt}=0.9\)) to find \(k\)
Part (b) Step 3: Separate variables and integrate ▶
Now we solve \(\frac{dr}{dt} = 3.6r^{-1/2}\). Separate variables by multiplying both sides by \(r^{1/2}\,dt\), giving \(r^{1/2}\,dr = 3.6\,dt\). Integrate both sides using the power rule.

Multiply both sides by \(r^{1/2}\) and by \(dt\):

$$r^{1/2}\,dr = 3.6\,dt$$

Integrate both sides:

$$\int r^{1/2}\,dr = \int 3.6\,dt$$

LHS: \(\int r^{1/2}\,dr = \frac{r^{3/2}}{3/2} = \frac{2}{3}r^{3/2}\)

RHS: \(\int 3.6\,dt = 3.6t\)

$$\frac{2}{3}r^{3/2} = 3.6t + c$$

M1 — Separates variables and integrates: \(r^{n} \to r^{n+1}\) on LHS, \(kt\) on RHS
A1 — Correct integration: \(\frac{2}{3}r^{3/2} = 3.6t + c\)
Part (b) Step 4: Apply boundary condition and reach the given answer ▶
We use the condition: when \(t = 10\), \(r = 16\). Substituting these values determines \(c\). Then we rearrange to match the target form \(r^{3/2} = 5.4t + 10\). Note: multiplying \(\frac{2}{3}r^{3/2} = 3.6t + c\) through by \(\frac{3}{2}\) will give \(r^{3/2} = 5.4t + \frac{3c}{2}\).

When \(t = 10\), \(r = 16\) (so \(r^{3/2} = 16^{3/2} = (\sqrt{16})^3 = 64\)):

$$\frac{2}{3}(64) = 3.6(10) + c$$

$$\frac{128}{3} = 36 + c$$

$$c = \frac{128}{3} - 36 = \frac{128 - 108}{3} = \frac{20}{3}$$

Substituting back and multiplying everything by \(\frac{3}{2}\):

$$\frac{2}{3}r^{3/2} = 3.6t + \frac{20}{3}$$

Multiply both sides by \(\frac{3}{2}\):

$$r^{3/2} = \frac{3}{2} \times 3.6t + \frac{3}{2} \times \frac{20}{3}$$

$$r^{3/2} = 5.4t + 10$$

$$\boxed{r^{3/2} = 5.4t + 10} \quad \square$$

dM1 — Substitutes \(t = 10\), \(r = 16\) into their integrated equation to find the constant

Part (c) Step 5: Find the radius when \(t = 20\) ▶
Substitute \(t = 20\) into \(r^{3/2} = 5.4t + 10\) and solve for \(r\). To solve \(r^{3/2} = k\), raise both sides to the power \(\frac{2}{3}\): \(r = k^{2/3}\).

$$r^{3/2} = 5.4(20) + 10 = 108 + 10 = 118$$

Raise both sides to the power \(\frac{2}{3}\):

$$r = 118^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{118})^2$$

$$r = (4.9066...)^2 = 24.07... \text{ cm}$$

To the nearest millimetre: \(\boxed{r = 24.1 \text{ cm}}\)

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals in the question:

  • The word "differential equation" appears explicitly
  • A rate of change is described: "rate of increase", "rate of decrease", "rate of change is proportional to..."
  • The question asks you to "show that" a formula involving \(e\), \(\ln\), or a power of the variable holds
  • A modelling context: population growth, cooling, filling/emptying tanks, balloon inflation/deflation
  • You see the instruction to "solve the differential equation"
  • Part (a) often asks you to set up or derive the DE, Part (b) asks you to solve it
  • Boundary/initial conditions are always given: "initially...", "when \(t = 0\)...", "at time \(t = 5\)..."

Types of Differential Equations in Edexcel

  • Simple separable (no fractions needed): \(\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{k}{r^2}\) — just integrate powers. (Oct 2020 P1, June 2024 P1)
  • Separable with \(\ln\): \(\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{H \cdot f(t)}{c}\) — separates to \(\frac{1}{H}\,dH\), integrates to \(\ln H\). (June 2018 P1)
  • Requiring partial fractions: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{V \cdot g(t)}{h(t)}\) where \(g(t)/h(t)\) needs decomposing. (June 2022 P2, June 2024 P2)
  • Logistic-type: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = V(a-V)\) — partial fractions on \(\frac{1}{V(a-V)}\). (June 2024 P2)
  • Modelling setup: Part (a) asks you to derive the DE from a verbal description using the chain rule. (Oct 2020 P1, June 2023 P2, June 2024 P1)
