The Approach

Integration questions come in several types. Identify the type first, then follow the appropriate strategy:

1
Basic power integration
Expand brackets and rewrite all terms as \(ax^n\). Then apply \(\int ax^n\,dx = \frac{a}{n+1}x^{n+1} + c\). Remember: \(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\), \(\frac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\), etc. Always add \(+c\) for indefinite integrals.
2
Area under/between curves
Set up the definite integral with correct limits. For area between two curves: \(\int_a^b (\text{top} - \text{bottom})\,dx\). If the curve goes below the x-axis, the integral gives a negative value — take the absolute value or split the integral at the roots.
3
Integration by substitution
Given \(u = g(x)\): find \(\frac{du}{dx}\), rearrange to get \(dx = \ldots\,du\), replace all \(x\)-terms (including \(dx\)) with \(u\)-terms, change limits if definite. The goal is an integral purely in \(u\).
4
Integration by parts
Use when you see a product like \(x\ln x\), \(x e^x\), \(x\sin x\), or \((\ln x)^n\). Formula: \(\int u\,\frac{dv}{dx}\,dx = uv - \int v\,\frac{du}{dx}\,dx\). Choose \(u\) = the thing that simplifies when differentiated (usually \(\ln x\), \(x^n\), or polynomial). May need to apply twice.
5
Integration with partial fractions
When the integrand is a rational function like \(\frac{f(x)}{(ax+b)(cx+d)}\): split into partial fractions first, then integrate each fraction separately using \(\int \frac{A}{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{A}{a}\ln|ax+b| + c\).

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting +c: Every indefinite integral must include the constant of integration. This is a free mark that many candidates throw away.
  • Sign errors with areas below the x-axis: If a curve dips below the x-axis, the integral gives a negative value. You must either split at the roots or take \(-\int\) for the negative region. Simply integrating across the whole interval gives the wrong answer.
  • Not changing limits in substitution: When using a substitution for a definite integral, you MUST convert the limits from \(x\)-values to \(u\)-values. Candidates who forget this and substitute back earn fewer marks.
  • Wrong choice of \(u\) and \(\frac{dv}{dx}\) in by parts: A poor choice (e.g. letting \(u = e^x\) when \(\ln x\) is present) leads to a harder integral. LIATE rule: Logs, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential — choose \(u\) from left to right.
  • Incomplete partial fractions: If the degree of the numerator equals or exceeds the denominator, you must do polynomial long division first before splitting into partial fractions.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write "Let \(u = \ldots\), \(\frac{du}{dx} = \ldots\)" explicitly — this earns a method mark even if subsequent work is flawed.
  • For "show that" integration questions, show every step of the substitution and simplification. The examiner needs to see the transition from \(x\) to \(u\).
  • When computing definite integrals, write the integrated expression with square brackets and limits before substituting numbers: \(\left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^2\).
  • Check your answer makes sense: areas should be positive, and "exact" answers should contain \(\ln\), \(\pi\), surds — not decimals.

Paper 1 — June 2019 — Question 8 (10 marks)

Figure 2 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation \(y = x(x+2)(x-4)\).

The region \(R_1\), shown shaded, is bounded by the curve and the negative x-axis.

(a) Show that the exact area of \(R_1\) is \(\dfrac{20}{3}\) [4 marks]

The region \(R_2\) is bounded by the curve, the positive x-axis and the line \(x = b\), where \(b\) is a positive constant and \(0 < b < 4\). Given that area of \(R_1\) = area of \(R_2\),

(b) verify that \(b\) satisfies the equation \((b+2)^2(3b^2 - 20b + 20) = 0\) [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Identify the limits of integration ▶
The region \(R_1\) is bounded by the curve and the negative x-axis. We need to find where the curve crosses the x-axis on the negative side. Since \(y = x(x+2)(x-4)\), the roots are \(x = 0\), \(x = -2\), and \(x = 4\). On the negative side, the relevant roots are \(x = -2\) and \(x = 0\).

The curve \(y = x(x+2)(x-4)\) crosses the x-axis at \(x = -2\), \(x = 0\), and \(x = 4\).

The region \(R_1\) is between \(x = -2\) and \(x = 0\) (the negative x-axis portion).

We need \(\displaystyle \text{Area} = \int_{-2}^{0} x(x+2)(x-4)\,dx\)

Between \(x = -2\) and \(x = 0\), the curve is above the x-axis (check: at \(x = -1\), \(y = (-1)(1)(-5) = 5 > 0\)). So the integral gives a positive value directly — no need for absolute value here.
Part (a) Step 2: Expand the cubic before integrating ▶
We cannot integrate a product directly — we need to expand into separate power terms. Multiply out \(x(x+2)(x-4)\) step by step.

First multiply \((x+2)(x-4)\):

$$(x+2)(x-4) = x^2 - 4x + 2x - 8 = x^2 - 2x - 8$$

Now multiply by \(x\):

$$x(x^2 - 2x - 8) = x^3 - 2x^2 - 8x$$

So we need: \(\displaystyle \int_{-2}^{0} (x^3 - 2x^2 - 8x)\,dx\)

M1 — Expanding brackets and attempting to integrate each term
Part (a) Step 3: Integrate term by term ▶
Now we apply the power rule \(\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\) to each term separately. Then we evaluate using the limits \(-2\) and \(0\).

Integrate each term: increase the power by 1, divide by the new power:

$$\int (x^3 - 2x^2 - 8x)\,dx = \frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{2x^3}{3} - \frac{8x^2}{2}$$

Simplify:

$$= \frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{2x^3}{3} - 4x^2$$

A1 — Correct integration: at least two terms correct
Part (a) Step 4: Apply the limits and evaluate ▶
For a definite integral, we substitute the upper limit and subtract the lower limit: \([F(x)]_a^b = F(b) - F(a)\). Here \(F(0)\) is straightforward (everything is 0), so we just need to compute \(F(-2)\) carefully.

$$\left[\frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{2x^3}{3} - 4x^2\right]_{-2}^{0}$$

At \(x = 0\): all terms are 0.

At \(x = -2\): \(\frac{(-2)^4}{4} - \frac{2(-2)^3}{3} - 4(-2)^2\)

$$= \frac{16}{4} - \frac{2(-8)}{3} - 4(4)$$

$$= 4 + \frac{16}{3} - 16$$

$$= -12 + \frac{16}{3} = \frac{-36 + 16}{3} = \frac{-20}{3}$$

So: \(F(0) - F(-2) = 0 - \left(\frac{-20}{3}\right) = \frac{20}{3}\)

$$\boxed{\text{Area of } R_1 = \frac{20}{3}} \quad \square$$

M1 — Substituting limits into integrated expression
A1 — Correct answer: \(\frac{20}{3}\)
This is a "show that" question, so you must show the substitution of \(x = -2\) explicitly. Candidates who jumped from the integrated expression to "\(= \frac{20}{3}\)" without showing the arithmetic lost the final mark.
Part (b) Step 5: Set up the equation for equal areas ▶
We are told area of \(R_2\) = area of \(R_1 = \frac{20}{3}\). The region \(R_2\) is bounded by the curve from \(x = 0\) to \(x = b\), where the curve is below the x-axis (since between 0 and 4 the curve dips below). So we integrate from 0 to \(b\) and the integral will be negative (curve below x-axis). We set \(-\int_0^b y\,dx = \frac{20}{3}\).

Between \(x = 0\) and \(x = 4\), the curve is below the x-axis (check: at \(x = 1\), \(y = 1(3)(-3) = -9 < 0\)).

So area of \(R_2 = -\int_0^b (x^3 - 2x^2 - 8x)\,dx = \frac{20}{3}\)

Therefore: \(\int_0^b (x^3 - 2x^2 - 8x)\,dx = -\frac{20}{3}\)

Using our antiderivative from Part (a):

$$\left[\frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{2x^3}{3} - 4x^2\right]_0^b = -\frac{20}{3}$$

$$\frac{b^4}{4} - \frac{2b^3}{3} - 4b^2 = -\frac{20}{3}$$

M1 — Setting up integral from 0 to \(b\) equal to \(\pm\frac{20}{3}\)
Part (b) Step 6: Rearrange and verify the given factored form ▶
We need to show this equation rearranges to \((b+2)^2(3b^2 - 20b + 20) = 0\). Strategy: multiply through by 12 to clear denominators, collect everything on one side, and factor.

Multiply both sides by 12 to clear fractions:

$$3b^4 - 8b^3 - 48b^2 = -80$$

Bring everything to one side:

$$3b^4 - 8b^3 - 48b^2 + 80 = 0$$

We need to show this equals \((b+2)^2(3b^2 - 20b + 20)\). Expand to verify:

\((b+2)^2 = b^2 + 4b + 4\)

\((b^2 + 4b + 4)(3b^2 - 20b + 20)\):

$$= 3b^4 - 20b^3 + 20b^2 + 12b^3 - 80b^2 + 80b + 12b^2 - 80b + 80$$

$$= 3b^4 - 8b^3 - 48b^2 + 80$$

This matches! (Note the constant 80 on the RHS balances the -80 we moved over.)

Therefore \(b\) satisfies \((b+2)^2(3b^2 - 20b + 20) = 0\). \(\square\)

A1 — Correct polynomial equation in \(b\)
M1 — Attempting to factor or verify the given form
A1 — Complete verification with clear working

Paper 2 — October 2020 — Question 6 (7 marks)

Given that

$$\frac{x^2 + 8x - 3}{x + 2} \equiv Ax + B + \frac{C}{x+2}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R},\; x \neq -2$$

(a) find the values of the constants \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\) [3 marks]

(b) Hence, using algebraic integration, find the exact value of

$$\int_0^6 \frac{x^2 + 8x - 3}{x + 2}\,dx$$

giving your answer in the form \(a + b\ln 2\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are integers to be found. [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Perform polynomial long division ▶
The numerator \(x^2 + 8x - 3\) has degree 2 and the denominator \(x+2\) has degree 1. Since the degree of the numerator is higher, we must do polynomial division first to get a quotient \(Ax + B\) plus a remainder \(\frac{C}{x+2}\). This is sometimes called "improper fraction" decomposition.

Divide \(x^2 + 8x - 3\) by \(x + 2\):

\(x^2 \div x = x\). Multiply: \(x(x+2) = x^2 + 2x\). Subtract from numerator:

$$(x^2 + 8x - 3) - (x^2 + 2x) = 6x - 3$$

\(6x \div x = 6\). Multiply: \(6(x+2) = 6x + 12\). Subtract:

$$(6x - 3) - (6x + 12) = -15$$

So: \(\dfrac{x^2 + 8x - 3}{x+2} = x + 6 + \dfrac{-15}{x+2}\)

Therefore: \(A = 1\), \(B = 6\), \(C = -15\)

M1 — Multiplying by \((x+2)\) and attempting to find A, B, C (or performing long division)
A1 — Two of \(A = 1\), \(B = 6\), \(C = -15\) correct
A1 — All three correct: \(A = 1\), \(B = 6\), \(C = -15\)
Part (b) Step 2: Integrate each term ▶
Now that we have decomposed the fraction into simpler terms, we can integrate each one separately. The key result is \(\int \frac{1}{x+2}\,dx = \ln|x+2|\). The polynomial terms \(x + 6\) integrate using the standard power rule.

$$\int_0^6 \left(x + 6 - \frac{15}{x+2}\right)\,dx$$

Integrate term by term:

$$= \left[\frac{x^2}{2} + 6x - 15\ln|x+2|\right]_0^6$$

M1 — Integrating to get at least \(\frac{x^2}{2}\) and a \(\ln\) term
A1ft — Correct integration of their expression (follow-through from Part a)
Part (b) Step 3: Substitute limits and simplify ▶
Now we evaluate at the upper limit \(x = 6\) and subtract the value at \(x = 0\). Be careful with the \(\ln\) terms — the question asks for the answer in the form \(a + b\ln 2\), so we need to express \(\ln 8\) and \(\ln 2\) in terms of \(\ln 2\).

At \(x = 6\):

$$\frac{36}{2} + 36 - 15\ln 8 = 18 + 36 - 15\ln 8 = 54 - 15\ln 8$$

At \(x = 0\):

$$0 + 0 - 15\ln 2 = -15\ln 2$$

Subtract: upper minus lower:

$$(54 - 15\ln 8) - (-15\ln 2) = 54 - 15\ln 8 + 15\ln 2$$

Since \(\ln 8 = \ln 2^3 = 3\ln 2\):

$$= 54 - 15(3\ln 2) + 15\ln 2$$

$$= 54 - 45\ln 2 + 15\ln 2$$

$$= 54 - 30\ln 2$$

$$\boxed{\int_0^6 \frac{x^2 + 8x - 3}{x+2}\,dx = 54 - 30\ln 2}$$

So \(a = 54\) and \(b = -30\).

M1 — Substituting both limits and subtracting
A1 — Correct exact answer: \(54 - 30\ln 2\)
The question specifies "in the form \(a + b\ln 2\)". Writing \(54 - 15\ln 8 + 15\ln 2\) without simplifying to a single \(\ln 2\) term loses the final accuracy mark. Always use the log law \(\ln a^n = n\ln a\) to combine.

We are given the substitution \(x = u^2 + 1\). To change the variable of integration from \(x\) to \(u\), we need to replace \(dx\) with something in terms of \(du\). Differentiate \(x = u^2 + 1\) with respect to \(u\).

$$\boxed{a = \frac{49}{36}}$$

Paper 1 — October 2021 — Question 11 (8 marks)

Figure 2 shows a sketch of part of the curve with equation \(y = (\ln x)^2\), \(x > 0\).

The finite region \(R\) is bounded by the curve, the line \(x = 2\), the x-axis and the line \(x = 4\).

(a) Use the trapezium rule, with all values in the table, to estimate the area of \(R\), giving your answer to 3 significant figures. [3 marks]

(b) Use algebraic integration to find the exact area of \(R\), giving your answer in the form \(a(\ln 2)^2 + b\ln 2 + c\), where \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are integers to be found. [5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Apply the trapezium rule ▶
The trapezium rule approximates the area by dividing it into strips. The formula is \(\frac{h}{2}[y_0 + y_n + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \ldots + y_{n-1})]\) where \(h\) is the strip width. From the table we have 5 values at \(x = 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, 4\), so there are 4 strips of width \(h = 0.5\).

From the table: \(y_0 = 0.4805\), \(y_1 = 0.8396\), \(y_2 = 1.2069\), \(y_3 = 1.5694\), \(y_4 = 1.9218\)

$$h = \frac{4 - 2}{4} = 0.5$$

Apply the trapezium rule:

$$\text{Area} \approx \frac{0.5}{2}\left[0.4805 + 1.9218 + 2(0.8396 + 1.2069 + 1.5694)\right]$$

$$= 0.25\left[2.4023 + 2(3.6159)\right]$$

$$= 0.25\left[2.4023 + 7.2318\right]$$

$$= 0.25 \times 9.6341 = 2.408... \approx 2.41$$

$$\boxed{\text{Area} \approx 2.41 \text{ (3 s.f.)}}$$

M1 — Correct structure of trapezium rule with \(h = 0.5\)
A1 — Correct substitution of all \(y\)-values
A1 — Answer: 2.41 (3 s.f.)
Part (b) Step 2: Set up integration by parts for \((\ln x)^2\) ▶
We need \(\int (\ln x)^2\,dx\). This requires integration by parts. The formula is \(\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\). We choose \(u = (\ln x)^2\) (because it simplifies when differentiated) and \(dv = dx\) (so \(v = x\)).

Let \(u = (\ln x)^2\) and \(\frac{dv}{dx} = 1\):

\(\frac{du}{dx} = 2\ln x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{2\ln x}{x}\)

\(v = x\)

Apply integration by parts: \(\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\):

$$\int (\ln x)^2\,dx = x(\ln x)^2 - \int x \cdot \frac{2\ln x}{x}\,dx$$

$$= x(\ln x)^2 - 2\int \ln x\,dx$$

M1 — Applying integration by parts with \(u = (\ln x)^2\)
A1 — Correct expression: \(x(\ln x)^2 - 2\int \ln x\,dx\)
Part (b) Step 3: Apply integration by parts a second time for \(\int \ln x\,dx\) ▶
We now need \(\int \ln x\,dx\). This also requires by parts. Let \(u = \ln x\) and \(dv = dx\). This is a standard result that you should ideally know: \(\int \ln x\,dx = x\ln x - x + c\).

For \(\int \ln x\,dx\): let \(u = \ln x\), \(\frac{dv}{dx} = 1\):

\(\frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{x}\), \(v = x\)

$$\int \ln x\,dx = x\ln x - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x}\,dx = x\ln x - \int 1\,dx = x\ln x - x$$

Substituting back:

$$\int (\ln x)^2\,dx = x(\ln x)^2 - 2(x\ln x - x) = x(\ln x)^2 - 2x\ln x + 2x$$

M1 — Applying by parts a second time to \(\int \ln x\,dx\)
A1 — Complete antiderivative: \(x(\ln x)^2 - 2x\ln x + 2x\)
Part (b) Step 4: Evaluate with limits \(x = 2\) to \(x = 4\) ▶
Now we evaluate \([x(\ln x)^2 - 2x\ln x + 2x]_2^4\). The answer must be in terms of \(\ln 2\) — since \(\ln 4 = 2\ln 2\), everything will reduce nicely. Be very careful with the algebra here.

At \(x = 4\): note \(\ln 4 = \ln 2^2 = 2\ln 2\)

$$4(2\ln 2)^2 - 2(4)(2\ln 2) + 2(4) = 4 \cdot 4(\ln 2)^2 - 16\ln 2 + 8$$

$$= 16(\ln 2)^2 - 16\ln 2 + 8$$

At \(x = 2\):

$$2(\ln 2)^2 - 2(2)\ln 2 + 2(2) = 2(\ln 2)^2 - 4\ln 2 + 4$$

Subtract lower from upper:

$$\left[16(\ln 2)^2 - 16\ln 2 + 8\right] - \left[2(\ln 2)^2 - 4\ln 2 + 4\right]$$

$$= 14(\ln 2)^2 - 12\ln 2 + 4$$

$$\boxed{\text{Area} = 14(\ln 2)^2 - 12\ln 2 + 4}$$

So \(a = 14\), \(b = -12\), \(c = 4\).

A1 — Correct final answer: \(14(\ln 2)^2 - 12\ln 2 + 4\)
The most common error was getting \(\ln 4\) and not converting it to \(2\ln 2\). The question specifies the form \(a(\ln 2)^2 + b\ln 2 + c\), so you must use \(\ln 4 = 2\ln 2\) throughout. Also note \((\ln 4)^2 = (2\ln 2)^2 = 4(\ln 2)^2\), not \(2(\ln 2)^2\).

How to Recognise the Integration Type

Read the question carefully for these signals:

  • "Use the substitution..." — they tell you what to use. Just follow the method mechanically.
  • "Express in partial fractions..." followed by "Hence find..." — partial fractions then integrate.
  • "Use algebraic integration" — means show full working (not a calculator or trapezium rule).
  • Product of two different types (e.g. \(x\ln x\), \(xe^x\)) — integration by parts.
  • Rational function \(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) where \(g(x)\) factorises — partial fractions.
  • Composite function with an inner function and its derivative present — reverse chain rule or substitution.
  • "Find the area of the shaded region" — definite integral, possibly needing to split at roots.

Key Integration Results

  • \(\displaystyle\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + c \quad (n \neq -1)\)
  • \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + c\)
  • \(\displaystyle\int e^{kx}\,dx = \frac{1}{k}e^{kx} + c\)
  • \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{ax+b}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\ln|ax+b| + c\)
  • \(\displaystyle\int \ln x\,dx = x\ln x - x + c\) (by parts)
  • \(\displaystyle\int x e^x\,dx = xe^x - e^x + c\) (by parts)
  • \(\displaystyle\int \sin(kx)\,dx = -\frac{1}{k}\cos(kx) + c\)
  • \(\displaystyle\int \cos(kx)\,dx = \frac{1}{k}\sin(kx) + c\)
