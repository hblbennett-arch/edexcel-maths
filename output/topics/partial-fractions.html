Guided Approach

The standard method for decomposing into partial fractions:

1
Check if the fraction is improper
Compare the degree of the numerator with the degree of the denominator. If the numerator's degree is greater than or equal to the denominator's degree, you must perform polynomial long division first. The result is a polynomial plus a proper fraction.
2
Write the correct form of the decomposition
For distinct linear factors \((ax+b)(cx+d)\): use \(\frac{A}{ax+b} + \frac{B}{cx+d}\).
For a repeated factor \((ax+b)^2\): use \(\frac{A}{ax+b} + \frac{B}{(ax+b)^2}\).
For three factors: add an extra term for each factor.
3
Multiply both sides by the full denominator
This clears the fractions and gives you an identity. Then either substitute strategic values of \(x\) (values that make each factor zero) or compare coefficients to find the unknown constants.
4
Write out the final partial fractions
Substitute your values of \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) back into the decomposition form. Check by recombining if time permits.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Sign errors when substituting: When you substitute \(x = -1\) (to make \((x+1) = 0\)), carefully track negatives through the algebra. This is the single most common error in partial fractions questions.
  • Forgetting to divide first for improper fractions: If the numerator has the same or higher degree than the denominator, you MUST perform algebraic division before decomposing. Writing \(\frac{A}{x-3} + \frac{B}{1-2x}\) when there should also be a constant term \(A + ...\) loses all marks.
  • Missing the repeated factor term: For \(\frac{1}{(x-1)^2(x+2)}\), you need \(\frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{B}{(x-1)^2} + \frac{C}{x+2}\), not just two terms. Missing the \(\frac{B}{(x-1)^2}\) term means you cannot possibly get the right answer.
  • Writing just the constants, not the fractions: You must write out the complete partial fractions (e.g. \(\frac{2}{x-3} + \frac{1}{x+1}\)), not just "\(A=2, B=1\)". The mark scheme explicitly states the fractions must be seen.
  • Incorrect integration of \(\frac{1}{ax+b}\): The integral is \(\frac{1}{a}\ln|ax+b|\), not \(\ln|ax+b|\). Forgetting the \(\frac{1}{a}\) factor when the coefficient of \(x\) is not 1 is very common.

What Top Candidates Do

  • State the form of the decomposition explicitly before starting any algebra — this earns a B1 mark even if subsequent work has errors.
  • Use the "cover-up" method for speed: cover up \((x-3)\) in the original fraction and substitute \(x=3\) to read off the numerator of the \(\frac{...}{x-3}\) term immediately.
  • After finding constants, write the complete fractions with a therefore symbol — this is where the A1 mark lives.
  • When partial fractions are used in integration (Parts b/c), write \(\int\) of the partial fractions separately — don't try to integrate before splitting.

Paper 2 — June 2022 — Question 14 (10 marks)

(a) Express \(\displaystyle \frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)}\) in partial fractions. [3 marks]

When chemical A and chemical B are mixed, oxygen is produced. The total volume of oxygen produced, \(V\) m\(^3\), \(t\) hours after the chemicals were mixed, is modelled by the differential equation

$$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{3V}{(2t-1)(t+1)}, \quad V > 0, \quad t > k$$

where \(k\) is a constant. Given that exactly 2 hours after the chemicals were mixed, a total volume of 3 m\(^3\) of oxygen had been produced,

(b) solve the differential equation to show that \(\displaystyle V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\) [5 marks]

(c) Explain the significance of the value of \(k\) in the context of the model. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Write the correct form of the decomposition ▶
We have two distinct linear factors in the denominator: \((2x-1)\) and \((x+1)\). For distinct linear factors, we write one constant over each factor. This form earns a mark immediately just for being written down correctly.

Since the denominator has two distinct linear factors, we write:

$$\frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{A}{2x-1} + \frac{B}{x+1}$$

M1 — Correct form of partial fraction decomposition stated
Part (a) Step 2: Multiply through and find the constants ▶
Multiplying both sides by \((2x-1)(x+1)\) clears the denominators and gives us an identity. Then we substitute values of \(x\) that make each factor zero to isolate \(A\) and \(B\) one at a time. This is faster and less error-prone than comparing coefficients.

Multiply both sides by \((2x-1)(x+1)\):

$$3 = A(x+1) + B(2x-1)$$

Substitute \(x = -1\) (makes \((x+1) = 0\), eliminating the \(A\) term):

$$3 = A(0) + B(2(-1)-1) = B(-3)$$

$$B = -1$$

Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) (makes \((2x-1) = 0\), eliminating the \(B\) term):

$$3 = A\left(\frac{1}{2}+1\right) + B(0) = A\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)$$

$$A = 2$$

M1 — Correct method to find both constants (substitution or comparing coefficients)
Part (a) Step 3: Write the final answer as complete fractions ▶
The A1 mark requires the complete partial fractions written out — not just the values of A and B. Always write the full decomposition as your final answer.

$$\frac{3}{(2x-1)(x+1)} = \frac{2}{2x-1} + \frac{-1}{x+1} = \frac{2}{2x-1} - \frac{1}{x+1}$$

A1 — Correct partial fractions written in full (not just values of A, B)
Candidates who wrote only "A = 2, B = -1" without writing the fractions out lost the A1 mark. You must show the complete decomposition.
Part (b) Step 4: Separate variables in the DE ▶
The DE has the form \(\frac{dV}{dt} = f(t) \cdot V\). To separate variables, we get all \(V\) terms on one side and all \(t\) terms on the other. Then we replace the \(t\)-fraction with our partial fractions from Part (a).

Starting with \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{3V}{(2t-1)(t+1)}\), separate variables:

$$\frac{1}{V}\,dV = \frac{3}{(2t-1)(t+1)}\,dt$$

Using Part (a), replace the right-hand side with partial fractions:

$$\frac{1}{V}\,dV = \left(\frac{2}{2t-1} - \frac{1}{t+1}\right)dt$$

B1 — Correct separation of variables
Part (b) Step 5: Integrate both sides ▶
Now we integrate each side. The left gives \(\ln|V|\). On the right, remember that \(\int \frac{1}{2t-1}\,dt = \frac{1}{2}\ln|2t-1|\) (the chain rule factor), so \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = \ln|2t-1|\). Don't forget the constant of integration.

$$\int \frac{1}{V}\,dV = \int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt - \int \frac{1}{t+1}\,dt$$

Left side: \(\ln|V|\)

Right side, first term: \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\ln|2t-1| = \ln|2t-1|\)

Right side, second term: \(\int \frac{1}{t+1}\,dt = \ln|t+1|\)

$$\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + c$$

M1 — Correct integration of the partial fractions to give ln terms
A1ft — Fully correct equation (following their A, B from Part a)
A very common error was writing \(\int \frac{2}{2t-1}\,dt = 2\ln|2t-1|\) instead of the correct \(\ln|2t-1|\). Remember the chain rule: dividing by the coefficient of \(t\) inside the bracket.
Part (b) Step 6: Use the boundary condition and simplify ▶
We're told \(V = 3\) when \(t = 2\). Substituting this lets us find \(c\). Then we use log laws to combine terms and exponentiate to get the given result \(V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\).

Substitute \(V = 3\), \(t = 2\):

$$\ln 3 = \ln(3) - \ln(3) + c$$

$$\ln 3 = 0 + c$$

$$c = \ln 3$$

Substitute back:

$$\ln V = \ln(2t-1) - \ln(t+1) + \ln 3$$

Use log laws: \(\ln a + \ln b = \ln(ab)\) and \(\ln a - \ln b = \ln\frac{a}{b}\):

$$\ln V = \ln\frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}$$

Exponentiate both sides:

$$\boxed{V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}} \quad \square$$

dM1 — Using boundary condition to find c and attempting to reach V = ...
A1* — Reaching the given answer with clear working
Part (c) Step 7: Interpret the value of \(k\) ▶
The question states \(t > k\). Looking at our answer \(V = \frac{3(2t-1)}{t+1}\), we need \(V > 0\) (given in the DE). Since \(t+1 > 0\) for all \(t > 0\), we need \(2t-1 > 0\), giving \(t > \frac{1}{2}\). So \(k = \frac{1}{2}\). In context, this means there's a 30-minute delay before oxygen starts being produced.

For \(V > 0\), we need \(2t - 1 > 0\), so \(t > \frac{1}{2}\).

Therefore \(k = \frac{1}{2}\).

In context: There is a delay of 30 minutes (half an hour) between mixing the chemicals and the first oxygen being produced.

B1 — \(k = \frac{1}{2}\)
B1 — Correct interpretation in context (time delay before oxygen is produced)

Paper 2 — June 2018 — Question 11 (7 marks)

$$\frac{1 + 11x - 6x^2}{(x-3)(1-2x)} \equiv A + \frac{B}{x-3} + \frac{C}{1-2x}$$

(a) Find the values of the constants \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\). [4 marks]

(b) Hence, or otherwise, prove that \(f(x)\) is a decreasing function for \(x > 3\). [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Recognise this is an improper fraction ▶
The numerator \(1 + 11x - 6x^2\) has degree 2. The denominator \((x-3)(1-2x) = -2x^2 + 7x - 3\) also has degree 2. When the degrees are equal, the fraction is improper and the decomposition must include a constant term \(A\) (the quotient from the division). This is why the form given is \(A + \frac{B}{x-3} + \frac{C}{1-2x}\) rather than just \(\frac{B}{x-3} + \frac{C}{1-2x}\).

The numerator has degree 2 and the denominator also has degree 2 (since \((x-3)(1-2x) = -2x^2 + 7x - 3\)).

Equal degrees means this is an improper fraction. The form is already given:

$$\frac{1 + 11x - 6x^2}{(x-3)(1-2x)} \equiv A + \frac{B}{x-3} + \frac{C}{1-2x}$$

Key insight: The constant \(A\) represents the result of the long division. We can find \(A\) quickly by comparing the leading coefficients: the numerator's leading coefficient is \(-6\) and the denominator's is \(-2\), so \(A = \frac{-6}{-2} = 3\).

B1 — Stating or using \(A = 3\) (by any valid method)
Part (a) Step 2: Multiply through and find B and C ▶
With \(A = 3\) known, we multiply both sides by \((x-3)(1-2x)\) to create an identity. Then we substitute the "magic" values \(x = 3\) and \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) to find \(B\) and \(C\) individually.

Multiply both sides by \((x-3)(1-2x)\):

$$1 + 11x - 6x^2 = A(x-3)(1-2x) + B(1-2x) + C(x-3)$$

Substitute \(x = 3\) (makes \((x-3) = 0\)):

$$1 + 33 - 54 = B(1 - 6) + C(0)$$

$$-20 = -5B$$

$$B = 4$$

Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) (makes \((1-2x) = 0\)):

$$1 + \frac{11}{2} - \frac{6}{4} = A(0) + B(0) + C\left(\frac{1}{2} - 3\right)$$

$$1 + 5.5 - 1.5 = C\left(-\frac{5}{2}\right)$$

$$5 = -\frac{5}{2}C$$

$$C = -2$$

M1 — Correct method to find B and C (substitution or comparing coefficients)
A1 — \(B = 4\)
A1 — \(C = -2\)
Part (a) Summary: Write the complete decomposition ▶

$$\frac{1 + 11x - 6x^2}{(x-3)(1-2x)} = 3 + \frac{4}{x-3} - \frac{2}{1-2x}$$

Equivalently: \(= 3 + \frac{4}{x-3} + \frac{2}{2x-1}\)

Many candidates struggled because they forgot the constant term and tried to equate directly to \(\frac{B}{x-3} + \frac{C}{1-2x}\), which is impossible for an improper fraction. The presence of \(A\) in the given identity was a strong hint.
Part (b) Step 3: Differentiate using the partial fraction form ▶
To prove a function is decreasing, we show \(f'(x) < 0\) for all \(x\) in the given domain. Differentiating the partial fraction form is much easier than differentiating the original — each term differentiates using the standard result \(\frac{d}{dx}(x-a)^{-1} = -(x-a)^{-2}\).

Rewrite using negative powers:

$$f(x) = 3 + 4(x-3)^{-1} - 2(1-2x)^{-1}$$

Differentiate:

\(\frac{d}{dx}[4(x-3)^{-1}] = -4(x-3)^{-2}\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[-2(1-2x)^{-1}] = -2 \times (-1)(1-2x)^{-2} \times (-2) = -4(1-2x)^{-2}\)

$$f'(x) = \frac{-4}{(x-3)^2} + \frac{-4}{(1-2x)^2}$$

M1 — Differentiating the partial fraction form to get terms involving \((x-3)^{-2}\) and \((1-2x)^{-2}\)
A1 — Correct derivative
Part (b) Step 4: Prove \(f'(x) < 0\) for all \(x > 3\) ▶
We now have \(f'(x) = \frac{-4}{(x-3)^2} - \frac{4}{(1-2x)^2}\). Both denominators are squared, so they are always positive. Each fraction has a negative numerator. A negative divided by a positive is negative. So the sum of two negative terms is negative. This completes the proof.

$$f'(x) = \frac{-4}{(x-3)^2} - \frac{4}{(1-2x)^2}$$

For \(x > 3\):

  • \((x-3)^2 > 0\) always (squared term), so \(\frac{-4}{(x-3)^2} < 0\)
  • \((1-2x)^2 > 0\) always (squared term), so \(\frac{-4}{(1-2x)^2} < 0\)

Since \(f'(x)\) is the sum of two negative quantities, \(f'(x) < 0\) for all \(x > 3\).

Therefore \(f(x)\) is a decreasing function for \(x > 3\). \(\square\)

A1 — Clear explanation: both terms are negative because squares are positive, therefore f'(x) < 0, therefore decreasing
You must state WHY each term is negative (because the denominators are squared, hence positive). Simply saying "f'(x) is obviously negative" without justification loses this mark.

Paper 2 — June 2023 — Question 10 (7 marks)

$$f(x) = \frac{3kx + 18}{(x+4)(x-2)}$$

where \(k\) is a positive constant.

(a) Express \(f(x)\) in partial fractions in terms of \(k\). [3 marks]

(b) Hence find the exact value of \(k\) for which \(\displaystyle \int_{-3}^{1} f(x)\,dx = 21\) [4 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Set up the decomposition ▶
Two distinct linear factors \((x+4)\) and \((x-2)\), numerator degree 1, denominator degree 2, so this is a proper fraction. The constants will be in terms of \(k\) since the numerator contains \(k\). The decomposition form is standard.

Since we have two distinct linear factors:

$$\frac{3kx + 18}{(x+4)(x-2)} = \frac{A}{x+4} + \frac{B}{x-2}$$

Multiply both sides by \((x+4)(x-2)\):

$$3kx + 18 = A(x-2) + B(x+4)$$

B1 — Correct form for the partial fractions and sets up the identity
Part (a) Step 2: Find A and B in terms of k ▶
Substitute the "magic" values: \(x = 2\) zeros out the \(A\) term, and \(x = -4\) zeros out the \(B\) term. Since the numerator contains \(k\), our answers for \(A\) and \(B\) will be expressions in \(k\).

Substitute \(x = 2\):

$$3k(2) + 18 = A(0) + B(6)$$

$$6k + 18 = 6B$$

$$B = k + 3$$

Substitute \(x = -4\):

$$3k(-4) + 18 = A(-6) + B(0)$$

$$-12k + 18 = -6A$$

$$A = 2k - 3$$

M1 — Substituting appropriate values to find A or B in terms of k
Part (a) Step 3: Write the complete answer ▶

$$\frac{3kx + 18}{(x+4)(x-2)} = \frac{2k-3}{x+4} + \frac{k+3}{x-2}$$

A1 — Correct partial fractions (must be written as fractions, not just values)
One correct fraction only (e.g. just \(\frac{k+3}{x-2}\) correct with the other wrong) scores B0 M1 A0. Both must be correct for the A1.
Part (b) Step 4: Integrate the partial fractions ▶
"Hence" tells us to use Part (a). We integrate each partial fraction separately. Each has the form \(\frac{\text{constant}}{x + a}\), which integrates to \(\text{constant} \times \ln|x+a|\). The key skill is keeping the \(k\) parameters organised.

$$\int_{-3}^{1} f(x)\,dx = \int_{-3}^{1} \frac{2k-3}{x+4}\,dx + \int_{-3}^{1} \frac{k+3}{x-2}\,dx$$

Integrate each term:

$$= \Big[(2k-3)\ln|x+4| + (k+3)\ln|x-2|\Big]_{-3}^{1}$$

M1 — Integrating to get terms of the form \(\alpha\ln|x+4| + \beta\ln|x-2|\)
A1ft — Correct integration (following through their A and B in terms of k)
Part (b) Step 5: Apply limits and solve for k ▶
Substitute the upper limit \(x = 1\) and lower limit \(x = -3\), subtract, set equal to 21, and solve for \(k\). Be careful with the signs inside the absolute values — check whether each quantity is positive or negative in the integration range.

At \(x = 1\): \(\quad (2k-3)\ln|5| + (k+3)\ln|-1| = (2k-3)\ln 5 + 0\)

At \(x = -3\): \(\quad (2k-3)\ln|1| + (k+3)\ln|-5| = 0 + (k+3)\ln 5\)

Note: \(\ln|{-1}| = \ln 1 = 0\) and \(\ln|1| = 0\)

Subtract (upper minus lower):

$$(2k-3)\ln 5 - (k+3)\ln 5 = 21$$

Factor out \(\ln 5\):

$$[(2k-3) - (k+3)]\ln 5 = 21$$

$$(k - 6)\ln 5 = 21$$

Divide by \(\ln 5\):

$$k = 6 + \frac{21}{\ln 5} = \frac{6\ln 5 + 21}{\ln 5}$$

$$\boxed{k = \frac{21}{\ln 5} + 6}$$

dM1 — Full attempt: integrating, applying limits, setting = 21, solving for k
A1 — Correct exact answer: \(k = 6 + \frac{21}{\ln 5}\) or equivalent
The answer must be exact — a decimal approximation scores M1 A0. Accept any equivalent form such as \(\frac{21 + 6\ln 5}{\ln 5}\). The modulus signs in \(\ln|-5| = \ln 5\) and \(\ln|-1| = 0\) caught many candidates out.

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals in the question:

  • An algebraic fraction with the denominator already factorised into linear factors
  • The instruction "express in partial fractions" or an identity with \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) given
  • Part (b) often says "Hence integrate" or involves a differential equation requiring separation of variables
  • Almost always appears on Paper 2 (the methods paper)
  • If the fraction has \(\frac{\text{degree }n}{\text{degree }n}\) (equal degrees) or higher numerator degree, there will be a constant term in the decomposition
  • If there is a squared factor like \((x-1)^2\) in the denominator, expect a repeated factor decomposition

Three Types You Must Know

  • Distinct linear factors: \(\frac{px+q}{(ax+b)(cx+d)} = \frac{A}{ax+b} + \frac{B}{cx+d}\)
  • Repeated linear factor: \(\frac{px+q}{(ax+b)^2(cx+d)} = \frac{A}{ax+b} + \frac{B}{(ax+b)^2} + \frac{C}{cx+d}\)
  • Improper fraction: Divide first to get a polynomial, then decompose the remainder. Or, if the identity is given with \(A + \frac{B}{...} + \frac{C}{...}\), use that directly.
