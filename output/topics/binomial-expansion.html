The Approach

Edexcel tests binomial expansion in two main flavours:

Type 1: Fractional/Negative \(n\) (Year 2 — most common)

1
Rewrite in the form \((1 + bx)^n\)
If the expression is like \((a + bx)^n\), factor out \(a^n\) first: e.g. \((4-9x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 4^{\frac{1}{2}}\left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 2\left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}\). The expansion formula only works directly when the first term inside the bracket is 1.
2
Apply the generalised binomial expansion
$$(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \cdots$$ Replace \(x\) with your expression (e.g. \(-\frac{9x}{4}\)) and compute each term carefully.
3
Simplify and state validity
Collect terms and don't forget to multiply by the factor taken out in Step 1. State the validity condition: the expansion is valid when \(|bx| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < \frac{1}{|b|}\).

Type 2: Positive Integer \(n\) (Year 1)

1
Use \(\binom{n}{r}\) notation
$$(a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$$ For finding a specific coefficient, identify the term containing \(x^k\) and calculate its coefficient using \(\binom{n}{r}\).

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting the validity condition: When \(n\) is not a positive integer, the expansion is only valid for \(|x| < 1\) (or \(|bx| < 1\) if you expanded \((1+bx)^n\)). This is almost always tested and worth a mark.
  • Sign errors with negative \(n\): When \(n = -2\), the term \(n(n-1) = (-2)(-3) = +6\), not \(-6\). Double negatives catch many candidates.
  • Forgetting to factor out the constant: Expanding \((4-9x)^{1/2}\) directly without first writing it as \(2(1 - \frac{9x}{4})^{1/2}\) produces wrong answers. You MUST have 1 as the first term.
  • Bracket errors: When the \(x\)-term is something like \(-\frac{9x}{4}\), forgetting to square the entire expression (including the negative) when computing term 3 is extremely common.
  • Confusing number of terms: "First 4 terms" means up to and including the \(x^3\) term (since the first term is the constant 1).

What Top Candidates Do

  • Write out the general formula explicitly before substituting — this earns method marks even if arithmetic goes wrong later.
  • Keep each term on a separate line when expanding, showing the binomial coefficient, the power of the bracket term, and the simplification.
  • For validity: state \(|bx| < 1\) then immediately solve for \(|x| < \frac{1}{|b|}\). Write both forms.
  • When approximating surds, clearly show: (i) what value of \(x\) you're substituting, (ii) why it gives the surd (show both sides of the equation), and (iii) that \(x\) is within the valid range.

Paper 1 — October 2020 — Question 1 (5 marks)

(a) Find the first four terms, in ascending powers of \(x\), of the binomial expansion of

$$(1 + 8x)^{\frac{1}{2}}$$

giving each term in simplest form. [3 marks]

(b) Explain how you could use \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) in the expansion to find an approximation for \(\sqrt{5}\). There is no need to carry out the calculation. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Write down the binomial expansion formula ▶
We need to expand \((1+8x)^{1/2}\) which is already in the form \((1+bx)^n\) with \(b=8\) and \(n=\frac{1}{2}\). Since \(n\) is not a positive integer, we use the generalised binomial expansion. We need 4 terms (constant, \(x\), \(x^2\), \(x^3\)).

The generalised binomial expansion is:

$$(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \cdots$$

Here \(n = \frac{1}{2}\) and we replace \(x\) with \(8x\):

$$(1+8x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + \frac{1}{2}(8x) + \frac{\frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{2}-1)}{2!}(8x)^2 + \frac{\frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{2}-1)(\frac{1}{2}-2)}{3!}(8x)^3 + \cdots$$

M1 — Correct structure for the binomial expansion with \(n = \frac{1}{2}\), showing at least term 3 or term 4 with correct binomial coefficient
Part (a) Step 2: Calculate each term ▶
Now we compute each term carefully. The key is to evaluate \(n(n-1)\), \(n(n-1)(n-2)\) etc. with \(n = \frac{1}{2}\), and compute powers of \(8x\). The most common error is with signs: \(\frac{1}{2} - 1 = -\frac{1}{2}\) and \(\frac{1}{2} - 2 = -\frac{3}{2}\).

Term 1 (constant): \(1\)

Term 2 (\(x\) term): \(\frac{1}{2} \times 8x = 4x\)

Term 3 (\(x^2\) term): \(\frac{\frac{1}{2} \times (-\frac{1}{2})}{2} \times (8x)^2 = \frac{-\frac{1}{4}}{2} \times 64x^2 = -\frac{1}{8} \times 64x^2 = -8x^2\)

Term 4 (\(x^3\) term): \(\frac{\frac{1}{2} \times (-\frac{1}{2}) \times (-\frac{3}{2})}{6} \times (8x)^3 = \frac{\frac{3}{8}}{6} \times 512x^3 = \frac{1}{16} \times 512x^3 = 32x^3\)

$$\boxed{(1+8x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + 4x - 8x^2 + 32x^3 + \cdots}$$

A1 — Correct unsimplified expression
A1 — All four terms correctly simplified: \(1 + 4x - 8x^2 + 32x^3\)
Note the alternating signs from the third term onwards. When \(n = \frac{1}{2}\), term 3 is negative but term 4 is positive because \((-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{3}{2}) = +\frac{3}{4}\). Many candidates lose marks by getting the sign wrong on term 4.
Part (b) Step 3: Explain how to approximate \(\sqrt{5}\) ▶
We need to show that substituting \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) into our expansion gives \(\sqrt{5}\). The strategy: substitute into the LHS \((1+8x)^{1/2}\) and show it equals \(\sqrt{5}\). Then the RHS (our expansion) gives the approximation. We need to show both sides explicitly.

Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) into the left-hand side:

$$(1 + 8x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \left(1 + 8 \times \frac{1}{32}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \left(1 + \frac{1}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \left(\frac{5}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}$$

So substituting \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) into the expansion gives an approximation for \(\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\).

Therefore: multiply the result by 2 to obtain an approximation for \(\sqrt{5}\).

M1 — Substituting \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) into \((1+8x)^{1/2}\) to obtain \(\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\) or equivalent
A1 — Complete explanation: substitute into expansion and multiply result by 2
You must show the connection between both sides. Simply stating "substitute \(x = \frac{1}{32}\) into the expansion" without showing why this gives \(\sqrt{5}\) earns M0. The examiner needs to see the left-hand side evaluated to see \(\sqrt{5}\) appear.

Paper 2 — October 2020 — Question 4 (3 marks)

In the binomial expansion of

$$(a + 2x)^7 \quad \text{where } a \text{ is a constant}$$

the coefficient of \(x^4\) is 15120.

Find the value of \(a\). [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Step 1: Identify the general term in \((a+2x)^7\) ▶
This is a positive integer power expansion (Year 1 content). We need the term containing \(x^4\). In the expansion of \((a+b)^n\), the general term is \(\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r\). Here \(n=7\), the first term is \(a\), and the second term is \(2x\). We need the term where the power of \(2x\) is 4.

Using the binomial theorem, the general term of \((a + 2x)^7\) is:

$$\binom{7}{r} \cdot a^{7-r} \cdot (2x)^r$$

For the \(x^4\) term, we need \(r = 4\):

$$\binom{7}{4} \cdot a^{7-4} \cdot (2x)^4 = \binom{7}{4} \cdot a^3 \cdot 2^4 \cdot x^4$$

Step 2: Calculate the coefficient and set equal to 15120 ▶
Now we compute the numerical parts: \(\binom{7}{4} = 35\) and \(2^4 = 16\). The coefficient of \(x^4\) is everything multiplied together except the \(x^4\) itself. We set this equal to 15120 and solve for \(a\).

Calculate \(\binom{7}{4} = \frac{7!}{4! \cdot 3!} = \frac{7 \times 6 \times 5}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = 35\)

Calculate \(2^4 = 16\)

The coefficient of \(x^4\) is: \(35 \times a^3 \times 16 = 560a^3\)

M1 — Correct expression for the coefficient: \(\binom{7}{4} \cdot a^3 \cdot 2^4\) or equivalent (= \(560a^3\))
Step 3: Solve for \(a\) ▶
We have \(560a^3 = 15120\). Dividing and cube-rooting gives us \(a\).

$$560a^3 = 15120$$

Divide both sides by 560:

$$a^3 = \frac{15120}{560} = 27$$

Take the cube root:

$$\boxed{a = 3}$$

A1 — Correct equation \(560a^3 = 15120\) or equivalent
A1 — \(a = 3\)
This is a fairly short question (3 marks, early in the paper). The most common error is using the wrong binomial coefficient: candidates confuse \(\binom{7}{4}\) with \(\binom{7}{3}\) — but since \(\binom{7}{4} = \binom{7}{3} = 35\), this particular mistake doesn't actually cause a wrong answer here. However, forgetting to include \(2^4\) (the power of 2 from \((2x)^4\)) definitely does.

Paper 2 — June 2022 — Question 7 (5 marks)

(a) Find the first four terms, in ascending powers of \(x\), of the binomial expansion of

$$\sqrt{4 - 9x}$$

writing each term in simplest form. [4 marks]

(b) A student uses this expansion with \(x = \frac{1}{9}\) to find an approximation for \(\sqrt{3}\). Using the answer to part (a) and without doing any calculations, state whether this approximation will be an overestimate or an underestimate of \(\sqrt{3}\), giving a brief reason for your answer. [1 mark]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Factor out a constant to get the form \((1 + bx)^n\) ▶
We cannot expand \(\sqrt{4-9x}\) directly because the formula requires the first term inside the bracket to be 1. We need to factor out the constant first. Since \(\sqrt{4-9x} = (4-9x)^{1/2}\), we factor out \(4^{1/2} = 2\).

Rewrite the expression:

$$\sqrt{4-9x} = (4-9x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = \left[4\left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)\right]^{\frac{1}{2}}$$

Apply the power to both factors:

$$= 4^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 2\left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$$

B1 — Taking out a factor of \(\sqrt{4} = 2\) and writing \(2(1 - \frac{9x}{4})^{1/2}\)
Part (a) Step 2: Expand \(\left(1 - \frac{9x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}\) using the formula ▶
Now we apply the generalised binomial expansion with \(n = \frac{1}{2}\) and replacing \(x\) with \(-\frac{9x}{4}\). Be very careful with the negative sign — when we square it, it becomes positive; when we cube it, it stays negative.

Using \((1+u)^n = 1 + nu + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}u^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}u^3 + \cdots\) with \(u = -\frac{9x}{4}\) and \(n = \frac{1}{2}\):

Term 1: \(1\)

Term 2: \(\frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{9x}{4}\right) = -\frac{9x}{8}\)

Term 3: \(\frac{\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2})}{2}\left(-\frac{9x}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{-\frac{1}{4}}{2} \times \frac{81x^2}{16} = -\frac{1}{8} \times \frac{81x^2}{16} = -\frac{81x^2}{128}\)

Term 4: \(\frac{\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{3}{2})}{6}\left(-\frac{9x}{4}\right)^3 = \frac{\frac{3}{8}}{6} \times \left(-\frac{729x^3}{64}\right) = \frac{1}{16} \times \left(-\frac{729x^3}{64}\right) = -\frac{729x^3}{1024}\)

M1 — Correct structure for term 3 or term 4 with correct binomial coefficient
A1 — Correct unsimplified expression for the expansion of \((1 - \frac{9x}{4})^{1/2}\)
Part (a) Step 3: Multiply by 2 and write the final answer ▶
Don't forget the factor of 2 we took out at the start. Multiply every term by 2 to get the final expansion of \(\sqrt{4-9x}\).

$$\sqrt{4-9x} = 2\left(1 - \frac{9x}{8} - \frac{81x^2}{128} - \frac{729x^3}{1024} - \cdots\right)$$

Distribute the 2:

$$= 2 - \frac{9x}{4} - \frac{81x^2}{64} - \frac{729x^3}{512} - \cdots$$

$$\boxed{\sqrt{4-9x} = 2 - \frac{9x}{4} - \frac{81x^2}{64} - \frac{729x^3}{512} + \cdots}$$

A1 — Correct final simplified expansion with all four terms
All terms are negative after the constant (since \(n = \frac{1}{2}\) and the bracket contains a minus sign). This observation is key for Part (b).
Part (b) Step 4: Overestimate or underestimate? ▶
The student uses only the first 4 terms. But the full (infinite) expansion has more terms beyond these. If we can determine the sign of those missing terms, we know whether the truncated approximation is too big or too small. Looking at our expansion: every term after the first is negative (since \(x > 0\)).

Note: when \(x = \frac{1}{9}\), \(\sqrt{4 - 9 \times \frac{1}{9}} = \sqrt{4-1} = \sqrt{3}\).

Looking at the expansion, all terms after the constant 2 are negative (since \(x > 0\)).

The expansion is infinite, and the student is using only the first four terms. The remaining (omitted) terms are also all negative.

The approximation is an overestimate, because the missing negative terms would make the true value smaller.

B1 — States "overestimate" with reason: all omitted terms are negative (so the true value is less than the approximation)
The key reasoning is: the omitted terms are negative, so adding them would decrease the sum. Therefore the truncated expansion (without them) is larger than the true value — an overestimate.

Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 11 (10 marks)

(a) Use binomial expansions to show that

$$\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}} \approx 1 + \frac{5}{2}x - \frac{5}{8}x^2$$

[6 marks]

(b) A student substitutes \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) into both sides of the approximation shown in part (a) in an attempt to find an approximation to \(\sqrt{6}\). Give a reason why the student should not use \(x = \frac{1}{2}\). [1 mark]

(c) Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{11}\) into \(\displaystyle\frac{1+4x}{1-x}\) to obtain an approximation to \(\sqrt{6}\). Give your answer as a fraction. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Rewrite as a product of two binomial expressions ▶
The expression \(\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}}\) involves a fraction under a square root. We can split it into a product of two separate binomial expressions, each of which we can expand individually. Using index laws: \(\sqrt{\frac{a}{b}} = a^{1/2} \times b^{-1/2}\).

Rewrite the expression using index laws:

$$\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}} = (1+4x)^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot (1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$$

We will expand each factor separately (up to the \(x^2\) term), then multiply the results.

B1 — Key step of writing the expression as \((1+4x)^{1/2}(1-x)^{-1/2}\) or equivalent setup for binomial expansion
Part (a) Step 2: Expand \((1+4x)^{\frac{1}{2}}\) up to \(x^2\) ▶
Apply the binomial formula with \(n = \frac{1}{2}\) replacing \(x\) with \(4x\). We only need terms up to \(x^2\) since the target answer is a quadratic.

$$(1+4x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + \frac{1}{2}(4x) + \frac{\frac{1}{2}(-\frac{1}{2})}{2!}(4x)^2 + \cdots$$

Term 2: \(\frac{1}{2} \times 4x = 2x\)

Term 3: \(\frac{-\frac{1}{4}}{2} \times 16x^2 = -\frac{1}{8} \times 16x^2 = -2x^2\)

$$(1+4x)^{\frac{1}{2}} \approx 1 + 2x - 2x^2$$

M1 — Correct attempt at the binomial expansion of \((1+4x)^{1/2}\)
Part (a) Step 3: Expand \((1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) up to \(x^2\) ▶
Now expand the second factor with \(n = -\frac{1}{2}\) replacing \(x\) with \(-x\). Be careful: \(n(n-1) = (-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{3}{2}) = +\frac{3}{4}\), and \((-x)^2 = x^2\) (positive!).

$$(1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}} = 1 + \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)(-x) + \frac{(-\frac{1}{2})(-\frac{3}{2})}{2!}(-x)^2 + \cdots$$

Term 2: \(-\frac{1}{2} \times (-x) = \frac{x}{2}\)

Term 3: \(\frac{+\frac{3}{4}}{2} \times x^2 = \frac{3}{8}x^2\)

$$(1-x)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{3}{8}x^2$$

M1 — Correct attempt at the binomial expansion of \((1-x)^{-1/2}\)
A1 — Both expansions correct and simplified
Part (a) Step 4: Multiply the two expansions together ▶
We need to multiply \((1 + 2x - 2x^2)\) by \((1 + \frac{1}{2}x + \frac{3}{8}x^2)\). Since we only need terms up to \(x^2\), we can ignore any products that would give \(x^3\) or higher. Systematically: collect the constant term, then all terms contributing to \(x\), then all terms contributing to \(x^2\).

Constant term: \(1 \times 1 = 1\)

Coefficient of \(x\): \(1 \times \frac{1}{2} + 2 \times 1 = \frac{1}{2} + 2 = \frac{5}{2}\)

Coefficient of \(x^2\): \(1 \times \frac{3}{8} + 2 \times \frac{1}{2} + (-2) \times 1 = \frac{3}{8} + 1 - 2 = \frac{3}{8} - 1 = -\frac{5}{8}\)

$$\boxed{\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}} \approx 1 + \frac{5}{2}x - \frac{5}{8}x^2} \quad \square$$

dM1 — Multiplying the two expansions and collecting terms up to \(x^2\)
A1* — Reaching the given answer with complete, rigorous working
Since this is a "show that" question, every step must be visible. The examiner needs to see both separate expansions AND the multiplication. Jumping from the two expansions directly to the final answer loses marks.
Part (b) Step 5: Why \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) should not be used ▶
The expansion of \((1+4x)^{1/2}\) is valid for \(|4x| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\). The expansion of \((1-x)^{-1/2}\) is valid for \(|{-x}| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < 1\). For the combined expansion to be valid, BOTH conditions must hold, so we need \(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\). Since \(\frac{1}{2} > \frac{1}{4}\), the expansion is not valid at \(x = \frac{1}{2}\).

The expansion of \((1+4x)^{1/2}\) is only valid when \(|4x| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\).

Since \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) does not satisfy \(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\), the expansion is not valid for this value of \(x\).

B1 — States that the expansion is not valid for \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) because it exceeds the range \(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\) (or \(|4x| < 1\))
Simply stating "\(\frac{1}{2}\) is too big" without reference to the validity condition (\(|x| < \frac{1}{4}\) or \(|4x| < 1\)) does NOT earn this mark. You must connect it to the convergence condition of the binomial expansion.
Part (c) Step 6: Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{11}\) to approximate \(\sqrt{6}\) ▶
First check: when \(x = \frac{1}{11}\), what does the LHS become? \(\frac{1+4(\frac{1}{11})}{1-\frac{1}{11}} = \frac{\frac{15}{11}}{\frac{10}{11}} = \frac{15}{10} = \frac{3}{2}\). So \(\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}\). We need to connect this to \(\sqrt{6}\). Now substitute into the RHS expansion.

First verify: with \(x = \frac{1}{11}\):

$$\frac{1 + 4 \times \frac{1}{11}}{1 - \frac{1}{11}} = \frac{\frac{15}{11}}{\frac{10}{11}} = \frac{15}{10} = \frac{3}{2}$$

So \(\sqrt{\frac{1+4x}{1-x}} = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}\)

Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{11}\) into the expansion:

$$\frac{\sqrt{6}}{2} \approx 1 + \frac{5}{2} \times \frac{1}{11} - \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{1}{121}$$

$$= 1 + \frac{5}{22} - \frac{5}{968}$$

Find common denominator (968 = 8 \times 121):

$$= \frac{968}{968} + \frac{220}{968} - \frac{5}{968} = \frac{1183}{968}$$

Therefore:

$$\sqrt{6} \approx 2 \times \frac{1183}{968} = \boxed{\frac{1183}{484}}$$

M1 — Substituting \(x = \frac{1}{11}\) into both sides and connecting to \(\sqrt{6}\)
A1 — Correct numerical evaluation (finding both sides as fractions)
A1 — Final answer \(\frac{1183}{484}\) (or equivalent such as \(\frac{2366}{968}\))
Note that \(\frac{1}{11} < \frac{1}{4}\) so this value of \(x\) IS within the valid range — unlike the \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) from Part (b). The approximation gives \(\sqrt{6} \approx 2.4441\ldots\) compared to the true value \(\sqrt{6} = 2.4495\ldots\) — quite accurate!

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals in the question:

  • "Find the first [3/4] terms, in ascending powers of \(x\)" — direct expansion question
  • "The coefficient of \(x^k\) is..." — positive integer \(n\) coefficient question (Year 1)
  • A surd or fractional power like \(\sqrt{1+3x}\) or \((2+x)^{-3}\) — generalised expansion (Year 2)
  • "State the range of values of \(x\) for which the expansion is valid" — validity condition
  • "Use your expansion to find an approximation" — substitute a value of \(x\)
  • Usually Q1–Q4 on Paper 1 or Paper 2 (early in paper), worth 3–6 marks. Harder combined versions (like June 2018 Q11) can appear later for 7–10 marks.

Key Formulae to Memorise

Generalised Binomial Expansion (for any \(n\)) Learn this

$$(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \cdots$$

Valid for \(|x| < 1\). When \(n\) is a positive integer, the series terminates. Otherwise, it is infinite.

Positive Integer Expansion In formula booklet

$$(a+b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r \quad \text{where} \quad \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$

Validity Conditions Learn this

  • \((1+bx)^n\) is valid for \(|bx| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < \frac{1}{|b|}\)
  • \((a+bx)^n = a^n(1 + \frac{b}{a}x)^n\) is valid for \(\left|\frac{b}{a}x\right| < 1\), i.e. \(|x| < \left|\frac{a}{b}\right|\)
  • When combining two expansions, the stricter (smaller) validity range applies
