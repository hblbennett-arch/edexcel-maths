Guided Approach

The standard 3-part structure for Edexcel optimisation questions:

1
Set up the expression (Part a — "Show that")
Identify the quantity to optimise (surface area, cost, length). Use the constraint (e.g. fixed volume) to eliminate one variable. Substitute into the target formula to get it in terms of one variable only.
2
Differentiate and solve (Part b — "Use calculus to find...")
Differentiate the expression from Part (a). Set \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\). Solve for the variable. This gives the critical value where the optimum occurs.
3
Verify the nature (Part c — "Prove it is a minimum/maximum")
Find \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2}\). Substitute your critical value. If \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} > 0\) then minimum. If \(< 0\) then maximum. Write a clear conclusion statement.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Forgetting to verify nature: Many candidates find the stationary point but don't prove it's a minimum/maximum. The second derivative test is essential for full marks.
  • Wrong formula for the constraint: Using \(V = \pi r^2 h\) for a shape that isn't a simple cylinder (e.g. hemisphere, cone, sector). Always check which volume formula applies.
  • Not rearranging before substituting: You must isolate \(h\) (or the second variable) from the constraint equation before substituting into the surface area formula.
  • Losing marks on "Show that": If Part (a) says "show that \(S = ...\)", you must show every algebraic step. Jumping from the substitution to the final answer loses marks.
  • Ignoring units or context: Final answers should be in the correct units and, where asked, rounded appropriately. State whether it's a minimum or maximum explicitly.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Label each step clearly: "Volume constraint:", "Surface area:", "Substituting h = ..."
  • Write \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) explicitly before solving — this earns a method mark even if algebra goes wrong.
  • For the second derivative test: state the value, state its sign, then write a conclusion: "Since \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} > 0\), this gives a minimum surface area."

Paper 2 — June 2019 — Question 13 (10 marks)

A manufacturer produces a storage tank modelled as a hollow circular cylinder closed at one end with a hemispherical shell at the other end. The cylinder has radius \(r\) metres and height \(h\) metres. The hemisphere has radius \(r\) metres. The volume of the tank is 6 m³.

(a) Show that the surface area of the tank, in m², is given by \(\displaystyle S = \frac{12}{r} + \frac{5\pi r^2}{3}\) [4 marks]

(b) Use calculus to find the radius of the tank for which the surface area is a minimum. [4 marks]

(c) Calculate the minimum surface area of the tank, giving your answer to the nearest integer. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Write down the volume constraint ▶
Part (a) is a "show that" — we're given the target answer \(S = \frac{12}{r} + \frac{5\pi r^2}{3}\). This is in terms of \(r\) only, but our shape has two unknowns (\(r\) and \(h\)). Strategy: use the volume constraint (which links \(r\) and \(h\)) to eliminate \(h\), then write the surface area purely in terms of \(r\). We start by writing down the constraint.

The tank consists of a cylinder (volume \(\pi r^2 h\)) and a hemisphere (volume \(\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3\)).

We're told the total volume = 6, so:

$$\pi r^2 h + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = 6$$

B1 — Writing down the correct volume expression
Part (a) Step 2: Rearrange the constraint to isolate \(h\) ▶
We have two variables in our volume equation (\(r\) and \(h\)), but the target formula for \(S\) is only in terms of \(r\). So we need to express \(h\) in terms of \(r\), then later substitute it into the surface area. Rearranging the constraint for \(h\) gives us the substitution we'll need.

We need to get \(h\) on its own so we can substitute it into the surface area formula later.

Starting with: \(\pi r^2 h + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = 6\)

Subtract \(\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3\) from both sides:

$$\pi r^2 h = 6 - \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3$$

Divide both sides by \(\pi r^2\):

$$h = \frac{6 - \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3}{\pi r^2}$$

Split the fraction into two separate terms:

$$h = \frac{6}{\pi r^2} - \frac{\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3}{\pi r^2}$$

Simplify the second term (cancel \(\pi\) and \(r^2\)):

$$h = \frac{6}{\pi r^2} - \frac{2r}{3}$$

Part (a) Step 3: Write the surface area formula ▶
Now we need an expression for the thing we're optimising — the surface area. We need to account for every face of the solid. The tank is: a flat circular base + a curved cylinder wall + a curved hemisphere. Carefully listing each face avoids the common mistake of forgetting one.

Identify all the faces of the tank:

  • Flat circular base (closed end): \(\pi r^2\)
  • Curved surface of the cylinder: \(2\pi r h\)
  • Curved surface of the hemisphere: \(2\pi r^2\)

Total surface area:

$$S = \pi r^2 + 2\pi r h + 2\pi r^2 = 3\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$$

M1 — Writing a surface area of the form \(A\pi r^2 + B\pi rh\) where \(A, B > 0\)
Part (a) Step 4: Substitute \(h\) into the surface area to get \(S\) in terms of \(r\) only ▶
We now have \(S\) in terms of \(r\) and \(h\), and \(h\) in terms of \(r\). Substituting eliminates \(h\) entirely, giving us \(S\) as a function of \(r\) alone — which is what the question asks us to show. Since it's a "show that", we must expand and simplify with every step visible.

Substitute \(h = \frac{6}{\pi r^2} - \frac{2r}{3}\) into \(S = 3\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h\):

$$S = 3\pi r^2 + 2\pi r \left(\frac{6}{\pi r^2} - \frac{2r}{3}\right)$$

Expand the bracket — multiply \(2\pi r\) by each term inside:

$$S = 3\pi r^2 + \frac{2\pi r \cdot 6}{\pi r^2} - \frac{2\pi r \cdot 2r}{3}$$

Simplify each term: cancel \(\pi\) and one \(r\) in the middle term:

$$S = 3\pi r^2 + \frac{12}{r} - \frac{4\pi r^2}{3}$$

Combine the \(\pi r^2\) terms (common denominator of 3):

$$S = \frac{9\pi r^2}{3} - \frac{4\pi r^2}{3} + \frac{12}{r}$$

Simplify: \(\frac{9\pi r^2 - 4\pi r^2}{3} = \frac{5\pi r^2}{3}\)

$$\boxed{S = \frac{12}{r} + \frac{5\pi r^2}{3}} \quad \square$$

A1 — Correctly simplified un-simplified expression
A1* — Reaching the given answer with rigorous, clear working
Many candidates lost the final A1* by jumping steps. Since this is a "show that" question, you must show every line. The examiner needs to see you combine \(3\pi r^2 - \frac{4\pi r^2}{3}\) explicitly.
Part (b) Step 5: Differentiate \(S\) with respect to \(r\) ▶
Part (b) says "use calculus to find the radius for minimum surface area". The word "calculus" is a strong hint: differentiate, set equal to zero, solve. To find a minimum, we first need the derivative \(\frac{dS}{dr}\). We'll rewrite terms like \(\frac{12}{r}\) using negative powers so the power rule applies cleanly.

Rewrite \(S\) using index notation to make differentiating easier:

$$S = 12r^{-1} + \frac{5\pi}{3}r^2$$

Differentiate term by term using the power rule \(\frac{d}{dr}(r^n) = nr^{n-1}\):

First term: \(\frac{d}{dr}(12r^{-1}) = 12 \times (-1) \times r^{-2} = -12r^{-2}\)

Second term: \(\frac{d}{dr}\left(\frac{5\pi}{3}r^2\right) = \frac{5\pi}{3} \times 2 \times r^{1} = \frac{10\pi r}{3}\)

$$\frac{dS}{dr} = -12r^{-2} + \frac{10\pi r}{3} = -\frac{12}{r^2} + \frac{10\pi r}{3}$$

M1 — Differentiating to get expression of the form \(-Ar^{-2} + Br\)
A1 — Correct derivative: \(\frac{dS}{dr} = -\frac{12}{r^2} + \frac{10\pi r}{3}\)
Part (b) Step 6: Set \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) and solve for \(r\) ▶
At a minimum (or maximum), the gradient is zero — the curve is flat. So setting \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) finds the critical point. We then solve the resulting equation for \(r\). The algebra involves getting \(r^3\) on one side, since the equation has \(r^{-2}\) and \(r^1\) terms — multiplying through by \(r^2\) clears the fraction.

$$-\frac{12}{r^2} + \frac{10\pi r}{3} = 0$$

Add \(\frac{12}{r^2}\) to both sides:

$$\frac{10\pi r}{3} = \frac{12}{r^2}$$

Multiply both sides by \(r^2\):

$$\frac{10\pi r^3}{3} = 12$$

Multiply both sides by 3:

$$10\pi r^3 = 36$$

Divide both sides by \(10\pi\):

$$r^3 = \frac{36}{10\pi} = \frac{18}{5\pi}$$

Take the cube root:

$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{18}{5\pi}} \approx 1.05 \text{ m (3 s.f.)}$$

M1 — Setting \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) and rearranging to give \(r^3 = k\)
A1 — Correct exact answer \(r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{18}{5\pi}}\) or equivalent
Candidates who wrote \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) explicitly earned the method mark even when subsequent algebra was flawed. Always show this step. An answer of \(r \approx 1.05\) from no working earns M0 A0.
Part (c) Step 7: Calculate the minimum surface area ▶
Part (c) asks for the actual minimum surface area value. We've found the \(r\) that gives the minimum — now we just substitute it back into the formula from Part (a) to compute the surface area at that radius. Note: this part doesn't ask us to prove it's a minimum (that would need the second derivative), it just asks for the numerical value.

Substitute \(r = 1.0464...\) back into the surface area formula:

$$S = \frac{12}{r} + \frac{5\pi r^2}{3}$$

$$S = \frac{12}{1.0464...} + \frac{5\pi (1.0464...)^2}{3}$$

$$S = 11.466... + 5.733...$$

$$S = 17.2... \approx \boxed{17 \text{ m}^2}$$

M1 — Substituting their value of \(r\) into the surface area formula
A1 — Correct answer: 17 m² (to nearest integer)
Note: the question asks "to the nearest integer", so you must write 17, not 17.2. Also note this part does not ask you to prove it's a minimum — that proof would normally be in a separate part.

Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 15 (10 marks)

A company makes a solid toy that looks like a piece of cheese. Face ABC is a sector of a circle with radius \(r\) cm and centre A, angle BAC = 0.8 radians. The prism has height \(h\) cm. The volume of the toy is 240 cm³.

(a) Show that the surface area of the toy, \(S\) cm², is given by \(\displaystyle S = 0.8r^2 + \frac{1680}{r}\), making your method clear. [4 marks]

(b) Using algebraic differentiation, find the value of \(r\) for which \(S\) has a stationary point. [4 marks]

(c) Prove, by further differentiation, that this value of \(r\) gives the minimum surface area of the toy. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Volume constraint — find \(h\) in terms of \(r\) ▶
Same strategy as the tank question: the target formula \(S = 0.8r^2 + \frac{1680}{r}\) is in terms of \(r\) only, but the shape has two dimensions (\(r\) and \(h\)). We need to eliminate \(h\) using the volume constraint. Step 1: find the volume of this prism (which is cross-sectional area × height), set it equal to 240, and isolate \(h\).

The cross-section is a sector with angle 0.8 radians and radius \(r\).

Area of a sector = \(\frac{1}{2}r^2\theta\):

$$\text{Area of sector} = \frac{1}{2}r^2(0.8) = 0.4r^2$$

Volume of prism = area of cross-section × length:

$$V = 0.4r^2 \times h = 240$$

Divide both sides by \(0.4r^2\):

$$h = \frac{240}{0.4r^2} = \frac{600}{r^2}$$

Part (a) Step 2: Identify all faces of the toy and write the surface area ▶
Now we write the total surface area. The prism has 5 faces: 2 sector-shaped ends and 3 rectangular sides (two straight, one curved). The tricky part is the curved face — its width is the arc length \(r\theta\), not \(r\). Drawing a net helps you see all faces clearly and avoid missing any.

The toy has 5 faces. Carefully list each one:

  • 2 sector faces (front ABC and back DEF): \(2 \times 0.4r^2 = 0.8r^2\)
  • 2 flat rectangular sides (along the straight edges AD and CF): \(2 \times r \times h = 2rh\)
  • 1 curved rectangular face (the arc BC extended to EF): arc length × height = \(r\theta \times h = 0.8rh\)

$$S = 0.8r^2 + 2rh + 0.8rh = 0.8r^2 + 2.8rh$$

M1 — Writing a surface area expression that includes sector(s) and rectangles with substitution attempted
A common error was forgetting the curved face (the arc side) or counting only one sector face. Drawing a net of the solid helps enormously here.
Part (a) Step 3: Substitute \(h = \frac{600}{r^2}\) to eliminate \(h\) ▶
We now have \(S\) in terms of both \(r\) and \(h\), and from Step 1 we have \(h = \frac{600}{r^2}\). Substituting removes \(h\), leaving \(S\) in terms of \(r\) only — which is what the question requires. Since it's "show that", we must simplify carefully and show every line.

$$S = 0.8r^2 + 2.8rh$$

Replace \(h\) with \(\frac{600}{r^2}\):

$$S = 0.8r^2 + 2.8r \times \frac{600}{r^2}$$

Simplify: \(r \times \frac{600}{r^2} = \frac{600}{r}\):

$$S = 0.8r^2 + \frac{2.8 \times 600}{r}$$

Calculate \(2.8 \times 600 = 1680\):

$$\boxed{S = 0.8r^2 + \frac{1680}{r}} \quad \square$$

A1 — Correct un-simplified expression after substitution
A1* — Reaching the given answer with clear, complete working
Part (b) Step 4: Differentiate \(S\) with respect to \(r\) ▶
Part (b) says "using algebraic differentiation, find the value of \(r\) for which \(S\) has a stationary point." The approach: differentiate, set to zero, solve. First we rewrite \(\frac{1680}{r}\) as \(1680r^{-1}\) so we can apply the power rule directly.

Rewrite using index notation:

$$S = 0.8r^2 + 1680r^{-1}$$

Differentiate term by term:

First term: \(\frac{d}{dr}(0.8r^2) = 0.8 \times 2 \times r^{1} = 1.6r\)

Second term: \(\frac{d}{dr}(1680r^{-1}) = 1680 \times (-1) \times r^{-2} = -1680r^{-2}\)

$$\frac{dS}{dr} = 1.6r - \frac{1680}{r^2}$$

M1 — Differentiating to get expression of the form \(Ar - Br^{-2}\)
A1 — Correct derivative
Part (b) Step 5: Set \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) and solve for \(r\) ▶
Stationary points occur where the gradient is zero. Setting \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 0\) gives an equation with \(r\) and \(r^{-2}\) terms. Multiplying through by \(r^2\) clears the fraction and gives us \(r^3 = \text{number}\), which we solve by cube-rooting.

$$1.6r - \frac{1680}{r^2} = 0$$

Add \(\frac{1680}{r^2}\) to both sides:

$$1.6r = \frac{1680}{r^2}$$

Multiply both sides by \(r^2\):

$$1.6r^3 = 1680$$

Divide both sides by 1.6:

$$r^3 = \frac{1680}{1.6} = 1050$$

Take the cube root:

$$r = \sqrt[3]{1050} \approx 10.2 \text{ cm}$$

dM1 — Setting derivative = 0 and rearranging to \(r^3 = k\)
A1 — \(r \approx 10.2\) (or exact: \(r = \sqrt[3]{1050}\))
Part (c) Step 6: Second derivative test — prove it is a minimum ▶
Part (c) says "prove, by further differentiation, that this is a minimum." The phrase "further differentiation" tells us to use the second derivative test. If \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} > 0\) at the stationary point, the curve is concave up there, confirming a minimum. We need three things for full marks: (1) the numerical value, (2) state its sign, (3) state the conclusion "minimum".

Differentiate \(\frac{dS}{dr} = 1.6r - 1680r^{-2}\) again:

First term: \(\frac{d}{dr}(1.6r) = 1.6\)

Second term: \(\frac{d}{dr}(-1680r^{-2}) = -1680 \times (-2) \times r^{-3} = \frac{3360}{r^3}\)

$$\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} = 1.6 + \frac{3360}{r^3}$$

Substitute \(r = \sqrt[3]{1050} \approx 10.2\):

$$\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} = 1.6 + \frac{3360}{1050} = 1.6 + 3.2 = 4.8$$

Since \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2} = 4.8 > 0\), this confirms the stationary point is a minimum.

Therefore \(r = \sqrt[3]{1050}\) gives the minimum surface area of the toy. \(\square\)

M1 — Finding \(\frac{d^2S}{dr^2}\) and substituting their value of \(r\)
A1 — Correct value, correct sign identified, and explicit conclusion stating "minimum"
You must: (1) state the numerical value of the second derivative, (2) state it is positive, and (3) conclude "therefore minimum". Missing any of these three elements loses the A1 mark.

Paper 1 — June 2018 — Question 2 (7 marks)

A curve \(C\) has equation \(y = x^2 - 2x - 24\sqrt{x}, \quad x > 0\)

(a) Find (i) \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)    (ii) \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) [3 marks]

(b) Verify that \(C\) has a stationary point when \(x = 4\) [2 marks]

(c) Determine the nature of this stationary point, giving a reason for your answer. [2 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a)(i) Step 1: Find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) ▶
Part (a) is pure differentiation — no constraints or geometry here. The \(\sqrt{x}\) term can't be differentiated directly in that form, so we rewrite it as \(x^{1/2}\) to use the power rule. This question gives us the tools (derivatives) that parts (b) and (c) will use.

First, rewrite \(\sqrt{x}\) as \(x^{\frac{1}{2}}\) so we can use the power rule:

$$y = x^2 - 2x - 24x^{\frac{1}{2}}$$

Differentiate each term using \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\):

First term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x\)

Second term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(-2x) = -2\)

Third term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(-24x^{\frac{1}{2}}) = -24 \times \frac{1}{2} \times x^{-\frac{1}{2}} = -12x^{-\frac{1}{2}}\)

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x - 2 - 12x^{-\frac{1}{2}}$$

Or equivalently: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x - 2 - \frac{12}{\sqrt{x}}\)

M1 — Differentiating to get \(Ax + B + Cx^{-\frac{1}{2}}\)
A1 — Correct: \(2x - 2 - 12x^{-\frac{1}{2}}\)
Part (a)(ii) Step 2: Find \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) ▶
The second derivative tells us about the nature (minimum/maximum) of stationary points — we'll need it in Part (c). Differentiating our answer from (a)(i) one more time is straightforward: apply the power rule again to each term.

Differentiate \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x - 2 - 12x^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) again:

First term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(2x) = 2\)

Second term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(-2) = 0\)

Third term: \(\frac{d}{dx}(-12x^{-\frac{1}{2}}) = -12 \times (-\frac{1}{2}) \times x^{-\frac{3}{2}} = 6x^{-\frac{3}{2}}\)

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2 + 6x^{-\frac{3}{2}}$$

B1ft — Correct second derivative (follow-through from their \(\frac{dy}{dx}\))
Part (b) Step 3: Verify stationary point at \(x = 4\) ▶
"Verify" means the question is telling us the answer (\(x=4\)) and asking us to prove it's correct. At a stationary point, \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\). So we substitute \(x=4\) into our derivative and show the result is indeed zero. This is not "solve \(\frac{dy}{dx}=0\)" — we just need to confirm the given value works.

A stationary point occurs where \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\). Substitute \(x = 4\):

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{x=4} = 2(4) - 2 - 12(4)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$$

Calculate each term: \(2(4) = 8\), and \((4)^{-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4}} = \frac{1}{2}\)

$$= 8 - 2 - 12 \times \frac{1}{2}$$

$$= 8 - 2 - 6 = 0 \checkmark$$

Since \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 0\) when \(x = 4\), there is a stationary point at \(x = 4\).

M1 — Substituting \(x = 4\) into \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) and evaluating
A1 — Showing it equals 0 AND stating conclusion "hence stationary point"
You must explicitly state the conclusion — just showing the calculation equals 0 without writing "therefore stationary point" or equivalent loses the A1.
Part (c) Step 4: Determine the nature of the stationary point ▶
"Determine the nature" means: is it a minimum or a maximum? We use the second derivative test — substitute \(x=4\) into \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) from Part (a)(ii). Positive means minimum (curve bends upward), negative means maximum (curve bends downward). We must state all three: the value, its sign, and the conclusion.

Substitute \(x = 4\) into the second derivative:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\bigg|_{x=4} = 2 + 6(4)^{-\frac{3}{2}}$$

Calculate \((4)^{-\frac{3}{2}} = \frac{1}{4^{3/2}} = \frac{1}{(\sqrt{4})^3} = \frac{1}{8}\):

$$= 2 + 6 \times \frac{1}{8} = 2 + \frac{3}{4} = 2.75$$

Since \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2.75 > 0\), the stationary point is a minimum.

M1 — Substituting \(x = 4\) into \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\) and calculating its value
A1 — Correct value (2.75), correct sign (\(> 0\)), and correct conclusion ("minimum")
All three elements are required: the numerical value, whether it's positive/negative, and the word "minimum" (or "maximum" if negative). References to "convex" are ignored by examiners.

Paper 1 — June 2022 — Question 8 (8 marks)

A car stops at two sets of traffic lights. The speed of the car, \(v\) ms\(^{-1}\), as it travels between the lights is modelled by:

$$v = (10 - 0.4t)\ln(t + 1), \quad 0 \leq t \leq T$$

where \(t\) seconds is the time after the car leaves the first set of lights.

(a) Find the value of \(T\). [1 mark]

(b) Show that the maximum speed of the car occurs when \(\displaystyle t = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(t+1)} - 1\) [4 marks]

(c) Using the iteration formula \(\displaystyle t_{n+1} = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(t_n+1)} - 1\) with \(t_1 = 7\), find the time taken for the car to reach maximum speed. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Find \(T\) (when the car stops at the second lights) ▶
\(T\) is when the car stops at the second set of lights, so \(v = 0\). The speed formula is a product: \((10-0.4t) \times \ln(t+1)\). A product equals zero when either factor is zero. Since \(\ln(t+1) = 0\) only when \(t=0\) (the starting point), the relevant factor is \(10-0.4t = 0\).

The car stops when \(v = 0\). Since \(\ln(t+1) \neq 0\) for \(t > 0\), we need:

$$10 - 0.4t = 0$$

Add \(0.4t\) to both sides:

$$10 = 0.4t$$

Divide by 0.4:

$$t = 25$$

$$\boxed{T = 25}$$

B1 — \(T = 25\)
Part (b) Step 2: Differentiate \(v\) using the product rule ▶
Part (b) is a "show that" for when maximum speed occurs. Maximum speed means \(\frac{dv}{dt} = 0\). Since \(v\) is a product of two functions (\((10-0.4t)\) and \(\ln(t+1)\)), we need the product rule to differentiate it. Then we'll set the result equal to zero and rearrange to match the given form.

\(v = (10 - 0.4t) \cdot \ln(t+1)\) is a product of two functions.

Let \(u = 10 - 0.4t\) and \(w = \ln(t+1)\):

\(\frac{du}{dt} = -0.4\)

\(\frac{dw}{dt} = \frac{1}{t+1}\)

Apply the product rule \(\frac{dv}{dt} = u\frac{dw}{dt} + w\frac{du}{dt}\):

$$\frac{dv}{dt} = (10 - 0.4t) \cdot \frac{1}{t+1} + \ln(t+1) \cdot (-0.4)$$

$$\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{10 - 0.4t}{t+1} - 0.4\ln(t+1)$$

M1 — Applying product rule correctly
A1 — Correct derivative
Part (b) Step 3: Set \(\frac{dv}{dt} = 0\) and rearrange to the given form ▶
This is the trickiest part — we need to rearrange \(\frac{dv}{dt} = 0\) into the form \(t = \frac{26}{1+\ln(t+1)} - 1\). The target has \(t\) on both sides, which tells us it can't be solved algebraically (hence Part (c) uses iteration). Our goal is just to manipulate the equation into the given form. Strategy: isolate terms, factor out \((t+1)\), then divide to get \(t+1 = ...\).

At maximum speed, \(\frac{dv}{dt} = 0\):

$$\frac{10 - 0.4t}{t+1} - 0.4\ln(t+1) = 0$$

Add \(0.4\ln(t+1)\) to both sides:

$$\frac{10 - 0.4t}{t+1} = 0.4\ln(t+1)$$

Multiply both sides by \((t+1)\):

$$10 - 0.4t = 0.4(t+1)\ln(t+1)$$

Divide both sides by 0.4:

$$25 - t = (t+1)\ln(t+1)$$

We want to isolate \(t\). Rewrite: \(25 - t = (t+1)\ln(t+1)\):

Add \((t+1)\) to both sides: \(26 = (t+1) + (t+1)\ln(t+1) = (t+1)(1 + \ln(t+1))\)

$$26 = (t+1)(1 + \ln(t+1))$$

Divide both sides by \((1 + \ln(t+1))\):

$$t + 1 = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(t+1)}$$

Subtract 1 from both sides:

$$\boxed{t = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(t+1)} - 1} \quad \square$$

A1* — Reaching the given result with clear, rigorous algebra
Part (c) Step 4: Use iteration to find the time of maximum speed ▶
The equation from Part (b) has \(t\) on both sides — it can't be solved by rearranging. Instead, we use the iteration formula as a numerical method: plug in the starting value \(t_1 = 7\), compute \(t_2\), then plug \(t_2\) in to get \(t_3\), and so on. The values converge to the solution. Keep iterating until values agree to the required accuracy.

Use \(t_{n+1} = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(t_n + 1)} - 1\) with \(t_1 = 7\):

$$t_2 = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(8)} - 1 = \frac{26}{1 + 2.0794...} - 1 = \frac{26}{3.0794...} - 1 = 7.443...$$

$$t_3 = \frac{26}{1 + \ln(8.443...)} - 1 = 7.387... \approx 7.387 \text{ (3 d.p.)}$$

$$t_4 = 7.396...$$

$$t_5 = 7.395...$$

The values converge to \(t \approx 7.4\) seconds (or more precisely 7.40 to 3 s.f.).

The car reaches its maximum speed after approximately 7.4 seconds.

How to Recognise This Question Type

Look for these signals in the question:

  • A 3D shape with a fixed volume or fixed capacity
  • Asked to find a dimension that minimises surface area (or cost, material, perimeter)
  • Part (a) always says "Show that..." — gives you the target expression
  • The words "Use calculus" confirm they want differentiation
  • Often the last major question on the paper (Q13–15), worth 8–10 marks
  • Alternatively: a speed/rate/quantity modelled by a function, asked to find when it is maximum
