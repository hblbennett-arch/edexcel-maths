Guided Approach

Trigonometry spans several question types. Here are the standard approaches for the main sub-topics:

1
Proving identities ("Show that" / "Prove")
Start from one side (usually the more complicated one). Convert everything to \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) using definitions: \(\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\), \(\sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}\), \(\text{cosec}\,\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}\), \(\cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\). Apply double angle formulae where needed: \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\), \(\cos 2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1\). Simplify until you reach the other side.
2
Solving trig equations
Reduce the equation to a single trig function (e.g. use \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\) or double angle formulae). Solve for the trig ratio, then use inverse trig to find the principal value. Use symmetry of the graph (or CAST diagram) to find all solutions within the given domain. Always check how many solutions are expected.
3
R-formula (harmonic form)
Express \(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta\) as \(R\sin(\theta + \alpha)\) or \(R\cos(\theta \pm \alpha)\). Find \(R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\). Find \(\alpha\) by comparing coefficients: e.g. for \(R\sin(\theta+\alpha)\), expand and match to get \(R\cos\alpha = a\) and \(R\sin\alpha = b\), hence \(\tan\alpha = \frac{b}{a}\). Then use the harmonic form to find maximum/minimum values and solve equations.
4
Small angle approximations
When \(\theta\) is small and in radians: \(\sin\theta \approx \theta\), \(\cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}\), \(\tan\theta \approx \theta\). Substitute these into the given expression and simplify algebraically to obtain the required polynomial approximation.

Common Pitfalls (from Examiner Reports)

  • Missing solutions: Forgetting that \(\sin\theta = k\) typically gives two solutions per period (one from each "side" of the sine wave). The CAST diagram or graph sketch helps avoid this.
  • Radians vs degrees: If the question specifies "radians" or uses \(\pi\) in the domain, your calculator must be in radian mode. Many candidates lose marks by giving degree answers when radians are required.
  • Domain restrictions: Only give solutions within the stated range. Writing \(\theta = 30^\circ, 150^\circ, 390^\circ\) when the domain is \(0 \leq \theta < 360^\circ\) loses marks for the extra solution.
  • Dividing by trig functions: Never divide both sides by \(\sin\theta\) or \(\cos\theta\) without checking if they could be zero — you lose solutions. Instead, factorise.
  • Wrong R-formula sign: \(a\sin\theta - b\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta - \alpha)\), not \(R\sin(\theta + \alpha)\). The sign inside must match the pattern.
  • Incomplete identity proofs: Must show every step from start to finish. Jumping to the answer or working from both sides towards the middle is not accepted.

What Top Candidates Do

  • Always sketch the relevant trig graph for the given domain — this makes it immediately clear how many solutions exist.
  • For identity proofs: write "LHS = " at the start and show every transformation ending with "= RHS" (or vice versa). Never work on both sides simultaneously.
  • In R-formula questions: state \(R = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}\) explicitly and show the expansion of \(R\sin(\theta+\alpha)\) with the coefficient matching — even if it seems obvious.
  • When solving \(\sin(\text{something}) = k\), write out all the raw solutions for the "something" first, then solve for the original variable.

Paper 2 — June 2018 — Question 12 (9 marks)

(a) Prove that

$$1 - \cos 2\theta \equiv \tan\theta\sin 2\theta, \quad \theta \neq \frac{(2n+1)\pi}{2},\ n \in \mathbb{Z}$$

[3 marks]

(b) Hence solve, for \(-\frac{\pi}{2} < x < \frac{\pi}{2}\), the equation

$$(\sec^2 x - 5)(1 - \cos 2x) = 3\tan^2 x\sin 2x$$

Give any non-exact answer to 3 decimal places where appropriate. [6 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Start from the RHS and expand using double angle formulae ▶
We need to prove LHS = RHS. The RHS contains \(\tan\theta\) and \(\sin 2\theta\) — two different "levels" of complexity. Strategy: start from the RHS, expand \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\) and \(\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\), then simplify to reach the LHS. Alternatively, start from LHS and use \(\cos 2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta\).

We will work from the RHS towards the LHS.

Start with the RHS:

$$\text{RHS} = \tan\theta\sin 2\theta$$

Replace \(\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\) and \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\):

$$= \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \times 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$

M1 — Using the identities \(\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\) and \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\)
Part (a) Step 2: Simplify and reach the LHS ▶
We have a fraction multiplied by something containing \(\cos\theta\) in the numerator — it should cancel with the denominator. Then we use the identity \(2\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos 2\theta\) to rewrite in terms of double angle.

$$= \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \times 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$

Cancel the \(\cos\theta\) (top and bottom):

$$= 2\sin^2\theta$$

Now use the identity \(\cos 2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta\), which rearranges to \(2\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos 2\theta\):

$$= 1 - \cos 2\theta = \text{LHS} \quad \square$$

M1 — Simplifying to reach \(2\sin^2\theta\)
A1* — Complete proof with all steps shown clearly
The proof must flow in one direction (either LHS to RHS, or RHS to LHS). Working from both sides simultaneously is not acceptable. Many candidates who started from LHS used \(\cos 2\theta = 1-2\sin^2\theta\), giving \(2\sin^2\theta\), then rewrote as \(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \cdot 2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \tan\theta\sin 2\theta\). Both approaches scored full marks.
Part (b) Step 3: Use the identity from (a) to simplify the equation ▶
The question says "hence" — so we must use the result from Part (a). Looking at the equation \((\sec^2 x-5)(1-\cos 2x) = 3\tan^2 x\sin 2x\), I can see \((1-\cos 2x)\) on the left and \(\sin 2x\) on the right. From Part (a): \(1-\cos 2x = \tan x\sin 2x\). Substituting this converts the left side to use \(\tan x\sin 2x\), letting us cancel \(\sin 2x\) (or factor).

From Part (a), we know \(1 - \cos 2x = \tan x\sin 2x\). Substitute into the LHS:

$$(\sec^2 x - 5)(\tan x\sin 2x) = 3\tan^2 x\sin 2x$$

Both sides contain \(\tan x\sin 2x\). Since we're told \(x \neq \pm\frac{\pi}{2}\), we can divide both sides by \(\tan x\sin 2x\) (provided \(\tan x\sin 2x \neq 0\), i.e. \(x \neq 0\) — we'll check this case separately):

$$\sec^2 x - 5 = 3\tan x$$

B1 — Using the result from (a) to replace \(1 - \cos 2x\) with \(\tan x\sin 2x\)
M1 — Dividing/cancelling to obtain an equation in \(\tan x\) and/or \(\sec x\) only
Part (b) Step 4: Convert to a quadratic in \(\tan x\) and solve ▶
We have \(\sec^2 x - 5 = 3\tan x\). Using the identity \(\sec^2 x = 1 + \tan^2 x\), this becomes a quadratic in \(\tan x\). Factor or use the formula to solve. Don't forget to check whether \(x = 0\) (which we excluded when dividing) is also a valid solution.

Replace \(\sec^2 x\) with \(1 + \tan^2 x\):

$$1 + \tan^2 x - 5 = 3\tan x$$

$$\tan^2 x - 3\tan x - 4 = 0$$

Factorise (find two numbers that multiply to \(-4\) and add to \(-3\)):

$$(\tan x - 4)(\tan x + 1) = 0$$

So either \(\tan x = 4\) or \(\tan x = -1\).

M1 — Using \(\sec^2 x = 1 + \tan^2 x\) to form a quadratic in \(\tan x\)
A1 — Correct quadratic leading to \(\tan x = 4\) and \(\tan x = -1\)
Part (b) Step 5: Find all solutions in the given domain ▶
The domain is \(-\frac{\pi}{2} < x < \frac{\pi}{2}\). In this interval, \(\tan x\) takes every real value exactly once (it's a one-to-one function). So each value of \(\tan x\) gives exactly one solution. Also, we need to check \(x = 0\): substituting into the original gives \((\sec^2 0-5)(1-\cos 0) = (1-5)(0) = 0\) and \(3\tan^2 0\sin 0 = 0\), so \(x=0\) is a valid solution too.

From \(\tan x = 4\):

$$x = \arctan(4) = 1.326 \text{ (3 d.p.)}$$

From \(\tan x = -1\):

$$x = \arctan(-1) = -\frac{\pi}{4}$$

Check \(x = 0\) (excluded when we divided by \(\tan x\sin 2x\)):

At \(x = 0\): LHS = \((1-5)(1-1) = 0\), RHS = \(3(0)(0) = 0\). LHS = RHS, so \(x = 0\) is also a solution.

Solutions: \(x = -\dfrac{\pi}{4},\quad x = 0,\quad x = 1.326\)

A1 — \(x = \arctan 4 = 1.326\) (or awrt 1.326)
A1 — \(x = -\frac{\pi}{4}\) (exact) and \(x = 0\)
The solution \(x = 0\) was frequently missed. When you divide by an expression, always check separately whether that expression being zero gives a valid solution. Also: answers in degrees score 0 — the domain is given in radians.

Paper 2 — June 2018 — Question 7 (9 marks)

(i) Solve, for \(0 \leq x < \frac{\pi}{2}\), the equation

$$4\sin x = \sec x$$

[4 marks]

(ii) Solve, for \(0 \leq \theta < 360^\circ\), the equation

$$5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta = 2$$

giving your answers to one decimal place. [5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (i) Step 1: Rewrite \(\sec x\) and rearrange ▶
We have \(4\sin x = \sec x\). The sec function means \(\frac{1}{\cos x}\). If we multiply both sides by \(\cos x\), we get the equation in terms of \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) only — then we can use a double angle formula or identity to solve.

$$4\sin x = \frac{1}{\cos x}$$

Multiply both sides by \(\cos x\):

$$4\sin x\cos x = 1$$

Recognise that \(2\sin x\cos x = \sin 2x\), so \(4\sin x\cos x = 2\sin 2x\):

$$2\sin 2x = 1$$

$$\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}$$

M1 — Multiplying by \(\cos x\) to get \(4\sin x\cos x = 1\)
M1 — Using \(\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x\) to obtain \(\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}\)
Part (i) Step 2: Solve \(\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}\) in the given domain ▶
The domain for \(x\) is \(0 \leq x < \frac{\pi}{2}\). Since we're solving for \(2x\), the effective domain for \(2x\) is \(0 \leq 2x < \pi\). In this range, \(\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}\) has two solutions: \(2x = \frac{\pi}{6}\) and \(2x = \pi - \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{5\pi}{6}\).

If \(0 \leq x < \frac{\pi}{2}\), then \(0 \leq 2x < \pi\).

\(\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}\) when \(2x = \frac{\pi}{6}\) or \(2x = \frac{5\pi}{6}\):

$$x = \frac{\pi}{12} \quad \text{or} \quad x = \frac{5\pi}{12}$$

Solutions: \(x = \dfrac{\pi}{12},\quad x = \dfrac{5\pi}{12}\)

A1 — One correct solution (e.g. \(x = \frac{\pi}{12}\))
A1 — Both correct solutions with no extras in range
A very common error was giving only one solution. Because the equation becomes \(\sin 2x = \frac{1}{2}\), and the range for \(2x\) extends to \(\pi\), there are two solutions. Always adjust the domain when you transform the variable.
Part (ii) Step 3: Express \(5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta\) in R-formula form ▶
The equation \(5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta = 2\) has both \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) on the same side. We can't solve this directly — but the R-formula converts it to a single trig function. Write \(5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta - \alpha)\) where \(R > 0\) and \(0 < \alpha < \frac{\pi}{2}\).

We want: \(5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta - \alpha)\)

Expand \(R\sin(\theta - \alpha) = R\sin\theta\cos\alpha - R\cos\theta\sin\alpha\)

Compare coefficients:

\(R\cos\alpha = 5\) and \(R\sin\alpha = 5\)

Find \(R\): \(R = \sqrt{5^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}\)

Find \(\alpha\): \(\tan\alpha = \frac{5}{5} = 1\), so \(\alpha = 45^\circ\)

Therefore: \(5\sin\theta - 5\cos\theta = 5\sqrt{2}\sin(\theta - 45^\circ)\)

B1 — \(R = 5\sqrt{2}\) (or equivalent)
M1 — Correct method to find \(\alpha\) from \(\tan\alpha = 1\)
A1 — \(\alpha = 45^\circ\)
Part (ii) Step 4: Solve \(5\sqrt{2}\sin(\theta - 45^\circ) = 2\) ▶
Now we have a single sine equation. Divide by \(5\sqrt{2}\) to isolate the sine, then use inverse sine to find the principal value. Since \(\sin\) is positive for a value less than 1, we'll get solutions in the first and second quadrants (relative to the shifted variable \(\theta - 45^\circ\)).

$$\sin(\theta - 45^\circ) = \frac{2}{5\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2}{5\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{5}$$

Let \(\phi = \theta - 45^\circ\). The domain for \(\theta\) is \(0 \leq \theta < 360^\circ\), so \(-45^\circ \leq \phi < 315^\circ\).

Principal value: \(\phi = \arcsin\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{5}\right) = 16.43^\circ\) (1 d.p.)

Second solution in range: \(\phi = 180^\circ - 16.43^\circ = 163.57^\circ\)

Convert back to \(\theta\): add \(45^\circ\) to each:

$$\theta = 16.43^\circ + 45^\circ = 61.4^\circ$$

$$\theta = 163.57^\circ + 45^\circ = 208.6^\circ$$

Solutions: \(\theta = 61.4^\circ,\quad \theta = 208.6^\circ\)

M1 — Solving \(\sin(\theta - \text{their}\,\alpha) = \frac{2}{\text{their}\,R}\) to get a first solution for \(\theta\)
A1 — Both correct to 1 d.p., no extras
Many candidates found only one solution. The shift in domain is crucial: when you substitute \(\phi = \theta - 45^\circ\), you must adjust the endpoints of the interval and find all \(\phi\) values in that adjusted range before converting back.

Paper 1 — October 2020 — Question 6 (7 marks)

(a) Express \(\sin x + 2\cos x\) in the form \(R\sin(x + \alpha)\) where \(R\) and \(\alpha\) are constants, \(R > 0\) and \(0 < \alpha < \frac{\pi}{2}\).

Give the exact value of \(R\) and give the value of \(\alpha\) in radians to 3 decimal places. [3 marks]

The temperature, \(\theta\) °C, inside a room on a given day is modelled by the equation

$$\theta = 5 + \sin\left(\frac{\pi t}{12} - 3\right) + 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi t}{12} - 3\right), \quad 0 \leq t < 24$$

where \(t\) is the number of hours after midnight.

Using the equation of the model and your answer to part (a),

(b) deduce the maximum temperature of the room during this day, [1 mark]

(c) find the time of day when the maximum temperature occurs, giving your answer to the nearest minute. [3 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Expand \(R\sin(x+\alpha)\) and compare coefficients ▶
We want \(\sin x + 2\cos x \equiv R\sin(x+\alpha)\). To find \(R\) and \(\alpha\), expand the right-hand side using the addition formula and then match up the coefficients of \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) on each side.

Expand \(R\sin(x+\alpha)\) using the addition formula:

$$R\sin(x+\alpha) = R\sin x\cos\alpha + R\cos x\sin\alpha$$

Compare with \(\sin x + 2\cos x\):

Coefficient of \(\sin x\): \(R\cos\alpha = 1\)

Coefficient of \(\cos x\): \(R\sin\alpha = 2\)

Part (a) Step 2: Find \(R\) and \(\alpha\) ▶
From the two equations \(R\cos\alpha = 1\) and \(R\sin\alpha = 2\), we find \(R\) by squaring and adding (which eliminates \(\alpha\)), and find \(\alpha\) by dividing (which eliminates \(R\)).

Square and add: \(R^2\cos^2\alpha + R^2\sin^2\alpha = 1^2 + 2^2\)

$$R^2(\cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha) = 5$$

$$R^2 = 5 \implies R = \sqrt{5}$$

Divide: \(\frac{R\sin\alpha}{R\cos\alpha} = \frac{2}{1}\)

$$\tan\alpha = 2 \implies \alpha = \arctan(2) = 1.107 \text{ rad (3 d.p.)}$$

Therefore: \(\sin x + 2\cos x = \sqrt{5}\sin(x + 1.107)\)

B1 — \(R = \sqrt{5}\) (exact)
M1 — Using \(\tan\alpha = 2\) (or equivalent method) to find \(\alpha\)
A1 — \(\alpha = 1.107\) (3 d.p.)
The value of \(R\) must be exact (\(\sqrt{5}\)), not a decimal. For \(\alpha\), the question specifies "3 decimal places" and "radians" — giving 1.11 or a degree answer loses the A1.
Part (b) Step 3: Deduce the maximum temperature ▶
The model is \(\theta = 5 + \sin\left(\frac{\pi t}{12}-3\right) + 2\cos\left(\frac{\pi t}{12}-3\right)\). From Part (a), the \(\sin + 2\cos\) part equals \(\sqrt{5}\sin\left(\frac{\pi t}{12}-3+1.107\right)\). The maximum value of any \(\sin\) function is 1, so the maximum of \(\sqrt{5}\sin(\text{something})\) is \(\sqrt{5}\). Adding the constant 5 gives the maximum temperature.

Using Part (a), the model becomes:

$$\theta = 5 + \sqrt{5}\sin\left(\frac{\pi t}{12} - 3 + 1.107\right)$$

The maximum value of \(\sin(\text{anything})\) is 1, so:

$$\theta_{\max} = 5 + \sqrt{5} \approx 7.24\text{ }^\circ\text{C}$$

Or exactly: \(\theta_{\max} = (5 + \sqrt{5})\text{ }^\circ\text{C}\)

B1ft — Maximum temperature = \(5 + \text{their}\,R = 5 + \sqrt{5}\) (follow-through from their \(R\))
Part (c) Step 4: Find the time when maximum temperature occurs ▶
Maximum temperature occurs when the \(\sin\) function equals 1, i.e. when its argument equals \(\frac{\pi}{2}\). We set up the equation and solve for \(t\) (the time after midnight).

Maximum of \(\sin\) occurs when the argument \(= \frac{\pi}{2}\):

$$\frac{\pi t}{12} - 3 + 1.107 = \frac{\pi}{2}$$

$$\frac{\pi t}{12} = \frac{\pi}{2} + 3 - 1.107$$

$$\frac{\pi t}{12} = \frac{\pi}{2} + 1.893$$

$$\frac{\pi t}{12} = 1.5708 + 1.893 = 3.4638$$

Multiply both sides by \(\frac{12}{\pi}\):

$$t = \frac{12 \times 3.4638}{\pi} = \frac{41.566}{\pi} = 13.228...$$

\(t \approx 13.23\) hours after midnight = 13 hours and \(0.228 \times 60 = 14\) minutes.

The maximum temperature occurs at approximately 1:14 pm (or 13:14).

M1 — Setting the argument of \(\sin\) equal to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) and solving for \(t\)
A1 — Correct value of \(t\) (awrt 13.2)
A1 — Correct time: 13:14 (or 1:14 pm) to the nearest minute
The final answer must be a time of day (not just a decimal). "13 hours 14 minutes" or "1:14 pm" are both acceptable. Giving "13.2 hours" without converting to minutes loses the final A1.

Paper 1 — October 2020 — Question 12 (8 marks)

In this question you must show all stages of your working. Solutions relying entirely on calculator technology are not acceptable.

(a) Show that

$$\text{cosec}\,\theta - \sin\theta \equiv \cos\theta\cot\theta, \quad \theta \neq (180n)^\circ,\ n \in \mathbb{Z}$$

[3 marks]

(b) Hence, or otherwise, solve for \(0 < x < 180^\circ\)

$$\text{cosec}\,x - \sin x = \cos x\cot(3x - 50^\circ)$$

[5 marks]

View Question Paper View Mark Scheme
Part (a) Step 1: Convert the LHS to sin and cos ▶
The LHS has \(\text{cosec}\,\theta - \sin\theta\). Our strategy: convert \(\text{cosec}\,\theta\) to \(\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\), then combine the two terms over a common denominator. This should simplify to something involving \(\cos\theta\). For the RHS, \(\cos\theta\cot\theta = \cos\theta \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\) — so that's our target.

Start from the LHS:

$$\text{LHS} = \text{cosec}\,\theta - \sin\theta$$

Replace \(\text{cosec}\,\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}\):

$$= \frac{1}{\sin\theta} - \sin\theta$$

B1 — Writing \(\text{cosec}\,\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}\) (or equivalent starting step using reciprocal/quotient identities)
Part (a) Step 2: Combine over a common denominator and use the Pythagorean identity ▶
We have \(\frac{1}{\sin\theta} - \sin\theta\). To combine these, write \(\sin\theta\) as \(\frac{\sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\). Then the numerator becomes \(1 - \sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta\) by the Pythagorean identity. This gives \(\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\), which is \(\cos\theta \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \cos\theta\cot\theta\).

$$= \frac{1}{\sin\theta} - \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta}$$

Combine into a single fraction:

$$= \frac{1 - \sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta}$$

Use the identity \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\), so \(1 - \sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta\):

$$= \frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}$$

Rewrite as a product: \(\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta} = \cos\theta \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \cos\theta\cot\theta\):

$$= \cos\theta\cot\theta = \text{RHS} \quad \square$$

M1 — Combining over a common denominator to get \(\frac{1-\sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\)
A1* — Complete proof reaching \(\cos\theta\cot\theta\) with clear working
Candidates who jumped from \(\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\) directly to "= RHS" without showing it equals \(\cos\theta\cot\theta\) lost the A1*. You must explicitly show the final step of splitting the fraction into \(\cos\theta \times \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\).
Part (b) Step 3: Use Part (a) to simplify the equation ▶
From Part (a): \(\text{cosec}\,\theta - \sin\theta = \cos\theta\cot\theta\). Our equation is \(\text{cosec}\,x - \sin x = \cos x\cot(3x-50^\circ)\). The LHS matches the identity's LHS with \(\theta = x\), so the LHS equals \(\cos x\cot x\). This gives us: \(\cos x\cot x = \cos x\cot(3x-50^\circ)\).

By Part (a), \(\text{cosec}\,x - \sin x = \cos x\cot x\). So the equation becomes:

$$\cos x\cot x = \cos x\cot(3x - 50^\circ)$$

Divide both sides by \(\cos x\) (valid since \(0 < x < 180^\circ\) and \(x \neq 90^\circ\) — we check this case separately):

$$\cot x = \cot(3x - 50^\circ)$$

M1 — Using Part (a) to replace the LHS with \(\cos x\cot x\), leading to \(\cot x = \cot(3x-50^\circ)\)
Part (b) Step 4: Solve \(\cot x = \cot(3x-50^\circ)\) ▶
\(\cot A = \cot B\) implies \(A = B + 180n^\circ\) (since \(\cot\) has period \(180^\circ\)). This gives \(x = 3x - 50^\circ + 180n^\circ\). We solve for \(x\) and find all values in \(0 < x < 180^\circ\).

\(\cot x = \cot(3x-50^\circ)\) implies \(x = (3x-50^\circ) + 180n^\circ\) for integer \(n\):

$$x - 3x = -50^\circ + 180n^\circ$$

$$-2x = -50^\circ + 180n^\circ$$

$$x = 25^\circ - 90n^\circ$$

Find values of \(x\) in \(0 < x < 180^\circ\):

\(n = 0\): \(x = 25^\circ\) (valid)

\(n = -1\): \(x = 25^\circ + 90^\circ = 115^\circ\) (valid)

\(n = -2\): \(x = 25^\circ + 180^\circ = 205^\circ\) (outside range)

\(n = 1\): \(x = 25^\circ - 90^\circ = -65^\circ\) (outside range)

Also check \(x = 90^\circ\) (excluded when dividing by \(\cos x\)): at \(x = 90^\circ\), \(\cos x = 0\) and \(\text{cosec}\,90^\circ - \sin 90^\circ = 1 - 1 = 0\), and RHS = \(\cos 90^\circ \cdot \cot(220^\circ) = 0\). So \(x = 90^\circ\) is also a valid solution.

Solutions: \(x = 25^\circ,\quad x = 90^\circ,\quad x = 115^\circ\)

The solution \(x = 90^\circ\) was missed by the majority of candidates. Whenever you divide by a trig expression (here \(\cos x\)), you must separately check whether the expression being zero gives a valid solution to the original equation. This is a recurring theme across all trig equation questions.

How to Recognise the Question Type

Trigonometry appears in several different forms. Look for these signals:

  • "Prove that" / "Show that" with trig expressions on both sides — this is an identity proof
  • "Solve, for [domain]" — a trig equation. Check: is the domain in degrees or radians?
  • "Express ... in the form \(R\sin(\theta + \alpha)\)" — R-formula, often followed by "hence find max/min" or "hence solve"
  • \(\sec\), \(\text{cosec}\), \(\cot\) appearing in the equation — convert to \(\sin/\cos\) first
  • "Given that \(\theta\) is small" — small angle approximation question (usually 3 marks, early in the paper)
  • "sector", "arc length", "radians" — use \(s = r\theta\) and \(A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta\)
  • \(\sin 2\theta\), \(\cos 2\theta\) appearing — double angle formulae needed, often combined with factorisation
  • "Hence" after proving an identity — use the identity to transform the harder equation into something solvable

Key Formulae and Identities

Pythagorean Identities

  • \(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1\)
  • \(1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta\)
  • \(1 + \cot^2\theta = \text{cosec}^2\theta\)

Double Angle Formulae

  • \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta\)
  • \(\cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta\)
  • \(\tan 2\theta = \frac{2\tan\theta}{1 - \tan^2\theta}\)

Small Angle Approximations (radians only)

  • \(\sin\theta \approx \theta\)
  • \(\cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}\)
  • \(\tan\theta \approx \theta\)

Sectors and Arcs (radians)

  • Arc length: \(s = r\theta\)
  • Sector area: \(A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta\)
